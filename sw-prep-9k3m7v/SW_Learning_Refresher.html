B1 · Price-control architecture

The building-blocks model — universal across UK utilities

Allowed Revenue = Return on RAB + Depreciation + Opex (allowed) + Tax + Incentive adjustments + Pass-throughs
  • RAB = Regulatory Asset Base. Investor's claim on the regulated business. Indexed to inflation (RPI historically, now mostly CPIH).
  • Return on RAB = RAB × allowed WACC. The single biggest lever in any price control.
  • Totex = opex + capex combined into one pot; regulator sets totex allowance, company decides mix. Standard since RIIO-1 (2013) and PR14.
  • Frontier shift = expected ongoing productivity improvement built into the allowance (typically ~1% p.a.).
  • Catch-up factor = penalty for being below the efficient frontier in the benchmarking.
  • RPEs = Real Price Effects, allowing input prices to deviate from general inflation.
  • ODIs = Outcome Delivery Incentives (Ofwat term). Rewards/penalties for performance. RIIO equivalent: ODIs + Information Quality Incentive (IQI).
  • Sharing factors = how cost over/underspend is split between company and customers.

B2 · Cost of capital, WACC & financeability

CAPM & WACC — the two equations you should be able to write blind

Re = Rf + βe × ERP     (cost of equity)
WACCvanilla = (1 − g) × Re + g × Rd    (pre-tax, vanilla)
WACCpost-tax = (1 − g) × Re + g × Rd × (1 − T)
  • Rf = risk-free rate. UK regulators use ILG (index-linked gilt) yields, often the trailing 10/20-year average.
  • ERP = equity risk premium. UK practice = TMR (total market returns) approach: pick a defensible TMR (e.g. 6.5–7%) and back out ERP = TMR − Rf.
  • βe (equity beta) = sensitivity of equity returns to market. βa (asset beta) = de-levered. βd (debt beta) = small but non-zero in UK practice.
  • g = notional gearing — regulator-set (typically 55–65% for utilities), not actual.
  • Rd = cost of debt. UK practice splits embedded (historical debt, fixed) and new (forward issuance, indexed to iBoxx).

Financeability tests

A WACC can be theoretically correct but leave a company unable to maintain investment-grade credit metrics. Regulators check:

  • AICR — Adjusted Interest Cover Ratio
  • FFO / Net debt — Funds From Operations to Net Debt
  • Gearing — debt / RAB ratio
  • RCF / Net debt — Retained Cash Flow ratio

Landmark cases to know

  • PR19 CMA appeal (2021) — water companies disputed Ofwat's PR19 cost of capital. CMA raised the allowed WACC. Set the modern template.
  • PR24 final determinations (Dec 2024) — Ofwat allowed WACC c.3.7% real (CPIH), gearing 55%. Multiple water companies again referred.
  • RIIO-2 (energy networks, 2021–26) — Ofgem cost of equity criticised as low; appealed at CMA.
Schellion's WACC view (her own published piece): let systematic geopol risk flow through Rf and ERP honestly; reflect sector exposure in beta only where clearly systematic; handle idiosyncratic UK political risk via cash-flow mechanisms (not WACC add-ons). Do not double-count via a country premium.

B3 · Efficiency modelling & benchmarking

The four standard methods

MethodWhat it doesWeakness
OLS / COLSRegress cost on cost drivers; COLS shifts the OLS line down to the most efficient firmAssumes all deviation is inefficiency; sensitive to outliers
SFA (stochastic frontier)Separates inefficiency from noise via a two-part error termDistributional assumptions are strong; small UK samples make estimation fragile
DEA (data envelopment)Non-parametric — wraps a frontier around best-performersNo statistical inference; sensitive to outliers; deterministic
Unit cost / productivityCompare unit cost on simple cost driversDoesn't control for environment / scale

Frontier shift vs catch-up

Frontier shift = how far the most efficient firm improves over time (productivity growth). Catch-up = how much an inefficient firm should close the gap to the frontier. Regulators set both as inputs into the totex allowance.

Schellion's critique (memorise the 4 points)

  1. Heterogeneity — UK firms operate in dramatically different environments (legacy water assets, telecoms density), so the homogeneity assumption fails.
  2. Stable production technology — sectors face step changes (decarbonisation, digitalisation, climate resilience), not incremental productivity.
  3. Short-term optimisation — traditional models reward in-period cost minimisation even when long-term asset health justifies higher spend.
  4. Data sufficiency — n=10 water companies, n=6 DNO groups. Inference is fragile and specification-sensitive.

Her remedy: scenario-based benchmarking + engineering evidence + risk-adjusted cost assessment. Move from deterministic to multidimensional.

B4 · Competition economics

The UK competition framework

  • Competition Act 1998: Chapter I = anti-competitive agreements (mirrors Art. 101 TFEU); Chapter II = abuse of dominance (mirrors Art. 102).
  • Enterprise Act 2002: merger control (SLC test — Substantial Lessening of Competition); market investigations.
  • CMA Phase 1 (40 working days) → if SLC concerns, Phase 2 (up to 24 weeks) in-depth.
  • CAT — Competition Appeal Tribunal hears appeals from CMA, sectoral regulators, and is the forum for collective proceedings (class actions).

Market definition

SSNIP test — Small but Significant Non-transitory Increase in Price. Hypothetical-monopolist framework: would a 5–10% price rise be profitable, or would substitution defeat it?

Product market + geographic market. Watch for cellophane fallacy (defining markets too widely when prices are already above competitive level).

Damages quantification methods

  1. Comparator-based (yardstick) — compare affected market with a clean comparator over time/space.
  2. Before-and-after — pre- and post-infringement prices.
  3. Theoretical (structural) models — Cournot, Bertrand, BLP demand estimation.
  4. Cost-based — compare prices to a but-for cost-plus benchmark.
  5. Financial-performance / lost-profits — profitability analysis on the claimant entity.

B5 · Disputes, damages & expert-witness practice

The expert-witness role

  • Civil Procedure Rules Part 35 + Practice Direction 35 — primary duty is to the court, not to the instructing party.
  • CJC Civil Justice Council Protocol on the instruction of experts in civil claims.
  • Party-appointed (most common) vs Single Joint Expert (smaller matters).
  • Hot-tubbing = concurrent evidence — experts give evidence at the same time and engage with each other directly.
  • Report structure: instructions → assumptions → methodology → analysis → conclusions → declaration of independence.

The counterfactual ("but-for world")

Core concept across damages, competition, regulatory appeals, and subsidy control. Defines what would have happened absent the alleged conduct/intervention. Quality of the counterfactual usually decides the case.

B6 · Subsidy Control regime

The regime in 60 seconds

  • Subsidy Control Act 2022 — UK's post-Brexit replacement for EU State Aid. Live since 4 January 2023.
  • CMA Subsidy Advice Unit (SAU) — reviews "Subsidies and Schemes of Particular Interest" (SSoPI) and "of Interest" (SSoI). Has now reviewed 100+ schemes.
  • Two-part assessment:
    1. CMO test — is it a subsidy at all? Compare to what a hypothetical Commercial Market Operator would have done.
    2. Schedule 1 principles — if it is a subsidy, does it satisfy the seven principles (objective, proportionality, incentive effect, balance of effects on competition/investment, etc.)?
  • CMA SAU August 2025 updated guidance — Schellion's published piece sits on this. Now demands:
    • Clearer articulation of market failure
    • Explicit link policy objective ↔ economic mechanism
    • Structured assessment of distortive effects
    • Consideration of indirect impacts, dynamic responses, broader market structure
  • Appeal route: Competition Appeal Tribunal — judicial review style, one-month window from publication of subsidy decision.

B8 · Causal inference toolkit

The 5 methods named on S&W's site — be able to name one valid use-case for each

MethodWhat it doesUse-case
DiD (difference-in-differences)Compares change in treated group vs change in control over timeCartel damages — affected market vs comparator pre- and post-cartel
IV (instrumental variables)Uses external variation correlated with treatment but not the outcome (except via treatment)Demand estimation when prices are endogenous
RDD (regression discontinuity)Compares units just above vs just below a sharp thresholdEligibility thresholds in subsidy schemes
Synthetic control (Abadie)Constructs a weighted comparator from donor pool to mimic treated unit pre-treatmentSingle-unit interventions — tax/policy in one region, one cartelised market
Structural demand (BLP, AIDS, etc.)Estimates demand parameters then simulates counterfactual pricesMerger simulation, but-for pricing in damages

Identification assumptions — what kills each method

  • DiD: parallel-trends assumption fails if treated & control would have diverged anyway.
  • IV: exclusion restriction fails if the instrument also affects the outcome directly.
  • RDD: manipulation around the threshold; bandwidth choice; functional-form sensitivity.
  • Synthetic control: donor-pool selection; pre-treatment fit; placebo tests.
  • Structural demand: functional-form choices; identification of supply side; instrument validity.

Honesty principle for the interview: you have used DiD and IV on Start Up Loans evaluation work. You have not personally run BLP demand estimation. Don't claim what you haven't done. Schellion respects depth.
