## Case Study Debrief

He may ask about the written e-scooter/case answer: "Walk me through your logic." Answer like this:

> "I started by separating what the facts proved from what they merely suggested. The facts suggested a concentrated licensed triopoly, similar prices, a sector-wide price rise, some switching frictions and reduced output. That is enough to raise a credible competition concern, but not enough to conclude explicit coordination or excessive pricing. So my logic was: first define the market and structure; second identify plausible theories of harm; third test those theories against alternative explanations; fourth recommend a proportionate evidence-gathering and remedy path."

If asked what you would improve with more time:

> "I would sharpen four things. First, I would do more work on the counterfactual: what would price, output and quality look like if the licensing cap were looser or if switching were easier? Second, I would separate fixed and variable cost evidence, because contribution margins are not profitability. Third, I would test the demand story more carefully; a 10% price rise and 5% volume fall suggests pricing power, but weather, service quality, commuting patterns or capacity could also explain it. Fourth, I would put more weight on non-price competition: availability, coverage, safety, app quality, parking compliance and reliability."

If asked whether you were too cautious:

> "Possibly, but deliberately so. In a one-hour exercise I wanted to avoid over-claiming. Similar prices and simultaneous price rises are warning signs, not proof of unlawful coordination. A regulator or adviser needs to be clear about the difference between a theory of harm and a finding. My recommendation was not to do nothing; it was to gather targeted evidence quickly and keep heavier remedies available if the evidence supports them."

If asked what evidence would change your mind:

> "Internal pricing documents, competitor monitoring, communications between firms, algorithmic pricing rules, evidence of retaliation, cost data around the timing of the price rise, granular ride-level demand data, switching/churn data, and the licensing authority's tender and cap rationale. If those showed coordinated price-setting or no cost/quality justification, I would move toward enforcement. If they showed structural weak competition but no unlawful conduct, I would focus on contestability, tender design and switching remedies."

If asked "why not a price cap?":

> "A price cap may look attractive because it gives direct consumer relief, but it is a heavy remedy. In a small, operationally complex market it risks reducing availability, safety investment or service quality if the cap is wrong. I would only consider it if market power and persistent consumer harm were clear and lighter remedies were inadequate. My first-line remedies would be improving contestability, transparency, switching and licensing design."

If asked "how would you quantify consumer harm?":

> "I would build a counterfactual price and quantity path. Depending on data, I would compare affected and unaffected cities, periods before and after the price rise, or firms with different exposure to the licensing constraint. I would estimate overcharge and lost usage, then quality-adjust that if service levels changed. I would be explicit about limitations because the causal link from price change to rides is not automatic."

## Economic Case Walkthrough

The case is the "curious case of the rising scooter fares". The Authority asks three practical questions: is competition working effectively, is there evidence of consumer harm, and is intervention required? The best answer is not "there is collusion". The best answer is: the market has credible signs of weak competition and consumer harm, but the cause is not yet proven, so the response should be staged and evidence-led.

Use this walkthrough if Jonathan asks you to talk through the case from first principles.

> "I read the complaint as a prompt, not as evidence. The consumer says all apps raised prices at the same time and now feel the same. Economically, that points me toward a few hypotheses: concentration and market power, tacit coordination, explicit coordination, a common cost shock, or weak consumer switching. The question is then which of those hypotheses the data actually support."

Step 1: define the decision problem.

> "The Authority is not asking for an academic essay. It needs a preliminary briefing that helps decide whether to investigate, whether consumers may be harmed, and what remedy path is proportionate. So I structured the answer around market structure, pricing/profitability, competitive dynamics, market failure and policy options."

Step 2: market structure.

> "Pedalford is a local app-based e-scooter rental market with three licensed operators. The licence cap means entry is blocked by regulation, not only by normal commercial barriers. The HHI is about 3,550 in Year 3, up from about 3,450 in Year 1, so it is highly concentrated. The market shares are not completely static: ZoomZip rises from 40% to 45%, FlashRide falls from 35% to 30%, and Scootly stays at 25%. That tells me competition is constrained, but not dead."

Step 3: pricing and margins.

> "I used the 12-minute average ride to convert the two-part tariff into a per-ride price. ZoomZip is GBP3.64, FlashRide GBP3.98 and Scootly GBP3.76. Direct cost per ride is GBP2.00, GBP2.17 and GBP1.95 respectively. So contribution margins are roughly 45%, 46% and 48%, with a market-weighted contribution margin of about 46%. That is consistent with pricing power, but I would not call it full economic profit because we do not have depreciation, licence fees, overheads, financing costs or capital employed."

Step 4: demand and consumer harm.

> "A 10% price rise alongside a 5% fall in rides gives a crude elasticity of about -0.5. That suggests demand is inelastic and the price rise increased revenue. The market-weighted Year 3 fare is about GBP3.77; a 10% lower fare would be about GBP3.43, so current riders may be paying roughly GBP0.34 more per ride, or around GBP1 million a year across three million rides. That is an indicative transfer, not the full welfare loss. The full harm would also include lost trips and any quality or innovation effects."

Step 5: competitive dynamics.

> "The two-week price-rise window is a warning sign, but it is not proof. Similar pricing could reflect tacit coordination, explicit agreement, price leadership, ordinary convergence in a homogeneous service, or common cost pressures. The share movement cuts both ways: it shows the market is not perfectly coordinated, because the cheaper operator is gaining share and the more expensive one is losing share. But that does not remove the structural concern, because entry is capped and switching costs soften the demand-side constraint."

Step 6: evidence needed.

> "The evidence I would ask for is deliberately targeted: price-change chronology, cost trends, internal pricing papers, board documents, competitor monitoring, communications, pricing algorithms, cross-ownership, customer switching and stored-credit data, ride-level demand, service quality, fleet availability, complaints and the licensing authority's rationale for the cap. The key test is whether the price rise follows costs, follows a leader, follows communication, or reflects the protected structure."

Step 7: intervention.

> "I would not jump straight to a fare cap. The proportional package is: targeted evidence gathering, immediate low-risk demand-side remedies such as clearer trip-price display and easier refunding of stored credit, and a serious review of the three-operator cap. If evidence of agreement or concerted practice emerges, escalate to enforcement. If the issue is structural weak competition, use licensing reform or competitive retendering. Use price control only as a backstop if harm is persistent and lighter remedies fail."

The clean conclusion:

> "Competition is not working fully effectively. There is credible evidence of consumer harm. But the current facts do not prove collusion or excessive economic profit. The right advice is a focused market investigation plus proportionate pro-competitive remedies, not an immediate finding or heavy price regulation."

## Economic Case Discussion Probes

### "Was your answer too cautious?"

> "I think the caution is a strength, provided it does not become inaction. Parallel pricing and a two-week price-rise window are serious warning signs, especially in a transparent three-firm market. But an economist should distinguish a theory of harm from a proven infringement. The right balance is to say: this is enough to investigate and to pursue low-regret remedies, but not enough to allege explicit coordination or impose a fare cap."

### "What was the central theory of harm?"

> "The strongest theory is structural market power created by the licence cap and reinforced by switching costs. Coordination is an important hypothesis, but the most defensible concern on the facts is that entry is capped, users face frictions, demand is inelastic, and firms can raise prices without losing much volume."

### "Why is the licence cap so important?"

> "Because it removes one of the main constraints on a concentrated market: entry. A three-firm oligopoly can still be competitive if entry is credible, but here no new operator can enter unless the licensing authority allows it. That means the Authority needs to look at its own market-design role, not only firm conduct."

### "What is the strongest evidence against collusion?"

> "The market shares move. ZoomZip gains share while FlashRide loses share, and ZoomZip is the cheaper operator. That suggests some competitive pressure remains. It does not rule out tacit coordination, but it makes a simple 'they all agreed and nobody competes' story less persuasive."

### "What is the strongest evidence for coordination?"

> "The market has the classic facilitating conditions: three firms, transparent prices, repeated interaction, homogeneous service, inelastic demand, switching costs and blocked entry. The fact that all three raised prices by about 10% within two weeks is a plus factor. I would treat that as enough to examine price leadership, signalling or communications."

### "Why are contribution margins not profitability?"

> "Because the cost table only gives direct ride and minute costs. E-scooter operators also bear fleet capital, depreciation, vandalism, charging and rebalancing logistics, insurance, licence fees, overheads, app development, financing and cost of capital. Contribution margins show pricing power and ability to cover fixed costs; they do not prove excessive economic returns."

### "What would the counterfactual be?"

> "A better-functioning Pedalford market where users can compare true trip prices easily, switch without stored-credit friction, and where the licensing regime allows credible entry or competitive retendering while managing congestion and safety. The counterfactual is not perfect competition; it is a contestable, transparent, well-designed local mobility market."

### "How would you improve the answer if you wrote it again?"

> "I would make the decision tree more explicit: if costs rose, the price rise may be pass-through; if costs did not rise and margins widened, market power is more concerning; if internal documents show monitoring or communication, enforcement becomes live; if the licence cap is the binding issue, the remedy is market design. I would also be sharper that the GBP1 million estimate is a transfer on current rides, not total welfare harm."

### "What would you say to a client operator?"

> "I would advise them to prepare a clean evidence pack: pricing chronology, cost changes, rationale for the increase, board papers, competitor-monitoring policies, algorithm governance, customer communications, complaint data, fleet quality and service metrics. The aim would be to show whether the price move was independently justified and whether customers were treated transparently."

### "What would you say to the Authority?"

> "Do not underreact, because the market design may be producing consumer harm. But do not overstate the evidence. Open a targeted evidence phase, preserve documents, monitor prices and service quality, reduce switching frictions quickly, and review whether the cap can be redesigned as a fleet/safety constraint rather than a hard operator limit."

## Energy And RIIO

Current facts to know:

- Ofgem published RIIO-3 Final Determinations on 4 December 2025 for electricity transmission, gas distribution and gas transmission. The period runs from 1 April 2026 to 31 March 2031.
- The strategic split is electricity growth versus gas uncertainty. Electricity transmission must support Clean Power 2030, connections reform and unprecedented network investment. Gas must maintain safety and resilience while managing an uncertain long-term role.
- Ofgem is using the RIIO toolkit: outputs, ODIs, licence obligations, Price Control Deliverables, totex allowances, Real Price Effects, uncertainty mechanisms, volume drivers, reopeners, UIOLI and the totex incentive mechanism.
- For electricity distribution, ED3 is separate. Ofgem published the ED3 Sector Specific Methodology Decision on 21 May 2026. ED3 starts on 1 April 2028 and runs to 31 March 2033. DNO business plans are due in Q4 2026, draft determinations in Q2 2027, final determinations in Q4 2027.

Good answer if asked "where is energy regulation now?":

> "The big theme is that price controls are being asked to do more than the traditional efficiency task. In RIIO-3, electricity transmission is fundamentally about enabling Clean Power 2030, strategic network planning and connections reform while still protecting consumers from inefficient spend. Gas is almost the opposite problem: resilience is still essential, but long-term demand and asset use are uncertain, so depreciation, repurposing and cost recovery become central. ED3 then takes the distribution question further: proactive investment, flexibility, data, low-carbon technologies and regional energy planning. My view is that this is exactly where regulatory finance, engineering evidence and economics need to work together."

How to link your experience:

> "At DCC I am not regulating a classic energy network, but the issues are very transferable: price control logic, charging design, financeability, long-run investment, incentives, customers paying for infrastructure, and the challenge of making a regulated business case transparent. I can speak both regulator and company."

## Water And Cunliffe

Current facts to know:

- The Independent Water Commission, chaired by Sir Jon Cunliffe, published its final report in July 2025. GOV.UK describes the themes as strategic direction and planning, legislative framework, regulator reform, regulation reform, company structures and governance, infrastructure and asset health, and implementation.
- The government's "A new vision for water" White Paper was updated on 19 February 2026. It responds to Cunliffe and commits to a new stronger single regulator, a water reform bill, a transition plan in 2026, and reforms around long-term strategic direction, investor confidence, customer protection, clean water and water security.
- The government says it will abolish Ofwat and bring relevant functions from Ofwat, the Drinking Water Inspectorate, the Environment Agency and Natural England into one new regulator.
- The White Paper also moves toward a more company-specific supervisory approach, better asset-health information, statutory resilience standards, stronger engineering capability, customer protection and investment stability.

Good answer if asked "what is happening in water?":

> "Water is probably the clearest live example of regulatory model reform in the UK. Cunliffe is not just about abolishing Ofwat; it is about trying to fix fragmentation across economic, environmental, drinking-water and planning functions. The government response is moving toward a single integrated regulator, clearer long-term strategic direction, more supervisory and company-specific regulation, better asset-health evidence and a fairer investment framework. For S&W, that creates advisory opportunities around price review evidence, financeability, cost of capital, asset health, environmental delivery, customer outcomes and transition to the new regime."

How to compare energy and water:

> "The frameworks are cousins: both use price reviews, allowed revenues, cost assessment, incentives, financeability and customer outcomes. The sector differences matter. Energy is dominated by net-zero electrification and network expansion, while water is dominated by trust, environmental performance, asset health, financial resilience and institutional reform. But the economic toolkit is transferable."
