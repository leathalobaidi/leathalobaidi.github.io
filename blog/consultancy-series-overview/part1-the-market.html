The £2 Billion Industry Nobody Talks About

Part 1 of 4: I pulled the Companies House accounts for around 90 UK economics consultancies. This is what the market looks like.

Leath · 10 April 2026 · 14 min read

Around ninety registered economics consultancies operate in the United Kingdom. The 23 largest, the ones that file full statutory accounts, earned combined revenue of roughly £2.28 billion in their most recent financial year (converting non-GBP filings at 2024 annual averages). One firm alone took £461 million. Another made headline operating margins of 56%. A third, owned by McKinsey, is in liquidation.

Most people outside the industry cannot name one of them. There is no trade body. No Vault ranking. No Financial Times league table. The firms advise the Competition and Markets Authority, Ofcom, Ofgem, the European Commission, and half the FTSE 100. They shape merger decisions worth tens of billions. They set the price of water, electricity, rail, and broadband. And they do it from offices in the City, Holborn and Oxford with names most economics graduates have never heard of.

This series maps it.

How I built the database

The starting point was a list of 95 firms identified from client disclosures, CMA consultation responses, regulator expert panels, LinkedIn, and alumni networks. Three firms were removed during review as non-core or dormant: Aqua Consultants (engineering / water, part of the Adler & Allan group); Entext Communications; and Pareto Economics (not operating). Two more were downgraded on further checking. That leaves 90 active firms in the database. Figures below are as of April 2026. For each firm I pulled the Companies House profile, officer list, and filing history through the Companies House MCP API.

For the 33 largest firms, I downloaded the most recent statutory accounts as PDFs, 355 documents in total, and parsed turnover, operating profit, headcount, partner or member remuneration, and balance sheet data by hand. For the top ten, I pulled five years of historic filings to build revenue and margin time series.

Then I enriched each firm with market segment, ownership structure, ultimate parent, headcount, office location, and the names of the senior economists. Everything in this series is sourced from publicly filed UK statutory accounts or the equivalent parent-company disclosures. Where a figure is a best estimate (partnership profit-sharing for LLPs without a clean operating line), I say so.

Scope note: “Economics consultancy” is not a regulated term. I drew the line around firms whose revenue comes from applied microeconomic analysis: competition, regulation, damages quantification, public policy evaluation, and macro forecasting. I excluded management consultancies where economics is a minor practice, investment banks, and academic institutes. Foreign-currency filings (A&M Europe in euros, Cornerstone Research UK in US dollars) are converted to GBP at 2024 annual averages (€/£ 0.85, $/£ 0.78) when aggregated into totals and concentration ratios; individual rows in the league table below are shown as filed.

The concentration matters. If you remove the top four, the market looks like a pure-play professional services niche: mid-sized partnerships and subsidiaries charging £500 to £1,200 an hour for highly specialised work. If you leave them in, the sector looks more like a subsector of global advisory, dominated by US-headquartered conglomerates where economics is one practice among many.

Both framings are correct, and which one matters depends on the conversation.

The policy segment is the largest by headcount, 37 firms, but the smallest by average revenue. Most policy shops run on government framework contracts: DfT, DHSC, BEIS, DEFRA, and the devolved administrations. Day rates are capped, utilisation is high, and margins are tight. The competition and litigation segments are the opposite. Fewer firms, larger deals, and partner profit shares that clear £2 million at the top of the market.

The league table

Here are the 23 firms for which I have full audited revenue and operating profit from the most recent filings. Figures are from statutory accounts at Companies House; where a firm reports in euros or US dollars, I have kept the reporting currency in the row and converted only in the aggregate claims above the table.

Read the margins carefully. These figures are as filed. For the LLPs in this table (RBB, Oxera, Baringa, FTI, A&M Europe, A&M Tax, AlixPartners UK, A&M Disputes) the operating-profit line is struck before any members’ remuneration charge — so the RBB 56.3% vs Oxera 38.3% gap is a like-for-like comparison, and the eighteen-point spread is real. What differs between these firms is how each treats what flows below the operating-profit line. RBB classifies £0 of its £61m profit as members’ remuneration (everything is available for discretionary division among members). Baringa also classifies £0; its £162m discretionary-profit line is operating profit minus interest and corporation tax, not a post-rem residual. Oxera alone among the three takes £8.3m of its £27m below the line as a members’-remuneration charge, leaving £18.7m distributable after tax. On a distributable-profit-over-turnover basis the comparison therefore bifurcates by P&L structure: RBB 55.6% and Baringa 36.0% (each essentially equal to its operating margin because neither charges below-line rem), Oxera 26.5% — on that basis the RBB–Oxera gap widens from eighteen points to roughly twenty-nine. RBB’s FY20→FY21 accounting-policy change did move its own members’ remuneration from above to below the line, so RBB’s pre-FY21 margins are not comparable with its post-FY21 margins (the standalone RBB piece covers this). The Ltd companies (Frontier, CRA UK, Oxford Economics, Brattle, Capital Economics) report post-owner-remuneration figures, which is a genuinely different basis. Look at the margins. RBB Economics clears 56.3% on £108 million of revenue. Baringa clears 36.8% on £450 million. Oxera clears 38.3%, A&M Europe clears 38.6%. These are software-company margins earned from billing economists by the hour.

Now look at Frontier Economics. Ninety-seven million in revenue and a £1.4 million figure on the profit line (shown here as the filed operating profit, which is equivalent to profit before tax for Frontier within a rounding margin), a 1.4% ratio. Frontier is employee-owned. The low margin is not distress. It is a distribution. Staff bonuses and profit share hit the P&L above the operating line, so what remains on the operating-profit line is whatever is left after everyone has been paid their share of the year’s performance. The same applies to Oxford Economics (2.0%) and Cambridge Econometrics (0.7%).

Margins in this table are a proxy for ownership structure, not a measure of business quality. I'll return to this in Part 4.

The pure-play economics market

Strip out the multi-practice advisory firms and what remains is the pure-play economics market: firms where every economist on the payroll does economic analysis and nothing else. No restructuring. No tax. No investigations. Applied microeconomics and, in some cases, macro forecasting.

Add the top five, RBB, Frontier, CRA, Oxford Economics, and Oxera, and you reach £432 million. Add the next five and the pure-play total clears £580 million. Extend to the full set of identifiable pure-play firms, including the policy shops below £20 million and the macro boutiques, and the pure-play UK economics market lands somewhere around £700 million.

Call it £290 million if you draw the box tightly around competition and regulation. Call it £700 million if you include macro and policy. Either number would shock anyone familiar with the size of, say, UK management consulting (£14 billion) or UK law (£46 billion). This is a small industry that punches far above its weight.

But the economists sit inside firms with thousands of employees and global dispute machines behind them. When a £2 billion cartel damages case lands at the Competition Appeal Tribunal, this is where it goes.

The macro research niche

A separate ecosystem sells macroeconomic forecasts and thematic research to banks, asset managers, corporates, and governments. The product is subscription research, not hourly consulting. The economics of the business looks more like specialist publishing than traditional consulting.

Oxford Economics is the heavyweight at £74 million in UK revenue (the global group is larger still, around $150m). Subscription research for banks, multinationals, and governments, held through a UAE vehicle by founder John Walker, who passed away unexpectedly in March 2026; a leadership transition announced in December 2025 had already installed Innes McFee as Chief Executive and Walker’s son Neil Walker as Deputy CEO. Capital Economics at £22 million sits in the same niche, bought out by Phoenix Equity Partners and expanding into India and the US. Pantheon Macroeconomics, Fathom Financial Consulting, Longview Economics, and TS Lombard are the tier below: founder-run boutiques with tight analyst benches and devoted subscriber lists.

What distinguishes the macro shops from the consulting firms is operating leverage. Once the research product exists, the marginal cost of selling it to the next hedge fund is near zero. Capital Economics' 19.8% operating margin is healthy for a consulting firm, but modest for a subscription business. The ceiling is higher. Bloomberg, in the limit, is what you get if a macro research shop keeps compounding for forty years.

The policy shop ecosystem

At the other end of the market are the policy shops. Their clients are departments and agencies. Their work is evaluation, appraisal, and modelling in support of government decision-making. Their day rates are published in framework agreements, and their margins look like what you get when you compete on a framework.

SQW Group, London Economics, Alma Economics, Cambridge Econometrics, Simetrica-Jacobs, and the thirty-plus smaller firms in this segment win work through public procurement. Cambridge Econometrics, owned by a charitable trust, booked £5.4 million in FY25 with a £36 thousand operating profit: a 0.7% margin. Simetrica-Jacobs made £1 million in UK revenue with a 3.3% margin.

These firms do not fund their own offices in Mayfair. They win contracts at the margin, pay salaries that cluster around the civil service Grade 7 band, and produce reports that get cited in House of Commons committee sessions. It is serious economics and often the most rigorous evaluation work being done in Britain. It is also the least profitable segment of the market.

When people in the industry talk about "moving from policy to competition," they mean moving from the policy shops at the bottom of this table to the firms at the top. The pay difference between an economist at a framework policy shop and an associate at RBB or Oxera is a factor of two, sometimes more. It is one of the starkest pay gradients in UK professional services.

The revenue table hides the real story.

Who owns these firms? RBB's 56% margin goes to 18 partners. Oxera's goes to the partnership. Frontier's is distributed to employees. But A&M Europe's €116 million sits inside a US-owned LLP within the private Alvarez & Marsal group; AlixPartners UK, the next LLP in the table, traces up to AlixPartners LLP (Michigan), which has been majority-controlled by a CDPQ-led PE consortium since 2017 — Jay Alix retains a minority interest but is no longer the controlling PSC. Oxford Economics is owned out of the UAE. Capital Economics was bought by private equity. And one firm covered later in the series, owned by McKinsey, is in liquidation as I write this.
