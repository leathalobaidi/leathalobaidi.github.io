S&W Case Exam Prep: CMA Mobility Scooter Scenario

Prepared for a one-hour economics case exam.

What I think they are likely to test

The most likely case is not pure price-control regulation. It is a competition and consumer-protection problem wrapped in a vulnerable-consumer market:

The CMA is concerned that the mobility scooter or wider disability-aids market may not be working well for consumers. Prepare an economic framework, evidence plan and recommendation.

Kas's "CMA wants to regulate this" line probably means one of three things:

  1. The CMA may be asked to review the market, investigate conduct, or use consumer enforcement powers.
  2. The CMA may recommend standards, codes, ADR, accreditation or stronger consumer rules to DBT.
  3. The CMA may investigate specific competition problems, especially restrictions on online selling or online advertised discounts.

It probably does not mean Ofgem-style price regulation.

Current factual base to remember

  • The CMA's 2017 mobility-scooter case was about suspected breaches of competition law in the online supply of mobility scooters. It closed in October 2017 on administrative-priority grounds, but the CMA had found that TGA Mobility had entered agreements with online retailers preventing online price advertising or below-price advertising. The CMA warned TGA that future restrictions could expose it to a penalty of up to 10% of worldwide turnover.
  • Earlier OFT/CMA mobility-aids cases found infringements involving restrictions on online sales or online price advertising for Pride and Roma mobility scooter models.
  • Citizens Advice reported in September 2024 that the disability-aids market was failing consumers, with one complaint every hour in 2023. It called for the CMA to review standards, investigate supply-chain issues, identify bad actors where appropriate, and for DBT to consider whether stronger protections such as mandatory ADR, single binding code standards and accreditation were needed.
  • S&W's own DMCC pricing article is directly relevant: the CMA is looking beyond final prices to pricing architecture, online journeys, discount claims, drip pricing, urgency, default options and whether consumers can understand and compare the true offer.

Likely prompts

Prompt 1: Market study

The CMA is considering whether the mobility scooter market is working well for consumers. How would you assess the problem and what evidence would you gather?

What they want: market failures, consumer vulnerability, evidence hierarchy, remedies.

Prompt 2: Competition enforcement

A mobility scooter manufacturer restricts online retailers from advertising discounts below RRP. Assess whether this could harm competition.

What they want: vertical restraints, resale price maintenance or minimum advertised price logic, online competition, consumer harm, possible justifications.

Prompt 3: Consumer protection

Consumers complain about high-pressure sales, poor-quality products, misleading discounts and weak aftersales service. What should the CMA do?

What they want: information asymmetry, behavioural economics, vulnerable consumers, remedies that are proportionate.

Prompt 4: Client-facing advisory

A retailer or manufacturer asks S&W to review its mobility-aid pricing and sales practices before CMA scrutiny. Design the workplan.

What they want: commercial S&W answer: diagnostic, data, customer journey testing, compliance economics, outputs, timetable.

Prompt 5: Quantitative method

How would you estimate the consumer harm from online price-advertising restrictions?

What they want: counterfactual, before-after, difference-in-differences, affected/unaffected models, price dispersion, quality adjustment, pass-through, limitations.

One-hour exam operating system

0-5 minutes: Decode the question

Write at the top:

  • Who is the client?
  • What decision do they need?
  • Is this competition enforcement, consumer enforcement, a market study, or private advisory?
  • What is the deliverable: memo, report plan, recommendation, evidence plan or board advice?

Do not start with general background. Start with the decision.

5-10 minutes: Write the executive summary first

Use this skeleton:

My initial view is that the CMA should treat this as a mixed competition and consumer-protection problem. The core economic concern is not simply whether mobility scooters are expensive, but whether consumers can compare offers, assess suitability and rely on aftersales support in a market where buyers may be vulnerable, products are complex and repeat purchasing is rare. I would separate the analysis into market functioning, specific supplier or retailer conduct, evidence of consumer harm, possible efficiency justifications, and proportionate remedies.

10-25 minutes: Build the theory of harm

Cover:

  • Information asymmetry: consumers may struggle to judge product quality, suitability, safety, warranty value and aftersales service.
  • Search frictions: consumers may not buy repeatedly, may need urgent purchase, may not know reliable suppliers, and may face mobility, digital or cognitive barriers.
  • Behavioural vulnerability: pressure selling, urgency, reference prices and opaque finance can distort choices.
  • Vertical restraints: online price-advertising restrictions can soften price competition and limit price transparency.
  • Quality and service trade-offs: some restrictions may be justified if they protect advice, fitting or aftersales support, but only if necessary and proportionate.

25-40 minutes: Evidence and empirical plan

Show that you are not just naming theories. You would test them:

  • Pricing data: transaction prices, RRP, discounts, finance charges, delivery fees, warranties, returns and repair costs.
  • Web evidence: scraped online prices, "from" prices, discount claims, stock/urgency messages and checkout journeys.
  • Contract evidence: supplier-retailer agreements, policies, emails, enforcement of pricing restrictions.
  • Consumer evidence: complaints, surveys, mystery shopping, comprehension testing and vulnerable-customer journeys.
  • Quality evidence: defect rates, repair waiting times, return rates, warranty claims, product recalls and reviews.
  • Market structure: manufacturer shares, retailer concentration, entry barriers, online/offline channel mix and margins.

40-50 minutes: Remedies

Give a remedy ladder, not a single dramatic answer:

  • Soft remedies: guidance, compliance letters, customer-facing transparency templates.
  • Behavioural remedies: remove online advertising restrictions, disclose total price earlier, substantiate reference prices, make warranty/repair obligations clear.
  • Consumer remedies: suitability checklist, cooling-off and cancellation clarity, mandatory ADR, accreditation, single code standards.
  • Enforcement: undertakings, orders, fines, redress where conduct is proven.
  • Market-study recommendations: recommend DBT or sector bodies improve standards if the problem is market-wide and beyond CMA enforcement alone.

50-60 minutes: Risks and polish

Add caveats that show maturity:

  • Mobility scooters are heterogeneous; quality-adjusted price matters.
  • Some in-person service may be genuinely valuable, so do not assume all online restrictions are harmful.
  • Vulnerability raises the weight on consumer protection but does not remove the need for evidence.
  • Remedies should preserve incentives for advice, safety and aftersales service.
  • CMA action should be proportionate and targeted.

Model answer: CMA mobility scooter market

Executive summary

My initial view is that the CMA should approach the mobility scooter issue as a mixed competition, consumer-protection and market-functioning problem. The central economic question is not simply whether prices are "too high". It is whether consumers can make informed choices in a market where products are complex, the purchase may be urgent, buyers may be vulnerable, and aftersales support is a key part of the value proposition.

I would recommend a staged approach. First, define the consumer journey and the relevant market concerns. Second, test specific theories of harm, including online price-advertising restrictions, misleading pricing, poor product quality, weak aftersales service and high-pressure selling. Third, gather evidence on prices, quality, consumer outcomes and supplier-retailer conduct. Finally, select proportionate remedies that improve transparency and competition while preserving genuine service quality and safety.

The strongest initial hypothesis is that harm may arise from the interaction of three factors: limited consumer ability to compare offers, restrictions or practices that weaken online price competition, and poor accountability for quality and aftersales support. That points toward a combination of targeted enforcement and market-wide remedies, rather than blunt price regulation.

1. Market context and consumer journey

Mobility scooters are not a simple commodity purchase. They are partly a transport product, partly a health and independence product, and partly an aftersales-service product. Consumers need to assess technical specifications, suitability for their mobility needs, reliability, maintenance, insurance, delivery, warranties and repair service. Many consumers may purchase only once, may be elderly or disabled, and may rely heavily on the seller's advice.

That matters because standard competitive pressure depends on consumers being able to compare alternatives and switch to better offers. If consumers cannot judge quality, cannot compare the true total price, or are pressured into decisions, then competition may not discipline suppliers effectively. In economic terms, the market may suffer from information asymmetry, search frictions, behavioural biases and weak post-sale accountability.

The relevant customer journey should therefore include the full path from awareness and advice through pricing, purchase, delivery, repair, warranty and complaints. The CMA should not assess the headline scooter price in isolation.

2. Theories of harm

The first theory of harm is a competition theory involving vertical restraints. If a manufacturer prevents online retailers from advertising prices, advertising discounts or selling online, that can weaken price competition and make it harder for consumers to compare offers. It may soften competition between retailers, protect higher offline prices and reduce the ability of online retailers to compete. The likely consumer harm is higher prices, lower discounting and less transparent comparison.

The second theory is a consumer-protection theory. If firms use misleading discount claims, artificial urgency, drip pricing, unclear finance costs, poor disclosure of mandatory fees or confusing warranty terms, consumers may make decisions they would not otherwise make. This fits the CMA's recent focus under the DMCC: the issue is not only the final price but the pricing architecture and sales journey.

The third theory is a market-functioning theory. Defective products, poor aftersales service and weak complaint resolution may indicate that competition is not rewarding quality. If consumers struggle to observe reliability before purchase and find it hard to obtain redress after purchase, poor-quality suppliers can remain in the market. This can create adverse selection: better suppliers may be under-rewarded because consumers cannot distinguish them from weaker suppliers at the point of sale.

The fourth theory is behavioural vulnerability. In this market, urgency and dependence matter. A consumer buying a mobility scooter may need it to preserve independence or manage daily life. That raises the risk that pressure selling, confusing pricing or poor information causes greater harm than in a routine retail market.

3. Counterfactual

The counterfactual should be a market where retailers can advertise genuine prices and discounts online, consumers can compare total prices and service packages, quality and aftersales obligations are clear, and consumers have effective redress. This does not mean the lowest possible price in every case. A higher price may be efficient if it funds proper fitting, advice or repair support. The key is whether consumers understand what they are paying for and whether restrictions are necessary to deliver that value.

4. Evidence required

The CMA should gather five categories of evidence.

First, price and transaction evidence: listed prices, actual transaction prices, RRP, discounts, delivery fees, finance costs, warranty costs and repair charges. This would allow the CMA to compare prices across online and offline channels, affected and unaffected products, and periods before and after any restrictions.

Second, conduct evidence: supplier-retailer agreements, pricing policies, emails, monitoring practices and any sanctions for retailers that advertised lower prices. This is crucial for distinguishing lawful recommended pricing from restrictions that constrain retailer freedom.

Third, consumer evidence: complaints, survey evidence, mystery shopping, user testing of websites and sales calls, and evidence from vulnerable consumers. The aim is to test whether consumers understood the total price, warranty terms, suitability, repair obligations and any urgency or discount claims.

Fourth, quality evidence: defect rates, repair waiting times, warranty claims, return rates, product recalls and independent review data. This allows the CMA to test whether higher prices are associated with better service or whether consumers are paying more without improved outcomes.

Fifth, market-structure evidence: manufacturer shares, retailer concentration, entry barriers, online/offline mix, margins and supply-chain relationships. This would show whether individual firms have market power or whether the problem is more systemic.

5. Empirical methods

For a one-hour case answer, I would propose practical methods rather than over-engineering the econometrics.

I would start with descriptive analysis of price dispersion, discounts and complaint rates. Then I would compare affected and unaffected products, suppliers or time periods. If there is a clear policy change or enforcement event, a before-after or difference-in-differences approach could estimate whether restrictions increased prices or reduced discounting. I would also examine whether reduced online price transparency was associated with wider price dispersion or higher average prices.

For quality, I would use a quality-adjusted price lens. A low headline price with poor repair support may not be a better consumer outcome. Equally, a high price should not be defended as service quality without evidence that consumers receive real advice, fitting, maintenance and redress.

For consumer understanding, I would use journey testing and survey evidence. The question is whether real consumers understand the true total price, the nature of discounts, the obligations of the retailer and the practical limits of the product. This would complement legal analysis with economic evidence on informed choice.

6. Efficiency justifications

The analysis should be balanced. A supplier might argue that restrictions on online discounting protect service quality, prevent free-riding on in-store advice, maintain safety standards or ensure consumers receive a proper assessment. These are serious arguments in this market.

However, the key economic test is necessity and proportionality. If the objective is service quality, the less restrictive alternatives could include minimum service standards, accredited fitters, clear warranty obligations, training requirements, or separate charges for advice and fitting. A broad restriction on online price advertising may not be necessary if the same objective can be achieved without reducing price transparency.

This balanced treatment is important. It shows that the analysis is not anti-business or anti-profit. It is about whether the restriction improves consumer welfare or mainly dampens competition.

7. Remedies

If the issue is specific anti-competitive conduct, the remedy should focus on stopping restrictions that prevent retailers advertising or offering genuine prices online. The CMA could seek undertakings, directions, penalties or compliance monitoring depending on the evidence.

If the issue is misleading consumer journeys, remedies should focus on clearer total price disclosure, evidence-backed discount claims, limits on artificial urgency, clearer warranty and repair terms, and testing that consumers understand key information before purchase.

If the issue is market-wide poor quality and weak redress, the CMA may need a market-study style response: recommendations for mandatory ADR, a single binding code, accreditation, standards for assessment and aftersales, and better complaints data. Some of those remedies may require DBT or sector-body action rather than CMA enforcement alone.

I would avoid starting with price regulation. It may be disproportionate, difficult to administer and could weaken service quality. The better first step is to make competition work better: transparent prices, genuine claims, fair sales journeys, quality accountability and effective redress.

8. Recommendation

I would recommend that the CMA open a focused market review or evidence-gathering exercise, with targeted enforcement workstreams where the evidence points to specific unlawful conduct. The priority should be to test whether consumers are being prevented from comparing prices and quality, whether online restrictions are dampening competition, and whether poor aftersales outcomes point to a wider market failure.

The final recommendation should be evidence-led and proportionate: remove anti-competitive restrictions where proven, strengthen transparency and complaint routes, and recommend market-wide standards if the problem is systemic. That approach would protect vulnerable consumers while preserving legitimate service quality, advice and innovation in the sector.

S&W-style answer if the client is a business

If the prompt asks you to advise a manufacturer or retailer, shift the answer from "what should the CMA do?" to "how should the client prepare?"

I would structure the work as a CMA-readiness review. First, map the client's pricing practices, sales channels and customer journey. Second, classify risks under competition, consumer protection and vulnerable-customer harm. Third, test the evidence: can the client substantiate RRPs, discounts, urgency claims, finance costs and service-quality claims? Fourth, review supplier-retailer agreements for any restrictions on online sales, price advertising or discounting. Fifth, redesign high-risk practices so the client can still compete commercially while giving consumers clear, timely and testable information.

Deliverables:

  • A short risk heatmap by practice and product line.
  • A customer-journey evidence pack with screenshots and test results.
  • A supplier-retailer agreement review focused on pricing and online restrictions.
  • A data request list covering prices, discounts, complaints, returns and repair outcomes.
  • A board-ready memo with options, proportionality and implementation steps.

PhD-level signals to include

Use these sparingly. One or two will lift the answer.

  • "The economically relevant question is the counterfactual: what would prices, quality and consumer choice look like absent the restriction?"
  • "I would distinguish allocative harm from distributional or vulnerability-related harm. Both matter, but they require different evidence."
  • "The remedy should target the market failure, not simply the symptom."
  • "A higher price is not automatically harmful if it reflects costly advice, fitting or aftersales service; the issue is whether consumers understand and receive that value."
  • "Where quality is hard to observe before purchase, competition can fail to reward good suppliers. That is why complaints, repairs and warranty evidence are economically important, not just operational details."
  • "The CMA should avoid conflating price level with pricing conduct. The concern is whether conduct distorts informed choice or restricts competition."
  • "If the claimed justification is prevention of free-riding, I would test whether less restrictive mechanisms could protect service quality without suppressing online price transparency."

Phrases to use under pressure

  • "I would separate the analysis into market functioning, conduct, evidence and remedies."
  • "The main theories of harm are information asymmetry, search frictions, behavioural vulnerability and weakened price competition."
  • "I would start with a consumer journey because the harm may occur before, during and after the transaction."
  • "The counterfactual is not perfect competition; it is a better-functioning market with transparent price and quality comparison."
  • "The key evidence is whether the practice changed consumers' choices, prices or quality outcomes relative to a credible comparator."
  • "The remedy ladder matters because over-intervention could damage legitimate advice and aftersales support."

Ten-minute pre-exam drill

Memorise this mini-answer:

This is a mixed competition and consumer-protection case. Mobility scooters are complex products bought by consumers who may be vulnerable and may purchase only once, so competition depends heavily on clear information, price transparency, suitability advice and aftersales support. I would test four theories of harm: online price restrictions weakening competition, misleading pricing or discounts distorting choice, poor quality and repair outcomes reflecting weak market discipline, and high-pressure selling exploiting vulnerability. The evidence would combine prices, contracts, customer-journey testing, complaints, repairs, returns and market shares. The remedy should be proportionate: stop proven anti-competitive restrictions, improve price and warranty transparency, strengthen redress and standards, and only recommend broader regulatory change if the evidence shows a systemic market failure.

Final thing to remember

The interviewer is not looking for encyclopaedic knowledge of mobility scooters. They are looking for whether you can turn an unfamiliar market into a disciplined economic answer:

  1. What is the market failure?
  2. What is the theory of harm?
  3. What evidence would prove or disprove it?
  4. What is the counterfactual?
  5. What remedies are proportionate?
  6. What are the risks and caveats?
  7. What should the client do next?

That is the PhD-level signal: not sounding complicated, but making the problem tractable.
