The Founders' Decade: How UK Economics Consulting Was Built, 2000-2010

Part 2 of 7: In January 2000, NERA ruled the UK economics market; by 2010, three defectors from NERA had built a rival that would eventually post the highest publicly documented profit-per-partner of any UK economics firm. The story of how it happened is the story of a decade.

Leath · 10 April 2026 · 15 min read

January 2000, and the whole of the UK economics consulting industry fits inside three or four postcodes around Covent Garden and Victoria. Everyone knows everyone. The partners take their lunch at the same Italian place off Charlotte Street; the senior economists had supervised each other's PhDs; the junior ones had studied for them at the same two or three universities. When a competition case reached the Office of Fair Trading, the economist for the acquirer and the economist for the target could usually trace a shared desk at London Economics, or a shared corridor at the Bank of England, or a shared rolodex at NERA. It was a village dressed in suits. And like any village, it was about to be ambushed.

NERA was the firm that mattered. Since the Thatcher privatisation wave, its London office had advised on the electricity sell-off, on water, on British Gas, on British Telecom — the entire gallery of state-owned industries released into shareholder hands. Its European competition practice, run by a Lancashire-born economist called Derek Ridyard, handled the biggest merger cases in Brussels. Lexecon kept a London affiliate. Oxera ran a quiet Oxford practice. London Economics still existed; Oxford Economic Forecasting and Cambridge Econometrics did the macro work; and a seven-month-old upstart called Frontier Economics, chaired by the woman who had once run John Major's Policy Unit, sat in a single London office with twenty-five people and a manifesto.

Nobody in January 2000 knew that the biggest story of the decade would walk out of NERA's office two years later, carrying thirteen colleagues — a sixteen-economist founding cohort in all — and a plan to outlast its founders.

The establishment, year 2000

Start with who was there. The UK economics consulting market in January 2000 contained perhaps a dozen serious players, and most traced their origins to one of three sources: the Thatcher privatisation programme, the Chicago Law and Economics movement, or the long diaspora from London Economics Ltd. What had once been a professors' cottage industry had, by the millennium, become something closer to a trade.

NERA sat at the top, and sat there comfortably. Founded on 10 April 1961 by Jules Joskow and Irwin Stelzer, the New York parent had been acquired by Marsh and McLennan in 1983 — the insurance broker's first non-broking acquisition in a decade — which gave the London office a deep-pocketed corporate sponsor and a blue-chip client list running from the Competition Commission through every privatised utility Whitehall had on the books. Derek Ridyard had fifteen years at the firm behind him, and five years at the UK Government Economic Service behind that. His European competition practice was the model that every other firm in London had been quietly trying to copy.

Lexecon had been in London since 1980, an unusually early international expansion for a US firm and the kind of move that, in the Reagan dawn, still required a certain missionary nerve. The London affiliate operated semi-independently under Michael Walker, who had come over from British Telecom's regulatory team and had co-authored the textbook The Economics of EC Competition Law with a NERA economist named Simon Bishop. The book was the standard reference for every competition lawyer in Europe. Walker was at Lexecon London. Bishop was at NERA London. They sat on opposite sides of most big merger cases. They had lunch anyway.

Oxera, in Oxford, was the privatisation shop. Helen Jenkins and Luis Correia da Silva had founded it in 1982 to advise on the economic structure of the newly privatised utilities; twenty years on, they were still running it. Oxera did regulated-industry work, finance economics, and competition, and it did that work quietly, from an office near Oxford station, without the London swagger or the London overheads. It was smaller than NERA and smaller than Lexecon. Its revenue compounded steadily throughout the decade.

And then there was Frontier. Launched in June 1999 by five economists drawn from London Economics, the Bank of England, and the Centre for the Study of Regulated Industries, it was chaired from the outset by Sarah Hogg — former head of John Major's Number 10 Policy Unit, a woman whose rolodex ran through every senior civil servant in Whitehall (she would later become Viscountess Hailsham on the death of her father-in-law in 2001). Frontier opened its doors with twenty-five consultants in a single London office. Its founders had given away all the equity to the founding staff on day one. The firm has been entirely owned by its staff ever since, and that single decision has remained its defining structural feature. They had also decided, quite deliberately, never to sell; and they had written down four founding values on something close to an index card: interesting, open, fun, profitable. A great many people in the market thought they were mad. The founders built the firm that way because, as they later put it, they personally believed in those values.

The founding story in the founders' own words. The most vivid account of why Frontier was built the way it was comes from Simon, one of the founders and still an Executive Director. He grew up in a working-class family in the north of England, raised by a working mother and grandmother, attended an average state school and a second-rate university, and was the first in his family to go. He arrived in London with a poor CV, a northern accent, no connections, and no clear plan, but a lot of ambition. What he found in his early career was a meritocracy of output: he was rewarded for what he produced, not for where he had come from. That experience seeded the founding values of Frontier — an insistence that the firm would be judged by work, not by background, and that ownership would sit with the people doing the work. It is a founding story almost nobody in the competition-economics market knows, and it is the reason Frontier is the only twenty-five-year-old firm in the sector that has never had an outside shareholder.
The London Economics diaspora. London Economics Ltd, founded in the late 1980s, was the training ground for a generation. Simon Gaysford, Phil Burns, and others left to found Frontier. Christian Koboldt left to found DotEcon. Danny Price went to Australia to run Frontier's Sydney sister firm. By 2000, "ex-London Economics" was on the CV of half the senior economists in the market.

Frontier's subsequent milestones are best read as proof of the founding values, not departures from them. The first international office opened in Cologne in 2003; through the 2010s and 2020s the firm pushed into Australia, Singapore, Spain, France, and Ireland. Through all of it it has remained entirely staff-owned, and its reported pre-tax margin has stayed under 2 per cent — not because the work is unprofitable, but because the partnership has chosen to return the upside to staff as shares and salaries rather than as profit distributions. It is the design choice the founders made in 1999, still visible in the accounts twenty-five years later.

The board tells a different story from the founding values. Companies House filings show a succession of chairmen drawn from the very top of the British establishment. Baroness Hogg (Sarah Hogg, former head of John Major's No.10 Policy Unit) chaired from founding in 1999 to 2013. Lord Turnbull (Andrew Turnbull, former Cabinet Secretary and Permanent Secretary to HM Treasury) served as director from January 2006 to November 2015. Lord O'Donnell (Gus O'Donnell, the next Cabinet Secretary after Turnbull and also former Permanent Secretary to the Treasury) served as director and Chairman in Europe from July 2013 to January 2025. And in January 2025, Dame Sharon White — former CEO of Ofcom (2015–19), former Second Permanent Secretary to HM Treasury (2013–15), most recently chairman of the John Lewis Partnership — joined as a director.

Four directors across twenty-five years: a head of the Policy Unit, two consecutive Cabinet Secretaries, and the most senior female regulator of the 2010s. All of it documented on Companies House. The firm founded on the principle of rewarding output over background has been governed, at board level, by the most connected people in British public life. Whether this is a contradiction of the founding values or a validation of them — the founders hired people who had reached the top on merit, regardless of which institution had minted them — is a question the accounts cannot answer.

The 2002 NERA exodus

April 2002. Global Competition Review ran a headline that froze the London market for a week: "Top economics trio leave NERA in the lurch."

The trio was Derek Ridyard, Simon Bishop, and Simon Baker. Ridyard was the head of NERA's European competition practice, fifteen years in. Bishop was the author of the textbook every competition lawyer in Europe owned. Baker was the senior competition economist across the London and Brussels offices. Between them they were most of NERA's European competition capacity; between them they had decided to leave; and between them they were taking a great deal of furniture with them.

They did not go to another firm. They started one. RBB Economics opened with sixteen people — which meant the three founders had walked out of NERA with thirteen colleagues trailing behind them, Andrea Lofaro among them. The name was the three initials on the door: Ridyard, Bishop, Baker. The founding philosophy fit on an index card. Build a brand that outlasts the individuals whose names founded it. Specialise in competition economics, and in nothing else. No management consulting; no macro forecasting; no public policy. Just the competition work, done properly, in a partnership structured to keep the senior economists committed for the long run.

The oddity of the market in 2002 was that Simon Bishop and Michael Walker, co-authors of the leading competition textbook, had finished up at rival firms. Bishop had founded RBB. Walker had moved in August 2000 to open the London office of Charles River Associates, alongside Christopher Doyle (ex-Cambridge and LBS telecoms) and Robert Laslett (financial services). The CRA London launch was announced by press release on 14 August 2000, with fifteen staff, and it was positioned around three practices: financial services, telecoms and media, competition. Walker brought the competition practice. He knew the market because he had helped write its rulebook.

So by the end of 2002 the picture looked like this. NERA had lost its European competition leadership. RBB had launched with sixteen people and was already pitching against its former employer. CRA had been in London two years and had the other half of the Bishop and Walker partnership on its payroll. The question that hung over the next eight years was whether the boutique model Ridyard had bet on could actually beat the incumbents.

It could.

The rest of the decade, 2003-2009

The years after the NERA exodus were the years the map of the market changed. Consolidation started, but it started from the American side of the Atlantic, and it started with a disputes conglomerate that had made its name on litigation support. In 2003, FTI Consulting acquired Lexecon.

FTI had been built as a litigation support conglomerate; buying Lexecon gave it a competition and financial economics practice with a twenty-six-year track record, a Chicago Law School pedigree, and a London affiliate that had been operating quietly since 1980. The price was not disclosed. The strategic logic was simple: competition economics is profitable, countercyclical, and a natural adjacency to the disputes business. Over the next five years FTI would use Lexecon as a platform on which to build something a good deal bigger than anyone in London had imagined.

In 2003, five former senior economists from the US Department of Justice Antitrust Division set up Competition Policy Associates, trading as COMPASS. The principals were Janusz Ordover and Robert Willig — both formerly senior at the DOJ Antitrust Division, Ordover as Deputy Assistant Attorney General for Economics in 1991-92 and Willig in the same role in 1989-91 — alongside Margaret Guerin-Calvert and the Orszag brothers, Jonathan and Peter; the latter would later serve as Obama's OMB director. In 2006, FTI acquired COMPASS as well. And in January 2008, FTI formally combined Lexecon and COMPASS into a single entity, Compass Lexecon LLC. The deal was quiet, the rebrand was gradual, and most of London did not fully register at the time that the combined firm was about to become the biggest competition economics practice in the world.

Around the consolidation, new specialist shops kept opening. Vivid Economics launched in 2006, founded by Cameron Hepburn, an Oxford academic and environmental economist, and by Robin Smale. Vivid built its practice around climate policy and environmental valuation, a segment that had not existed as a commercial niche a decade earlier. CEPA, founded in 2001, added Mark Cockburn from PwC in 2002 and grew into a steady regulated-industries shop. Brattle had been in London since 1997, serving European utility liberalisation, and kept compounding without much drama. The sector grew wider as it grew taller.

Analysis Group, one of the biggest US economics consultancies, was not in London. Its UK entity would not be incorporated until February 2017. For the whole of the founders' decade, the London litigation economics market operated without one of the three or four most important American firms in the category. That absence mattered, because it left room for the UK boutiques to build institutional relationships with the Competition Commission, the Office of Fair Trading, and the High Court without having to compete against a fully resourced Analysis Group London office. The competitive dynamics of the 2000s would have looked materially different had Analysis Group been in London from the start. They were not, and so they were not.

Revenue and scale by 2010

By the end of the decade the pecking order had reshuffled. NERA was still the biggest specialist economics firm in London by headcount and by billings, but its European competition practice had been hollowed out — a gap in the middle of the building where the furniture used to sit. RBB had grown from sixteen people to several times that figure. Frontier had passed a hundred staff on its employee-owned model and was beginning to open European offices, starting with Cologne in 2003. Oxera was larger than it had been at the start of the decade, still partner-led, still based in Oxford.

The statutory accounts tell the shape of it. Most of these firms filed small or medium-company accounts at Companies House, which means turnover figures were either disclosed or can be estimated from staff costs and balance sheet movements. Where firms did disclose, the picture was consistent: competition-focused boutiques were growing faster than the incumbents, and the incumbents were reinvesting rather than bleeding. Nobody was losing money. Partner compensation was rising across the board. The decade was, commercially, a long boom.

There was no private equity involvement anywhere in the sector — a fact easily overlooked from the vantage point of 2026, when private capital sits under nearly every name on the letterhead. Capital Economics was still independent under Roger Bootle, doing macro research for financial clients from an office in Westminster. Oxera's founders still owned Oxera. Frontier's founders had distributed equity to the founding staff and were refusing to sell. RBB was a partnership. The only "ownership events" in the decade were the FTI acquisitions of Lexecon and COMPASS, both on the American side of the Atlantic, both absorbed into a bigger disputes-consulting conglomerate rather than sold to a financial sponsor. The professionalised, PE-backed architecture that would define the 2015-to-2025 period had not yet arrived. That would come later.

What Capital Economics says about the decade. Roger Bootle launched Capital Economics in 1999 as an independent macro research firm. Through the whole of 2000-2010, it stayed independent, profitable, and unbought. Lloyds Development Capital would eventually take a stake in 2014. For the founders' decade, Capital Economics represented the road not taken: a firm that could have sold, and did not.

The end of the decade, 2010

Stand at the end of 2010 and look at what had been built. The UK economics consulting market had doubled in size — perhaps more. NERA had survived the 2002 exodus and was still the largest single-brand economics firm in London, though no longer by the margin it had once commanded. RBB had proven the thesis that a focused, partner-led boutique could beat the incumbents on competition work. Frontier had proven the thesis that an employee-owned firm could grow without selling. Oxera had proven the thesis that an Oxford-based firm could build a London-class client base without ever moving to London. CRA had successfully planted a US flag on British soil. Brattle had compounded. DotEcon had won the Queen's Award for Innovation for its spectrum auction work — that came in 2011, but the decade-long build-up was what earned it.

Compass Lexecon, formed in January 2008, was still finding its shape. The combined FTI entity had Washington DC, Chicago, and a small London footprint inherited from the old Lexecon Ltd affiliate. Globally, though, the biggest specialist competition economics firm in the world was still LECG, a publicly listed California consultancy with offices everywhere, a Madrid practice led by a young Spanish economist called Jorge Padilla, and more than 650 staff on its payroll. It was the one name that made NERA look small.

Nobody in 2010 knew that LECG had a year to live.

Five things that happened in the founders' decade

  1. The NERA exodus. In April 2002, Ridyard, Bishop, and Baker walked out with thirteen NERA colleagues — a sixteen-economist founding cohort — and built RBB from scratch; the template for every future boutique was set that week.
  2. The FTI roll-up. Lexecon in 2003, COMPASS in 2006, Compass Lexecon formed in January 2008 — the first sign that US conglomerates had noticed economics consulting was a buyable asset.
  3. CRA London. In August 2000, Michael Walker brought the other half of the competition textbook to an American firm, handing CRA instant credibility in European competition cases.
  4. Frontier's employee ownership. A founding commitment never to sell; unusual in a sector where almost every other firm would eventually take outside capital.
  5. No private equity, anywhere. The decade ended without a single PE-backed UK economics consultancy on the record. By 2025, that position would be close to reversed.

Part 3: 2010-2015, The Professionalisation

Three things were about to happen that would reshape the market within eighteen months. LECG, then the world's biggest economics consulting firm, would collapse in 2011, scattering forty-plus European competition economists across London. Compass Lexecon would absorb most of them, vaulting from a mid-tier London office to the dominant competition brand in Europe. And two bankers at a Zurich-based structured products desk were about to found Fideres, a litigation economics firm that would one day testify before the Treasury Select Committee on LIBOR.
