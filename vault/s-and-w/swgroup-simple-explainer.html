Article 1

Pricing practices: the price is not the only problem

The simple idea: businesses can change prices, offer discounts, and sell add-ons. But customers must be able to understand the real deal before they decide. Under the DMCC, the CMA can punish pricing journeys that confuse, pressure, or mislead people.

Published 04 Jun 2026 Open source article

The issue in one sentence

A sales page may show a price that looks attractive, but the full journey can hide fees, exaggerate discounts, or rush the customer into buying.

The fix in one sentence

Show the true total price early, explain why prices differ, and keep evidence that every claim is genuine.

Bad pricing journey versus clear pricing journey

1. Headline price Looks low, but it may not include required fees.
2. Pressure signals Countdowns, stock warnings, or labels may push a quick decision.
3. Surprise total The customer discovers the real price too late.
1. Real price early Mandatory charges are visible from the start.
2. Honest labels Premium tiers, discounts, and availability are explained.
3. Informed choice The customer can compare, pause, and decide.

Step by step

  1. Some online pricing tools are useful. Dynamic prices can match demand. “From” prices can point people to cheaper options. Discounts can help people spot a bargain.
  2. They become risky when the customer cannot see the truth. If required fees appear late, a discount is based on a fake old price, or a timer is not real, the customer may make a bad choice.
  3. The CMA is looking at the whole journey. The regulator is not only asking “Was the final price legal?” It is asking “Was the customer guided fairly from start to finish?”
  4. Businesses need proof, not just wording. They should be able to show where a discount came from, why a “from” price is real, and whether urgency messages match actual stock or time limits.

Common pricing risks

Practice Simple meaning Safe version
Drip pricing Fees are added later. Show unavoidable fees early.
Discount claims “Was £100, now £60.” Use a real, recent comparison price.
Urgency claims Timers or “only 2 left” messages. Only use them when the claim is true and evidenced.
Pre-selected add-ons Extras are already ticked. Let customers actively choose extras.
Tiered or premium labels Different versions cost very different amounts. Explain what the customer gets for each tier.

Remember this

  • High prices are not automatically unlawful.
  • Hidden, confusing, or pressured pricing is the danger zone.
  • Clear information and real evidence reduce the risk.

Oasis ticket lesson

The article says the CMA did not find real-time dynamic pricing in that case. The concern was clearer disclosure: customers needed better information about ticket tiers, queues, and premium labels.

Simple test: Could a normal customer understand the real total price and compare it before feeling pushed to buy?

Efficiency models: old scorecards can punish necessary investment

The simple idea: regulators compare utilities to decide how much money they should be allowed to collect from customers. The problem is that old models often assume a stable world. Today’s infrastructure world is not stable.

Published 22 May 2026 Open source article

The issue in one sentence

Traditional benchmarking can treat a company as inefficient when it is actually facing harder assets, climate risk, cyber risk, or decarbonisation costs.

The fix in one sentence

Use models that combine statistics with engineering evidence, long-term value, and risk scenarios.

Step by step

  1. Regulators need to stop waste. If a water, energy, telecoms, transport, or postal company spends too much, customers may pay too much.
  2. So regulators compare companies. They use models to ask: “What should an efficient company spend?” This affects allowed revenues and investment incentives.
  3. But the old comparisons can miss reality. Two companies may look similar in a spreadsheet, but one may have older assets, harder geography, more climate exposure, or more security work.
  4. Short-term cheapness can be false efficiency. A company can look cheap today by delaying maintenance, then create bigger costs and failures later.
  5. The answer is a wider evidence base. Statistics still help, but they should sit beside engineering evidence, risk analysis, and long-term asset health.

Next-generation efficiency modelling

Structural cost modelling

Include real cost drivers: asset age, network shape, climate exposure, service quality, and resilience needs.

Long-term value

Reward spending that prevents future failures, protects asset health, and reduces whole-life cost.

Scenario benchmarking

Test outcomes under different futures: demand, climate, technology, security threats, and policy change.

Remember this

  • Cheap today can be expensive tomorrow.
  • Different networks can have genuinely different efficient costs.
  • A good model checks cost, risk, engineering reality, and long-term value.

Why small samples matter

Some regulated sectors have only a few comparable firms. With small samples, a model can change a lot when assumptions change. That makes judgment and transparency more important.

Simple test: Does the model reward the company that protects the network for the next decade, or only the company that spends less this year?

Article 3

Political risk and WACC: risk must be put in the right box

The simple idea: utilities need investors to fund long-life assets. Investors care about political and geopolitical risk. Regulators need to reflect that risk carefully, without charging customers twice for the same risk.

Published 20 May 2026 Open source article

The issue in one sentence

If regulators understate political risk, utilities may struggle to attract investment; if they overstate it, customers may pay too much.

The fix in one sentence

Separate broad market risk from UK-specific policy risk, then use the right tool for each one.

Where political risk belongs

Systematic risk: use WACC/CAPM inputs

  • Global wars, sanctions, and trade shocks
  • Broad inflation, interest-rate, and sovereign-market changes
  • Sector exposure that moves with the wider market
  • May appear in the risk-free rate, equity risk premium, or beta

Idiosyncratic risk: use regulatory design

  • UK-specific policy reversals
  • Sudden bill freezes or sector interventions
  • Project-specific political exposure
  • Better handled through reopeners, pass-throughs, guarantees, or protections

Step by step

  1. Price controls use WACC. WACC is the blended cost of debt and equity. It helps decide how much revenue a regulated utility can earn.
  2. WACC depends partly on CAPM. CAPM estimates the return equity investors need: risk-free rate plus beta times the equity risk premium.
  3. Political risk can enter through those inputs. Global risk can affect government bond yields, market risk premiums, and sector beta.
  4. But not all political risk belongs in WACC. Some UK-specific policy risks are better handled by changing cash-flow rules, not by adding a vague extra premium.
  5. The regulator must be explicit. It should say which risk is included, where it is included, and what happens if the risk gets worse.

Decision path for regulators

1. Name the risk War, tariffs, inflation shock, bill freeze, net-zero policy shift, vendor ban.
2. Ask if it is broad market risk If yes, let market data feed risk-free rate, ERP, or beta.
3. Use the right mechanism If it is specific policy risk, use protections, reopeners, or pass-throughs.

Remember this

  • Too low a WACC can scare away investment.
  • Too high a WACC can overcharge customers.
  • The best answer is disciplined classification, not a loose add-on.

Why not just add a political premium?

The article says a broad extra premium can double-count risks already in market data and can become too subjective for the UK framework.

Simple test: Is this a market-wide risk investors cannot diversify, or a specific policy risk that changes cash flows?

Tiny glossary

CMA

The UK Competition and Markets Authority. It checks whether markets and consumer practices are fair.

DMCC

The Digital Markets, Competition and Consumers Act 2024. It gives the CMA stronger consumer enforcement powers.

Efficiency model

A model regulators use to judge what a well-run utility should spend.

WACC

Weighted average cost of capital. A blended estimate of the cost of debt and equity funding.

CAPM

A finance model used to estimate the return equity investors require for risk.

Beta

A measure of how much a company’s returns move with the wider market.

ERP

Equity risk premium. The extra return investors expect for holding shares instead of safer assets.

Reopener

A rule that lets a price control be revisited if a major unexpected event happens.
