The hired-gun economy

Jonathan Orszag billed at $2,000 an hour. FTI paid $72 million for his firm in 2006 and watched its market capitalisation rise seven-fold over the decade that followed. On the day Orszag launched Econic Partners in February 2025, FTI lost roughly a billion dollars of market capitalisation by the closing bell. This is the structural story the series has been circling around: what kind of business is expert-witness economics, and where does it belong.

Leath · April 2026 · 12 min read

A $2,000-an-hour puzzle

At an academic conference in late 2023, the longtime US federal judge Jed Rakoff described the narrow coterie of economists who turn up in antitrust trials and valuation disputes as, simply, “hired guns”. The reporter who carried the remark in the Financial Times a few weeks later, Sujeet Indap, added a price tag to the observation. Jonathan Orszag, one of the busiest economists on the circuit, was billing at close to $2,000 an hour.

These two facts do not sit comfortably together. An hourly rate that high describes a very small number of people — the kind who command blockbuster valuation mandates in blockbuster litigation. And yet the expert-witness industry those hourly rates finance has become a substantial niche in professional services, rivalling, by some measures, investment banking and management consulting for the pay of its top earners. Orszag himself put it in a sentence: “It is no longer a professor above a garage working with some grad students.”

The question raised by the FTI–Orszag dispute, which played out across a three-year sequence of court filings and Financial Times reports between 2023 and 2025, is where this business belongs. Does the expert belong inside a diversified, publicly traded advisory group — the model FTI Consulting perfected over the two decades after it bought Lexecon? Or does the expert belong inside a private partnership, the model Orszag built at Econic Partners and that Ridyard, Bishop and Baker built at RBB in London twenty-three years earlier? The series has been circling this question. The FTI–Orszag fight answers it.

Two quotes, a year apart

Orszag made the structural argument twice, and it is worth reading both versions. The first surfaced in FTI’s own complaint against him: an email to FTI chief executive Steve Gunby, sent before the relationship broke down, in which Orszag wrote: “In the vast majority of our matters, clients hire the expert and not the firm. That is why when economic experts change firms the clients generally move with them.”

Read as a negotiating posture from inside a listed company, that sentence is a threat. Read as a description of the business, it is simply true. Unlike investment banking, where the franchise sits in the institution’s relationships with chief executives and boards, and unlike management consulting, where the franchise sits in a methodology and a brand, the expert-witness franchise sits in one individual’s name on a court-filed report. When that individual walks, the franchise walks.

A year later, with Econic Partners incorporated and Goldman Sachs Alternatives having wired the capital, Orszag said the same thing in public: “When clients hire an expert, they hire an individual not a brand. In the end, there is only one person on the witness stand, and it is about that individual’s analysis and credibility. The brand they are associated with is not that relevant, and that is different from banking or management consulting.”

The first quote is the private warning. The second is the public thesis. Both describe the same structural fact, and the balance sheet of the listed company that employed Orszag for two decades has now tested the thesis in the open market.

What FTI’s balance sheet now shows

FTI bought Lexecon in December 2003 for about $130 million in cash, Competition Policy Associates (trading as COMPASS) in 2006 for another $72 million in cash and FTI stock, and welded the two into Compass Lexecon LLC in January 2008. Over the seventeen years that followed, Compass Lexecon became the dominant competition-economics practice in Europe, the largest economic-consulting business in the United States, and FTI’s second-largest segment by revenue. The parent’s market capitalisation, $1 billion or so at the 2006 acquisition, rose roughly seven-fold to above $7 billion by the end of 2023. Most of the growth can be traced, directly or indirectly, to the Compass Lexecon economics bench and the forensic-accounting adjacency it unlocked.

Then, in November 2023, FTI fired Orszag in a dispute over profit-sharing and control, and sued him. Orszag countersued. Through 2024 the case worked its way through the US federal courts while Orszag quietly prepared a rival firm. On 19 February 2025 he launched Econic Partners with Goldman Sachs Alternatives backing and with more than twenty-five economists behind him; Dennis Shaughnessy, FTI’s own former chair, was among them.

The market reaction was recorded in a single session. FTI’s shares fell fourteen per cent; its market capitalisation dropped to $5.9 billion. The company guided 2025 operating profit down by around $35 million against a group total of roughly $500 million, attributing the hit to lost revenue and the retention bonuses needed to hold the remaining bench. Three months later, on the first-quarter call, Gunby told analysts the $35 million number was “likely to be higher than that,” and that close to ten per cent of the economics division’s staff had already left. Kirsten Edwards-Warren, the London-based co-head of Compass Lexecon’s EMEA economic-consulting business, had followed Orszag across, along with Enrique Andreu, who had led the firm’s Brussels office. Econic would shortly have two hundred employees, about half of them former FTI staff. Truist’s analyst, Tobey Sommer, closed his post-call note with a single line: “margins will be something we continue to monitor.”

The seventeen-year accretion is being priced back out in quarterly increments.

The governance-risk parallel

The second argument for a pure-play partnership over a diversified conglomerate has nothing to do with economics and everything to do with what happens in the other practices under the same corporate roof. In November 2020, the New York Times published an investigation into FTI’s strategic-communications work on behalf of oil-industry clients; the allegations included running industry-backed pro-fossil-fuel campaigns and, in one instance, creating a fake Facebook profile to monitor environmental activists. The fallout was swift. MSCI, CDP and First Sentier terminated their relationships with FTI; Morningstar opened a search for replacements; Legal & General Investment Management and M&G requested urgent meetings; FTI denied the Times’ characterisation and said the fake-profile employee was no longer with the firm.

None of this touched Compass Lexecon directly. The strategic-communications team sat in an entirely different FTI segment, served entirely different clients, and the economists on the Compass Lexecon bench had no involvement in the work the Times reported on. But the clients leaving were asset managers and ratings providers, and some of them were also clients of Compass Lexecon on the other side of the building. When one brand umbrella covers oil-industry PR and competition-economics expert testimony, a reputational event in the first can contaminate the second without any of the individuals in the second having done anything wrong.

This is the cross-contamination risk that a pure-play expert-witness partnership is structured to avoid. Orszag’s own argument, put to the Financial Times on the Econic launch, was that diversified publicly traded firms are structurally disadvantaged against private partnerships because they cannot share profit as generously. The governance-risk case runs in parallel. Even if the economics bench wanted to stay, the balance sheet it sits on is exposed to every other practice in the group, and the price can be paid suddenly.

The rebuttal from the other bench

None of this means the partnership model is a clean moral virtue; it is simply a different set of conflicts. The most awkward passage in the January 2024 Financial Times piece is the one that records an earlier case in which Compass Lexecon represented both sides of an antitrust dispute. According to a ProPublica investigation, a federal judge described Orszag’s analysis in that matter as “unmoored,” and one of the clients sued the firm, saying they had not known that Compass Lexecon was advising the plaintiff and the defendant simultaneously.

This is the honest counter-argument. The same structural fact that makes the expert-witness economist so valuable — that clients hire the individual, not the firm — is also what makes it possible for a firm to end up on both sides of the same case without anyone quite noticing. A large partnership can have the same problem, but it is harder to see, because the individual names sit behind the partnership mark; a small partnership can avoid it, but only by refusing mandates and bleeding revenue. Neither structure resolves the underlying tension. The work is adversarial by design. The only question is which corporate form allocates the conflicts best.

Rakoff’s observation at that conference — that other jurisdictions use legal models where expert witnesses for competing sides co-operate rather than clash to put forward a common set of facts to the court — is a hint at a different answer, but one the US legal system is unlikely to reach for. The UK partly blends the adversarial model with a single-joint-expert tradition in some civil matters; the economics consulting market nonetheless runs on the American template. That template is what the UK partnerships have been built to serve.

What this means for the British partnerships

Read the FTI–Orszag fight against the UK market the rest of this series describes, and a pattern comes into focus. RBB Economics, eighteen partners on a single floor of 199 Bishopsgate, posts the highest profit-per-partner of any UK professional-services LLP that files in full. Oxera, employee-owned since its 2003 management buy-out, sits in the top tier of Global Competition Review every year. Frontier Economics, employee-owned since 2016, does the same. None of them is a listed conglomerate. None of them carries a strategic-communications arm. Each is, in one way or another, the structural answer to the question the FTI–Orszag fight has just asked.

The 2002 NERA spinout that founded RBB is, in retrospect, a smaller-scale version of the 2025 Compass Lexecon spinout that founded Econic. Three senior economists walked out of a large corporate employer they felt no longer paid them for the work they personally generated; they set up a partnership; they took the clients with them on the strength of their own names; and over the next two decades they built a firm that is now, on a per-partner basis, more profitable than any of the firms they left behind. The Orszag argument to Gunby — clients hire the expert, not the firm — is the argument Derek Ridyard would have made to NERA’s management in 2001, had the conversation been recorded.

The first external test of the thesis for Econic came quickly. Global Competition Review ranked Econic “Elite” in its first year of operation, in the 2026 rankings published in January 2026. No firm in the GCR 100’s history had ever debuted at Elite. The ranking panel cited the seniority of the founding team and the pipeline of EU merger mandates that had followed them across. Read that against the 2002 RBB story and the arithmetic becomes unforgiving: if the market can endorse a new entrant at Elite within twelve months of launch, the incumbent’s brand has no franchise value independent of its individuals. That is the FTI–Orszag thesis, priced into a ranking.

The structural take. Expert-witness economics is the only part of professional services where the client buys one person’s credibility as the deliverable. Every other corporate form — listed diversified group, private-equity platform, subsidiary-of-a-law-firm — layers agency costs on top of that product. The partnership model strips them out. Twenty-five years of UK filings now say that this pays. The FTI–Orszag fight is simply the public version of what the filings already knew.

Coda: the next $2 billion firm

Orszag, according to FTI’s complaint, had boasted during the negotiations that he could build a firm worth $2 billion to $3 billion. At the time, that figure read as bravado from a senior partner losing a pay dispute. A year on, with Goldman’s money wired, an Elite GCR debut and two hundred economists on the books, it reads as a base case. Orszag’s own description of the strategy to the Financial Times was that Econic would be an “equal opportunity poacher from every firm.” FTI’s $35 million-plus operating-profit hit, revised up in Q1, is what the first year of that strategy looks like on the other side of the ledger.

The uncomfortable question for every other listed firm that houses an expert-witness franchise is whether the same trade is waiting to be done in their own files. RBB demonstrated the partnership model in 2002 on a starting budget of roughly £2 million of first-year revenue. Orszag is demonstrating it in 2025 on a starting budget of a Goldman Sachs cheque. The economics graduate reading this in April 2026, three years into a senior associate track at Compass Lexecon, or NERA, or FTI, is the next data point. The thesis, by then, will not even need to be argued. It will already have been priced.

Key takeaways

  1. The expert-witness economics franchise sits in the individual on the witness stand, not in the firm’s brand. Orszag made this argument privately to FTI’s chief executive in 2023 and publicly to the Financial Times in 2025; FTI’s share-price response to the Econic launch is the market’s endorsement of it.
  2. FTI paid $130 million for Lexecon in 2003 and $72 million for COMPASS in 2006, and saw its market capitalisation rise roughly seven-fold by 2023. On 20 February 2025, the day after Orszag announced Econic, the shares fell fourteen per cent and cut the market capitalisation to $5.9 billion.
  3. FTI guided the operating-profit hit at $35 million against a group total of roughly $500 million, then revised that number up at the Q1 2025 call. Close to ten per cent of the economics division had defected within three months of the launch.
  4. The 2020 New York Times oil-industry investigation is the governance-risk parallel: clients including MSCI, CDP and First Sentier terminated relationships with FTI’s strategic-communications arm for reasons entirely unrelated to Compass Lexecon. Cross-contamination of this kind is what a pure-play partnership is structured to avoid.
  5. The partnership model is not a moral virtue — the 2010s ProPublica investigation into a Compass Lexecon dual-representation matter, in which a federal judge described Orszag’s analysis as “unmoored,” is the honest counter-argument. Expert-witness economics is adversarial by design; no corporate form resolves the conflicts, only allocates them.
  6. The UK competition-economics partnerships — RBB, Oxera, Frontier — are the stress-test laboratories for the Orszag thesis. The 2026 GCR 100’s first-ever Elite debut, awarded to Econic within twelve months of launch, is the market’s first endorsement. The 2002 NERA spinout that founded RBB is, in retrospect, a smaller-scale version of the same trade.
  7. Orszag’s own $2–3 billion self-valuation for Econic, delivered to FTI during the pay dispute, now reads as a base case. The next such breakaway is being drafted in somebody’s head right now, and the thesis will be the same.
