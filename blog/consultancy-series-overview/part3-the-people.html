The Economics Consultancy Network: The Moves That Actually Matter

I went looking for concurrent cross-board directorships and found almost none. What the data shows instead is a market that moves: AlixPartners' 2024–25 economics build-out, the Compass Lexecon exodus to Econic, Grant Thornton's new practice, and the CMA-to-consulting pipeline.

Published April 2026 · 12 min read · Based on 1,138 officer appointments tracked at Companies House across ~90 firms*

Part 1: The Market Part 2: The Ownership Part 3: The People Part 4: The Money →

Parts 1 and 2 mapped the market. Around ninety firms. Somewhere near £2.3 billion of revenue after converting non-GBP filings. Roughly half foreign-owned or PE-held, the rest in partnerships, trusts or founder hands. That tells you the shape. It does not tell you who runs it.

The names on the boards do. I pulled every officer appointment at Companies House across the ninety firms. The dataset covers 1,138 appointments across 380 unique people*. Most appear once. A handful appear twice. One appears three times. I started this piece expecting that those overlaps would reveal the architecture of the market.

They do not. They reveal career sequencing.

Strip out resignations, sequential moves between firms, and the intra-group plumbing that PE-owned holdcos create, and only two genuinely concurrent cross-firm seats remain in the entire UK economics consultancy market: Neil Shearing and Craig Legge, both inside Capital Economics group. Everyone else who appears on more than one firm's officer list got there by moving.

That negative finding turned out to be more interesting than the positive one I set out to find. The real architecture of this market is not who sits where today. It is who has moved, and where, in the last eighteen months.

The two real concurrent seats

Capital Economics group plumbing

Neil Shearing & Craig Legge

Current seats:

Both sit across the two-entity structure created when Phoenix Equity Partners bought the group in 2018. Shearing is the public face of the firm since Roger Bootle stepped back; Legge signs the accounts. These are the only two people in the entire 380-officer sample with genuinely concurrent seats across two firms in the database. Even this pair is internal group plumbing rather than a cross-market bridge, a PE-owned trading company with a research subsidiary is built this way by design.

The overlaps that turned out to be moves

Five other people showed up on multiple firms' officer lists in my first pass. Every one of them turned out to be a sequential career move, sometimes with many years between the two tenures. Pulled apart carefully:

Jonathan Loynes, Capital Economics to Pantheon, six years apart

Loynes was Chief European Economist at Capital Economics for eighteen years. He stepped down in 2018; Neil Shearing succeeded him. He resigned as an officer of both Capital Economics entities on 20 July 2018. Six years later, in March 2024, he joined Pantheon Macroeconomics as a Non-Executive Director when Ian Shepherdson was announced as Pantheon's Chairman. The Companies House filing for the Pantheon appointment lands in September 2024, which is the statutory filing lagging the board announcement. His current LinkedIn billing is “NED, Trustee, Economist”; he is a trustee of Pro Bono Economics. He holds one current board seat at an economics consultancy in our database, not three.

Joanne Hewitt-Schembri, A&M to FTI, single move in 2025

Hewitt-Schembri was Managing Director at A&M Europe from September 2019, co-leading the UK Complex & International Insolvency team and the European Restructuring Plan practice. On 8 January 2025 she joined FTI Consulting as Senior Managing Director in Corporate Finance & Restructuring; FTI announced the hire publicly on 24 February 2025. The A&M and FTI appointments are sequential, not concurrent. Note too that her specialism is UK restructuring and insolvency rather than competition economics in the narrow sense.

Mark Hawken, AlixPartners to A&M, separated by nearly a decade

Hawken resigned from AlixPartners UK LLP in September 2017. He joined A&M Europe LLP on 10 March 2025, seven and a half years later, as Managing Director in A&M Global Restructuring and Turnaround. Also UK restructuring rather than competition economics. Two tenures, never overlapping.

Keith Williamson, A&M Asia rotation, not a bridge

Williamson's brief AlixPartners appearance on the Companies House register in 2011 corresponds to a pre-A&M stint as Managing Director in AlixPartners' London investigations practice. He spent most of the subsequent decade leading A&M's Disputes & Investigations practice in Hong Kong (2011–2020), which is why he disappears from UK LLP registers in that window. He rejoined the UK A&M Disputes LLP register in 2022 on returning to London. Forensic/investigative accounting, not economics consulting. No concurrent seats.

Mark Pragnell, a ten-month Cebr NED, now ended

Pragnell founded Pragmatix Advisory in January 2020 after running Cebr as Managing Director through 2019. On 1 January 2024 he briefly rejoined Cebr's board as a director, and resigned on 29 October 2024. The overlap between his Pragmatix and Cebr seats lasted ten months and has ended. No published explanation for the short return; Douglas McWilliams is Cebr's Executive Deputy Chairman and Graham Brough is CEO. Pragnell now holds a single board seat at Pragmatix, which remains the clearest alumni-boutique story in the dataset even after the Cebr NED episode ended.

The moves that do matter: 2024–2026

Eighteen months of lateral hires, new launches, and competitor defections tell you more about the market than any stills of who is sitting where today. Four clusters stand out.

AlixPartners · 2024–25

The European economics build-out

In October 2024 Nicola Mazzarotto (ex-KPMG Global Head of Economics) and Caitlin Wilkinson (ex-KPMG Partner, ex-UK Competition Commission) joined as Partners, with Iona McCall promoted to Co-Head EMEA Economics. June 2025: Julie Bon (former CMA Deputy Chief Economist) joined as Senior Advisor. December 2025: Stefan Hunt (ex-CMA Chief Data & Technology Insight Officer, briefly at Keystone Strategy) joined as Partner to lead a new Behavioural Economics & Technology Evidence team. AlixPartners says the European economics practice doubled in size over the preceding three years. This is the clearest build-out in the market.

Econic Partners · February 2025

The Compass Lexecon exodus

Jonathan Orszag (Compass Lexecon co-founder), Mark Israel (ex-Compass Lexecon President), Kirsten Edwards-Warren (ex-Co-Head EMEA Compass Lexecon), Enrique Andreu, Kevin Murphy (Chicago Booth) and Nathan Miller (ex-DOJ Antitrust Chief Economist) launched Econic Partners in February 2025. Global Competition Review reported more than twenty-five former Compass Lexecon economists moved across. Backed by Goldman Sachs Alternatives and the Willig/Ordover families. The single biggest UK/European economics consulting story of 2025.

Grant Thornton · November 2021

The new entrant

Grant Thornton UK LLP launched its Economic Consulting practice in November 2021 with Schellion Horn (ex-A&M Managing Director, ex-FTI, ex-Deloitte, ex-PwC) and Chris Williams (ex-A&M, ex-Deloitte, ex-KPMG partner) as founding partners. The practice has grown via senior hires through 2022 onwards. Grant Thornton is the first new Big-Four-adjacent entrant to the UK competition economics market in a decade.

CMA, OFT · continuous

The regulator-to-consulting pipeline

The Competition and Markets Authority and its predecessors feed the private sector continuously. Recent moves: Andrea Coscelli (CMA CEO) to Keystone Strategy (January 2023); Stefan Hunt (CMA) via Keystone to AlixPartners (December 2025); Julie Bon (CMA Deputy Chief Economist) to AlixPartners Senior Advisor (June 2025); John Fingleton (OFT CEO) to his own boutique Fingleton (2013); Simon Chisholm (CMA/OFT Head of Merger Economics) to CRA. In Part 6 of the historical series I pulled the CMA side of this pipeline out into its own piece.

The famous founders

Cross-board seats are one layer of the network. The founders who still own their firms are another. Twelve firms in the sample have founding partners still active on the board twenty years after incorporation. The ones that matter are below.

Derek Ridyard, Simon Bishop, Simon Baker

RBB Economics · Founded 2002

The R, the B and the second B. All three came out of NERA, the grandfather firm of European competition economics. They left together in 2002 and built RBB into the partnership model that other boutiques tried to copy. Simon Bishop retired in April 2023.

In FY2025 the firm's highest-paid member took home £5.77m, LLP accounts disclose the figure without naming the recipient. It is the highest-disclosed member remuneration at any pure-play UK economics consultancy in our dataset. Part 4 compares it with A&M Europe's £11.8m top earner and Baringa's £11.3m derived figure, both at firms with materially larger, multi-practice revenue bases.

John Walker (1949–2026)

Oxford Economics · Founded 1981

Walker founded Oxford Economic Forecasting as a spin-off from Oxford University in 1981. He held 50 to 75 percent of the ultimate parent, Oxford Economics Group, from incorporation until his death in March 2026. A British national resident in the UAE.

The firm turned over 74 million pounds in its most recent year with a margin of about 2 percent. Longest-running founder in the sample. The lowest margin in the top ten. That combination will look strange until you read Part 4.

Roger Bootle

Capital Economics · Founded 1999

Bootle founded Capital Economics in 1999 after a career at HSBC and in the City. He built it into a 22-million-pound macro research house and sold to Phoenix Equity Partners in 2018. Neil Shearing now leads the firm. The deal was bond-financed. Part 4 looks at the debt.

Peter Lyburn, James Frayne, Rachel Wolf

Public First · Founded 2016

Alumni of Conservative policy circles. Part polling, part policy, part economic consultancy. Peter Lyburn is now the ultimate beneficial owner at the 50 to 75 percent band via his holding vehicle Stonehaven. Public First is the clearest example in the sample of the policy-to-consultancy pipeline.

Mark Pragnell

Pragmatix Advisory · Founded 2020

Left Cebr in 2019 after running it as Managing Director. Incorporated Pragmatix in January 2020. 30+ years as a consultant economist, with earlier time at Capital Economics as Director of Strategy & Consultancy. The clearest alumni boutique story in the dataset: a senior economist leaves a known brand, launches a narrower consultancy, and carries the client relationships with him.

NERA. Founded in 1961 as National Economic Research Associates. The grandfather of the industry. Still active in London as a Marsh McLennan subsidiary. Most of the founders of the boutiques that followed came through NERA at some point.

Oxford Economics. John Walker, 1981. Controlling owner for forty-four years until his death in March 2026.

Capital Economics. Bootle, 1999. Twenty-seven years later, sold to private equity but still trading under the name.

Frontier Economics. Founded in 1999 as a management buyout from London Economics. The MBO model rather than the clean-start model, but the same result: founders still on the board 26 years later.

DotEcon. Still active with original founding partners in place. A niche auctions and competition shop that has never tried to scale.

RBB. Ridyard and Baker, 2002. Twenty-four years in. Bishop retired in 2023.

The dynasty test. Of the top twenty firms by revenue in the sample, eleven still have a founder or founding-era director on the board. In most knowledge industries that number would be three or four. Economics consulting is unusually sticky at the top.

What the network tells you

Put the three layers together, the (almost nil) concurrent seats, the moves, the founder grip, and the officer counts, and what emerges is a small, durable network in which leadership is the stable layer and talent churns underneath it.

At the top: founders with long tenures. John Walker owned Oxford Economics for forty-four years until his death in March 2026. Roger Bootle built and exited Capital Economics over twenty-six. RBB still runs on an eighteen-partner model with a managing partner who took home £5.77m in FY25. NERA has existed in London for sixty-five years. Leadership at the firm level does not churn.

Underneath: the mid-senior bench does. AlixPartners is buying from KPMG and the CMA. Econic is taking senior economists out of Compass Lexecon in batches. Grant Thornton is staffing its new practice from A&M and Deloitte. The CMA is feeding the private sector through Coscelli, Hunt, Bon and others. If you want to predict where a firm's practice will be in two years, read the 2024–25 lateral moves, not the board composition.

The small number of genuinely concurrent cross-firm seats in the data, two, is itself a finding. In a market where partners hoard the upside, multi-firm director positions would dilute partnership returns. The data is consistent with a market in which firms guard their boards carefully and let the talent move instead.

Key takeaways

  1. Across 1,138 officer appointments at roughly ninety UK economics consultancies, only two people, Neil Shearing and Craig Legge, hold genuinely concurrent seats on more than one firm. Both are intra-group plumbing inside Capital Economics.
  2. The other apparent “cross-board” overlaps resolve, on inspection, to sequential career moves separated by months or years of non-overlap: Loynes (2018 resign, 2024 join Pantheon), Hewitt-Schembri (January 2025 A&M→FTI), Hawken (2017 resign AlixPartners, March 2025 join A&M), Williamson (Asia rotation), Pragnell (ten-month Cebr NED, resigned October 2024).
  3. The market's actual architecture is visible in the 2024–26 moves: AlixPartners' European economics build-out, Econic's exodus from Compass Lexecon, Grant Thornton's new practice, and the continuous CMA-to-consulting pipeline.
  4. RBB runs on an 18-partner model. Baringa has 178 members. The spread between elite boutique and broad LLP is an order of magnitude.
  5. Eleven of the top twenty firms still have a founder or founding-era director on the board. Leadership at the firm level is unusually sticky.
  6. The dominant origin story in UK economics consulting is still the alumni boutique. Nearly every mid-tier firm was founded by someone who left a bigger firm.
