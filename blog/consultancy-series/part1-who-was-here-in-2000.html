Who Was Here in 2000

The year the UK economics-consultancy market did not yet exist in its modern shape, a portrait of the four firms, 15 people and three cases that seeded everything that followed.

Leath · April 2026 · 10 min read

1 January 2000

It is the first morning of the new century, and the small world of UK competition economics is asleep in Holborn and Oxford. Derek Ridyard has been at NERA for 13 years, running the European competition practice from a Marsh & McLennan office in London. He will stay until April 2002, when he co-founds RBB Economics with Simon Bishop and Simon Baker. John Bridgeman is in his fifth year as Director General of the Office of Fair Trading; John Vickers, currently Chief Economist at the Bank of England, is set to succeed him in September. Derek Morris is in his second year as Chairman of the Competition Commission, having moved across from the chair of its predecessor, the Monopolies and Mergers Commission. A few miles away, Sarah Hogg, as non-executive chair, presides over an eight-month-old firm called Frontier Economics, founded by Z Biro, P J Burns, D M Elliott, S Gaysford and M G Webb, around 20 consultants strong and in search of a first full year of revenue. Colin Mayer, who joined Saïd Business School at its founding in 1994 as its first professor, is formally still attached to Oxera, the small Oxford boutique founded in 1982 to feed off the privatisation programme. Eight months from now, in August 2000, Charles River Associates will open its first London office with around 15 staff, led by three new vice presidents: Christopher Doyle on telecommunications, media and e-business; Robert Laslett on financial services; and Michael Walker on competition policy. Walker, a Lexecon and British Telecom alumnus, has just co-authored The Economics of EC Competition Law with Simon Bishop, the NERA partner who will follow Ridyard out the door 18 months later to co-found RBB; the book will go through three editions and become the standard textbook on EU competition economics. It is a market of half a dozen senior names and not much else.

Compass Lexecon does not exist. Nor does the Competition and Markets Authority. Nor Ofcom. Nor Berkeley Research Group. Nor Keystone Europe. Nor AlixPartners' London economics practice. Nor Charles River Associates' London office. RBB Economics does not exist either; it will not for another 27 months. Capital Economics is around a year old, renamed at Companies House on 13 January 1999 by a former HSBC chief economist named Roger Bootle, who is at this point its sole director; the business is essentially Bootle himself, deriving income from speeches, articles and presentations to a short list of subscribers, on its way to £265,908 of turnover for the year ending 30 April 2000.

The entire UK competition-economics market in January 2000 amounts to four firms, roughly 200 consultants, and perhaps a dozen senior economists whose names matter to the people who mind such things. 25 years later, the same market is 90 firms, several thousand UK-based economists, and a combined disclosed UK turnover north of £2 billion a year, with £190 million private-equity valuations in play and an RBB Economics LLP whose highest-paid designated member took home £5.77 million last year. The gap between those two pictures is the subject of this series. This piece is about who was actually in the room on day one.

The four firms that mattered

NERA, the default option

National Economic Research Associates was founded in New York in 1961 by Jules Joskow and Irwin Stelzer, two applied microeconomists who thought there was money to be made advising regulated utilities on the rate cases that then clogged the American courts. The London office opened in the 1980s under Stelzer, drawn across the Atlantic by a rarer trove of billable work: the Thatcher privatisation programme, with its gas, telecoms, water and electricity companies all to be valued, unbundled, and set against newly invented regulators. By 2000, NERA London was the dominant player in UK regulatory and competition economics, roughly 80 professional economists, a suite of offices in High Holborn, and a client roster that read like a directory of the FTSE 100 and the Whitehall regulatory estate. It was, quite simply, where the work went first.

NERA had been owned by Marsh & McLennan Companies since a 1983 acquisition, which parked it alongside Mercer and the rest of the MMC consulting empire. This ownership structure mattered later. When Ridyard, Bishop and Baker walked out in 2002, they were not leaving an independent boutique; they were leaving a subsidiary of a public American insurance broker, and the constraints of that parent, the compensation caps, the internal politics, the thick layer of New York oversight, are what gave the walk-out its logic.

The senior London partners in January 2000 were a short list. Derek Ridyard, who had been at NERA since 1987, ran European competition economics; Simon Bishop, a senior competition economist, specialised in merger analysis and market definition; Simon Baker handled coordinated effects and cartel economics. Hovering at the margin was Irwin Stelzer himself, by then writing weekly columns for The Sunday Times and advising Rupert Murdoch, with a loose formal role at NERA London that was more totemic than operational. The three future RBB partners sat three doors down from each other.

Lexecon, the US transplant

Lexecon had been founded in Chicago in 1977 by Judge Richard Posner, William Landes and Andrew Rosenfield, Posner and Landes from the University of Chicago Law School faculty, Rosenfield a former student, with Daniel Fischel and Dennis Carlton joining shortly after founding and becoming the public faces of the firm for the next three decades. For most of the 1990s it was owned through Nextera, a holding vehicle that sat somewhere between a consulting firm and an ideological project. In January 2000 the London office was small, a dozen or so consultants, but it carried the intellectual heft of the Chicago School behind it, and when the instructing solicitor wanted an expert who could quote Bork in cross-examination, this was where to go. The core practice was American cartel litigation; the London presence was a bridgehead, not a market leader.

Lexecon was acquired by FTI Consulting in 2003 for roughly $130 million. FTI later acquired COMPASS in 2006, the firm had been founded in 2003 by Robert Willig and Janusz Ordover of Princeton, and merged the two in 2008 to create Compass Lexecon, which is today the largest competition-economics firm in Europe. In 2000 none of this had happened yet. Lexecon was a respectable US firm with a London annex. Nothing more.

LECG, the network firm

LECG, the Law and Economics Consulting Group, was founded in 1988 in Berkeley, California, by David Teece, a New Zealand-born professor at UC Berkeley's Haas School who became one of the most cited scholars in industrial organisation and corporate strategy. LECG was built on a different model from NERA or Lexecon: a network firm in which senior academics were "experts affiliated with LECG" rather than full-time employees. The economics came from the affiliate network; LECG provided case-management infrastructure, billing plumbing, and a nameplate to hang outside chambers. It was an elegant structure, and for a decade it worked.

By 2000, LECG had a London office but was not yet the force it would become. Its European competition practice was built up through the 2000s, accelerating from 2004 when Jorge Padilla joined as Senior Managing Director from NERA Madrid; by 2009 he was European CEO. LECG went public in 2003 on Nasdaq at $13 a share, peaked at about $25, and then fell steadily through the late 2000s; in 2011 it filed for liquidation. Padilla's team transferred to Compass Lexecon; the rest of the LECG London alumni scattered; David Teece, a year ahead of the collapse, founded Berkeley Research Group and absorbed many of his former colleagues there. The LECG bankruptcy is one of the most consequential events in the history of UK economics consulting, and it is almost entirely forgotten now. It gets a chapter to itself in Part 3.

Oxera, the Oxford boutique

Oxera was founded in 1982 as "Oxford Economic Research Associates", a spin-out from the University of Oxford's economics department that was, in effect, a sibling operation to the London NERA office: two boutiques set up in parallel to service the Thatcher privatisations. Its early academic leadership was anchored by Colin Mayer, later Saïd Professor of Management at Oxford, who served as chairman of Oxera from 1986 until 2010. A small clarification is worth entering here, Derek Morris, who is often confused with Oxera because of the shared Oxford postcode, was in fact chairman of the separate firm Oxford Economic Forecasting Ltd, now Oxford Economics, from 1984 to 1998, before chairing the Monopolies and Mergers Commission from 1998 and taking up the Competition Commission chair when the CC succeeded the MMC in April 1999. By the turn of the century, Mayer had moved on to academia, and Oxera's operating leadership had passed to Luis Correia da Silva and Helen Jenkins, who became joint managing directors after a management buy-out in 2003 and ran the firm for the next 20 years.

Oxera in 2000 was small, perhaps 40 consultants, and its centre of gravity was still Oxford rather than London. Its specialism was regulatory economics for utility regulators: Ofwat, the predecessors of Ofgem, Oftel before Ofcom absorbed it. It was a boutique by choice, and it remained one.

The regulators as talent factory

In 2000 there was no CMA. The competition function of the UK government was split between two bodies, and the division is worth tabulating because the next generation of private-sector boutiques was staffed almost entirely out of the pool they kept.

Body Head in Jan 2000 Role
Office of Fair Trading (OFT) John Bridgeman Director General, 1995–September 2000. Investigated cartels and consumer protection. Referred complex mergers to the Competition Commission. Succeeded by John Vickers in September 2000 (DG to 2003, then Chair 2003–2005 after the OFT became a corporate board).
Competition Commission (CC) Derek Morris Chairman, April 1999–2004; previously chair of the predecessor Monopolies and Mergers Commission from 1998. Heard references from the OFT, conducted in-depth market investigations, made binding remedies.

Bridgeman had spent his career running British Alcan Aluminium and arrived at the OFT in 1995 as the Major government's choice. Vickers, then Chief Economist at the Bank of England and a member of its Monetary Policy Committee, would succeed him in September 2000; he had been Professor of Economics at Oxford before the Bank, and would go on to chair the Independent Commission on Banking in 2010 and become Warden of All Souls in 2008. Morris was a fellow Oxford economist; he had chaired the MMC from 1998, became chair of the CC when it succeeded the MMC in April 1999, served until 2004, then returned to academia and later became Provost of Oriel College, Oxford. Both men were the senior generation of UK applied microeconomists, and both moved between the regulator and academia in a way that no chief economist of the CMA has done since. Inside the OFT, the senior economist was Margaret Bloom, head of Cartels Branch, who left in 2005 for King's College London's Centre for European Law, a move that prefigured the regulator-to-academia pattern still visible in Amelia Fletcher at UEA and Chris Pike at the OECD. The OFT and CC of 2000 together employed perhaps 50 professional economists. That is the pool from which the entire private-sector economics industry was recruited for the next decade.

The three cases that shaped the decade

Airtours / First Choice (2002)

In 1999 the European Commission blocked the Airtours/First Choice holiday-package merger on the theory that the deal would create "collective dominance", a market structure in which a small number of firms can tacitly coordinate without ever meeting in a smoke-filled room. The decision was the first modern application of the "coordinated effects" doctrine to a European merger, and it was essentially a piece of economic theory dressed up as a legal finding. Airtours appealed, and in 2002 the Court of First Instance annulled the Commission's decision. The judgment is now taught in every European antitrust course. The economics was done by NERA on the Commission side and by Lexecon on the Airtours side; both firms built London practices on the back of it.

Microsoft EU (2004)

The European Commission's investigation into Microsoft's bundling of Windows Media Player and its tying of workgroup servers ran from 1998 to 2004, long enough for an entire generation of junior economists to grow up on it. The economics was central to both the liability finding and the remedies; NERA, Lexecon and LECG all had roles. The case is the one that put European antitrust economics on the map as a transatlantic industry. Until Microsoft, the US firms had dominated. After Microsoft, the European boutiques had a credible claim to competing.

Virgin Atlantic / British Airways loyalty rebates (1999–2003)

British Airways' loyalty-rebate scheme for travel agents was challenged by Virgin Atlantic under Article 82 (now Article 102 TFEU). The Commission fined BA in 1999; the decision was upheld by the CFI in 2003. The economics of rebate pricing, the question of when a discount stops being a promotion and becomes an abuse of dominance, was pioneered in this case by economists on both sides. NERA, LECG and Lexecon built their European abuse-of-dominance practices on the back of it.

These three cases share a pattern worth noting. All three were won by the Commission; all three turned on economic theory rather than the old price-cost arithmetic; all three required expert-witness testimony from partner-level economists. They created the demand that the next generation of boutiques would arrive to meet.

The first breakaway: April 2002

In April 2002, Derek Ridyard, Simon Bishop and Simon Baker walked out of NERA London together and took 13 other NERA staff with them, making a founding cohort of 16 economists including Andrea Lofaro. The Global Competition Review headline, which remains the most important three-word summary of the UK economics market's history, was: "Top economics trio leave NERA in the lurch." The three founders named the firm after their initials, Ridyard, Bishop, Baker, and began trading as RBB Economics in April 2002. The current LLP entity, OC315356, was incorporated at Companies House on 26 September 2005; the founding practice itself is three-and-a-half years older than its filed entity. The firm today files from 199 Bishopsgate in the City of London.

What the 2002 spinout established was a template. A small senior team, three partners rather than 30, walks out together, not one at a time, because the point is to transfer the practice in a single night, not to drip clients away over 18 months. They take a significant chunk of juniors with them; 13 in RBB's case, bringing the founding cohort to 16, 25 in Econic Partners' case 23 years later. They register as an LLP rather than a limited company, because the partnership structure lets them distribute profit to members without a corporation-tax layer in between. And they name the firm in a way that is deliberately generic, "RBB", "Econic", "Flint", so that no single founder's departure can damage the brand later on. Every breakaway this century has followed some version of that template: Frontier in 1999, RBB in 2002, Vivid in 2006, Fingleton Ltd in 2013, Flint in 2015, Pragmatix in 2020, Econic Partners in 2025. The template was invented by Ridyard, Bishop and Baker.

Three are dead; seven are emeritus or retired; five are still active in the market, Ridyard, Bishop and Baker at RBB, Padilla at Compass Lexecon, Teece at BRG. The youngest of the five is in his sixties. The founders of the UK competition-economics market are almost entirely out of the room. The generation replacing them is the CMA-drain cohort from Part 6, Coscelli, Hunt, Calanchi, Sala, Walker, Bon, people who were graduate students or junior case handlers in 2000, and who now inherit the billing rates that Ridyard and his peers invented.

Why the market looked nothing like today's

Three features of the 2000 market simply do not exist anymore. The first was the absence of any domestic-owned large firm. NERA was MMC-owned; Lexecon was Nextera-owned, en route to FTI; LECG was US-listed; Oxera was independent but small; Frontier was eight months old. The dominant firms in the UK competition-economics market were branches of foreign parents. Today the dominant firms are either still US-parented, FTI/Compass Lexecon, BRG, AlixPartners, A&M, or they are RBB and Baringa, the two domestic independents that emerged in 2002, RBB from a NERA breakaway that April, Baringa when its founders spun out of The Structure Group's London arm after two years as TSG Europe, specifically to take the market back from the foreign incumbents.

The second absence was private equity. The first private-equity investment in a UK economics consultancy was LDC's minority stake in Capital Economics in October 2014, 14 years after our January 2000 snapshot. For the first decade and a half of the century, this industry ran entirely on partnership distributions and foreign corporate ownership. The idea that a competition-economics boutique could be worth nine figures to a buyout fund would have been fanciful in 2000; it would have sounded like a category error.

The third absence was any single unified regulator. The OFT handled cartels and consumer protection; the Competition Commission handled market investigations and merger remedies; Ofcom did not exist, and telecoms and broadcasting were regulated by Oftel and the Independent Television Commission respectively. Ofwat, Ofgas and Offer (the ancestors of Ofgem), and Ofrail all existed, and each operated in its own silo. The CMA, which unified the OFT and the CC in 2014, is what created the modern career path, CMA economist to private consultancy, that drives everything in Part 6. In 2000 the equivalent escalator was "OFT economist to NERA or LECG", and there were perhaps four or five people riding it in any given year.

Key takeaways

  1. In January 2000 the UK competition-economics market consisted of four firms, NERA, Lexecon, LECG, Oxera, plus two infant start-ups, Frontier (eight months old, incorporated 15 April 1999) and Capital Economics (around 12 months old in its current name, renamed at CH on 13 January 1999). RBB, Compass Lexecon, BRG and every other contemporary name did not yet exist.
  2. Three of the four dominant firms were foreign-owned: NERA sat inside Marsh & McLennan, Lexecon inside Nextera (soon FTI), LECG was US-listed. Only Oxera was domestic and independent.
  3. The regulator landscape was fragmented across the OFT, the Competition Commission, Oftel, Ofgas, Offer, Ofwat and Ofrail. The CMA would not exist for another 14 years.
  4. The first modern breakaway, RBB from NERA in April 2002, established the template that every later spinout followed: a small senior team, 13 juniors (a 16-economist founding cohort in all), an LLP structure, and a generic initials brand.
  5. Of the 15 key people in the 2000 cohort, only five are still operationally active in the market in 2026; the founders have retired or died, and the generation replacing them is the CMA-drain cohort from Part 6.
  6. Three European antitrust cases, Airtours, Microsoft, and BA/Virgin loyalty rebates, created the demand that the post-2000 boutiques were founded to meet. Without those cases, there is no RBB, no Compass Lexecon, no Econic Partners.
