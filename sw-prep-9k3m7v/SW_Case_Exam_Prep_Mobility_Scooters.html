CH 1Why this topic — the 12-year mobility-aids story

UK mobility scooters and disability aids have produced three Chapter I infringement findings in a decade, all on the same pattern: manufacturers restricting online retailers from advertising or showing prices online. The CMA has built a case-law trail. This is why the topic is alive in 2026.

YearCaseWhat happened

March 2013Statement of objections to RomaOFT issues SO against Roma Medical Aids and certain retailers.
August 2013Roma decisionOFT finds Roma and 8 named online retailers (Careco, Discount Mobility Plus, Discount Mobility Shop, GBL Wheelchair, Mobility Independence, MT Mobility, Protec Mobility) infringed Chapter I by prohibiting online sales and online advertising of any prices for Roma-branded scooters between July 2011 and April 2012.

The pattern across all three cases is identical: a mobility-aid manufacturer imposes restrictions on its online retailers preventing them from showing online prices below a set level (or showing any prices at all). The economic theory is straightforward — this is online resale price maintenance (RPM). It deprives consumers of price competition exactly where they comparison-shop most easily.

"The mobility-aids sector has produced three consecutive Chapter I infringement decisions over a decade — Roma in 2013, Pride in 2014, TGA in 2017 — all on the same theory: online RPM through advertising bans. The CMA has built a case-law trail, and the DMCC 2024 now sits on top of it for the consumer-protection side. The case study almost certainly tests one of those two doctrines, or both."

CH 2The two laws on the table

A · Chapter I of the Competition Act 1998

The UK's domestic version of Article 101 TFEU. Prohibits agreements between undertakings that have the object or effect of preventing, restricting or distorting competition within the UK. Resale price maintenance — including online advertising restrictions that achieve the same effect — is a hardcore restriction by object. No effects analysis is required to establish infringement; the agreement is presumptively anti-competitive.

Penalties: up to 10% of worldwide turnover of the undertaking. Director disqualification possible. Limited immunity exists for small agreements (combined party turnover ≤£20m), but that immunity can be withdrawn — as in the TGA case.

B · The Digital Markets, Competition and Consumers Act 2024 (DMCC)

Came into force in 2024–25 in tranches. This is the major regulatory shift on the consumer-protection side, and it is Schellion's published topic. Three things the DMCC does:

  1. Gives the CMA direct enforcement powers on consumer protection. Previously the CMA had to go to court to enforce consumer law. Now it can impose penalties up to 10% of global turnover for breaches of unfair commercial practices — same level as competition fines.
  2. Codifies prohibited practices. Drip pricing, fake urgency, misleading reference prices, dynamic pricing without disclosure, fake reviews, undisclosed defaults — all explicitly within scope.
  3. Creates a new digital-markets regime targeting firms with Strategic Market Status, with conduct requirements set firm-by-firm.

The CMA has since published CMA207 "Unfair Commercial Practices" guidance (latest update 18 Nov 2025) and CMA209 price transparency guidance. Vulnerable consumers feature heavily.

Why both laws matter together for mobility aids

Mobility scooters trigger both regimes in one case:

  • Chapter I: the vertical RPM-via-advertising-ban pattern (still alive given the recurrence)
  • DMCC: misleading discount claims, fake urgency ("only 2 left at this price!"), reference-price manipulation, high-pressure sales journeys, drip pricing of finance / delivery / warranty

What they actually mean

  • Restriction by object — agreement type that's anti-competitive on its face; no need to prove anti-competitive effects.
  • Hardcore restriction — RPM, market sharing, output limitation, bid rigging. Not block-exempt.
  • Online RPM — restrictions on the prices retailers can advertise or sell at online; functionally equivalent to traditional RPM.
  • Limited immunity from penalty — small-agreements exception (≤£20m combined turnover). Available unless the CMA withdraws it.
  • Drip pricing — only the lowest possible price is shown up front; mandatory fees added later in the journey.
  • Reference price — the "was £" higher price shown alongside the "now £" price to imply a discount.
  • Dark pattern — UX design choice that nudges consumers toward outcomes against their interest. Now an explicit DMCC target.

CH 3The four most likely case prompts

Stage 2 case studies are typically a single one-page prompt with 2–4 sub-questions, and they want a structured answer in 60 minutes. Based on the topic hint, these are the four likely shapes.

Prompt A — Manufacturer-side advice

"A mobility-aids manufacturer has historically required its online retailers not to advertise prices below £X. It is now considering a 'minimum advertised price' policy across its digital channel. Advise on the competition and consumer-protection risks, and what evidence would be needed to defend the policy."

What this is testing: Do you spot the Roma/Pride/TGA pattern? Can you distinguish lawful unilateral conduct from unlawful agreements? Can you frame the role of an economist in defending a policy (counterfactual, market structure, theory of harm)?

Prompt B — Retailer-side damages claim

"An online retailer believes its sales were suppressed by a manufacturer's RPM during the period 2018–2024. Set out how you would quantify damages, what evidence you would need, and what defences the manufacturer might raise."

What this is testing: Damages methodology — but-for world, comparator method, DiD, pass-on. Cross-examination preparedness.

Prompt C — CMA-side market study scoping

"The CMA is considering opening a market study into the UK disability-aids sector following sustained consumer-protection complaints. Advise on the appropriate scope, theories of harm, and analytical workplan."

What this is testing: Can you scope a market study? Do you know the difference between a competition inquiry and a consumer-protection inquiry? Vulnerable-consumer overlay?

Prompt D — DMCC pricing-practice readiness

"A retailer of mobility scooters operates an online store with extensive 'was £X / now £Y' discount claims and 'limited time' urgency messaging. Assess DMCC compliance risk and recommend an evidence-based programme of remediation."

What this is testing: Direct alignment with Schellion's own published piece. Reference prices, drip pricing, urgency, vulnerable consumers, customer-journey analysis.

The strongest answer to any of these prompts uses the same architecture: market failure → theory of harm → counterfactual → evidence → methods → remedies → caveats. The next eight chapters build that architecture.

CH 4The Schellion angle — her own DMCC piece

Schellion authored S&W's flagship piece "Pricing practices: the DMCC, Oasis, Emma Sleep and the CMA". You should read it before the interview and quote a phrase back during the case discussion. Three things to internalise from it.

Her four pricing-practice risk categories

  1. Dynamic pricing — Oasis ticket sale; prices changing during the consumer's purchase journey.
  2. Drip pricing — incremental fees revealed after engagement.
  3. Discount claims — Emma Sleep / "was £X" reference prices.
  4. Urgency messaging — "only 2 left" / "ends today" / "30 people viewing now".

The S&W economist's role (her words, paraphrased)

"We help clients assess whether pricing structures are efficiency-enhancing or likely to create consumer detriment; whether reference prices are meaningful; whether price differentials reflect genuine differences in products or services; and whether disclosures are likely to support informed decision-making in practice."

The economist's evidence base (S&W's framing)

  • Customer-journey analysis — heat-mapping, choice architecture
  • Consumer understanding testing — survey-based
  • Substantiation of savings claims — historical price data
  • Substantiation of scarcity claims — inventory data
  • Alternative pricing-design counterfactuals
"Schellion's recent S&W piece on the DMCC frames the economist's role precisely — sitting at the boundary between pricing strategy, consumer behaviour, competition issues and evidence. The mobility-aids sector is the ideal test case because it lights up both regimes simultaneously: Chapter I on the RPM side, DMCC on the discount-and-urgency side."

CH 5Seven testable theories of harm

A senior economist's first move in any case is to enumerate the theories of harm, distinguish them, and prioritise. Here are the seven that any mobility-aids case will sit on.

#Theory of harmRegimeEvidence anchor
1Online RPM via advertising ban. Manufacturer restricts retailers from advertising below set price; consumers can't price-comparison-shop.Chapter I CA98Contracts, dealer-manual clauses, internal emails. Object infringement so effects are presumed.
2Online sales ban. Retailer cannot sell online at all for this brand. Cuts off the competitive channel entirely.Chapter I CA98Same — dealer agreements.
3Misleading reference prices. "Was £1,500, now £899" where £1,500 was never a genuine selling price for any meaningful period.DMCC 2024Historical price data; sales volume at the reference price.
4Fake urgency / scarcity. "Only 2 left at this price" or countdown timers not tied to actual inventory or pricing decisions.DMCC 2024Inventory data; price-engine logs.
5Drip pricing. Headline price excludes mandatory items (delivery, assembly, mandatory warranty, finance set-up).DMCC 2024Customer-journey analysis; price progression.
6High-pressure home-sales tactics. Door-to-door / in-home demonstration with sales-closing pressure, exploiting consumer's reduced ability to walk away.DMCC 2024 + general consumer lawConsumer surveys; complaints data; mystery-shopping.
7Aftersales / repair failure. Product quality, warranty refusal, repair delays, lifetime cost much higher than headline.Sale of Goods / Consumer Rights ActComplaints data; repair-record analysis; expected-life modelling.

An AD-level answer enumerates these, then says explicitly which are in scope for the question and which are not.

CH 6Constructing the counterfactual

The counterfactual is where the case is won or lost. Three counterfactuals to have at your fingertips.

CF-1: Pre-restriction prices in the same brand

If the restriction came in at a known date (e.g. July 2011 for Roma), use pre-restriction prices in the same brand as the but-for benchmark. The Roma case used exactly this window (July 2011–April 2012 vs immediately before).

CF-2: Comparator products (DiD)

Use a comparator mobility product not subject to the restriction (e.g. a smaller scooter line, or a brand not subject to RPM). Test parallel trends pre-restriction.

DiD: Δ(restricted brand) − Δ(comparator brand) = overcharge attributable to the restriction

CF-3: Cross-channel

For online-specific restrictions, compare online prices to offline prices in the same brand over time. If the online channel is artificially restricted, online prices should fail to fall (or fall slower) than offline.

Worked example — Roma-style DiD

Imagine you are quantifying overcharge from an online advertising ban that lasted nine months.

Pre-ban (avg online list)During-ban (avg online list)Δ
Restricted brand (Roma scooters)£1,200£1,350+£150
Comparator brand (un-restricted)£1,100£1,180+£80
Difference-in-differences£70 per unit overcharge

Aggregate by units sold during the restriction period × £70 + interest. Then test:

  • Parallel trends pre-restriction
  • Alternative comparators
  • Specification robustness (hedonic adjustment, log vs level)
  • Pass-on (here mostly to end consumers — limited pass-on defence available)

CH 8Evidence that wins

  • The dealer agreement language. The Roma/Pride/TGA cases turned on the contractual clauses. Get them and read them.
  • Internal emails and meeting notes. Object infringements often need only document evidence — the price-fixing or RPM intent is in the documents.
  • Pricing data over time. Daily/weekly online list prices for the period covering pre, during, and post restriction — both restricted and comparator brands.
  • Inventory / sales volume data. Necessary for aggregating overcharge and testing scarcity claims.
  • Customer complaints data. Citizens Advice cited "one complaint per hour" in 2023; this is a population-level signal that complements firm-level data.
  • Consumer survey / understanding. Particularly for DMCC claims — does the consumer actually understand the reference price?
  • Trade publications and industry reports. Mobility-aid trade press (British Healthcare Trades Association), Citizens Advice 2024 "Left in the Lurch" report.

CH 9The vulnerable-consumer overlay

This is where mobility aids differ from standard consumer-protection cases. Three facts to deploy.

  • 16 million disabled people in the UK rely on disability aids (Citizens Advice 2024).
  • 1 complaint per hour received by Citizens Advice about disability-aid purchases in 2023.
  • Multi-dimensional vulnerability — physical limitations, often elderly, often one-time buyers, often relying on family/carers, sometimes purchasing under time pressure following a hospital discharge or condition change.

The DMCC and CMA207 guidance both explicitly require consideration of how a practice would affect the average member of the group at which the practice is targeted, including where that group is vulnerable. The legal standard moves down to the most vulnerable plausible consumer.

"Mobility aids carry a vulnerability overlay that competition and consumer-protection law treat as a higher duty of care. The 'average consumer' standard under the DMCC and the CMA207 guidance shifts to the average vulnerable consumer when the product is targeted at people with disabilities. That changes the threshold for what counts as misleading or unfair — and the evidentiary burden on the firm to demonstrate fair dealing."

CH 10Remedies and penalties

Chapter I outcomes

  • Infringement decision with reasoned analysis published.
  • Financial penalty up to 10% of worldwide turnover. Reductions for leniency (whistleblower) and settlement (formal admission).
  • Director disqualification available.
  • Cease-and-desist — manufacturer ends the restriction.
  • Follow-on damages at the CAT — retailers and/or consumers can sue. Roma/Pride/TGA decisions are good foundation for follow-ons.

DMCC outcomes

  • Direct CMA enforcement notice — no court route needed.
  • Penalties up to 10% of global turnover (consumer-protection breaches now mirror competition).
  • Consumer redress orders — return / refund / compensation.
  • Compliance commitments — undertakings to rectify pricing presentation.
  • Director disqualification for serious or repeated breaches.

Behavioural / structural

  • Mandatory price-transparency commitments
  • Customer-journey redesign with disclosure standards
  • Independent monitoring trustee
  • Compliance training programme

CH 12Killer phrases to deploy

Opening (do not skip)

"This is best framed as a mixed competition and consumer-protection case. Mobility scooters are complex products bought by consumers who may be vulnerable and may only purchase once, so competition depends heavily on price transparency, suitability advice and aftersales support."

The case-law spine

"There is a 12-year case-law trail in this sector — Roma 2013, Pride 2014, TGA 2017 — all on the same theory of online resale price maintenance via advertising restrictions. The CMA has signalled clearly. This is not novel doctrine."

The DMCC pivot

"Since 2024 the DMCC has shifted enforcement of consumer protection from court-led to CMA-direct, with penalties of up to 10% of global turnover. That changes the calculus on misleading discounts, urgency messaging and drip pricing — issues Schellion's published S&W work has flagged explicitly."

The counterfactual move

"My preferred approach is a difference-in-differences using a comparator product or channel that was not subject to the restriction, supported by a hedonic adjustment for product attributes and a parallel-trends test. I would triangulate against a pre-restriction time series in the same brand."

The vulnerability move

"The 'average consumer' standard moves down to the average vulnerable consumer where the product is targeted at people with disabilities — Citizens Advice's 2024 work documented 16 million disabled people and one complaint per hour about disability aids. The evidentiary bar on the firm is correspondingly higher."

The honest close

"Without seeing the dealer agreements and the firm's pricing data I can only frame this at the doctrinal level. My view would update materially if the contractual language is less restrictive than the Roma/Pride/TGA pattern, or if the consumer-survey evidence shows the discount and urgency claims are well understood by the target group."
