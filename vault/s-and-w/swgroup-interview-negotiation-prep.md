# S&W Economics Interview and Negotiation Prep

## Core position

You are not pitching yourself as a generic economist. You are pitching a specific commercial proposition:

> I have worked inside the regulator, inside a government finance institution, and inside a regulated infrastructure company. I can help S&W turn complex regulatory finance and policy problems into clear client propositions, evidence-led submissions, credible proposals and practical delivery.

The strongest fit is regulatory economics and finance: price controls, cost of capital, financeability, regulated infrastructure, consultation responses, business cases, pricing models, stakeholder evidence and cross-functional delivery.

The secondary fit is practice-building: thought leadership, proposal structure, internal cross-sell, workshops, reusable methods and translating complex analysis for boards, regulators, government and clients.

## How well you can do

This can be a very strong fit if S&W needs someone who can help Schellion and Jonathan build credibility in regulated utilities, infrastructure and public-sector economics. Your edge is that you have real operating experience, not only advisory language.

The main risk is if they expect an immediate independent sales book. You should not oversell that. Your better argument is that you can help partners win by creating sharper propositions, better proposal content, credible methods and warm-entry client conversations.

The role is attractive if there is real pipeline, senior support, proposal infrastructure and a sensible runway while the practice is built. It is riskier if the role is under-supported, under-defined, or judged quickly on utilisation before enough work exists.

## Listening plan

Schellion may talk a lot and give the vision upfront. Let that happen. Your job is to listen, capture her exact language, then connect your evidence to her priorities.

Listen for:

- What work has already been won.
- Whether the practice has a real pipeline in your areas.
- Whether the role is delivery-heavy, proposal-heavy, BD-heavy or a blend.
- How much support exists below and above you.
- Whether they need regulatory finance depth, generalist economics flexibility, or immediate sales.
- How S&W wants to differ from Big Four and pure-play economics firms.
- Whether they are building proprietary tools, methods or repeatable propositions.
- What success means after six months.

Do:

- Answer in two to five minutes, then pause.
- Use one proof point per question.
- Ask follow-up questions that show commercial judgment.
- Mirror their language once you have heard it.

Avoid:

- A full CV tour.
- Sounding like AI/tools are a substitute for judgment.
- Over-claiming an independent client book.
- Asking too many heavy pipeline questions before rapport exists.

## Negotiation

Do not negotiate only salary. Negotiate the operating conditions that make success realistic.

Clarify:

- Title and grade.
- Base salary and bonus.
- Notice period.
- Probation terms and assessment criteria.
- Utilisation expectations during the build phase.
- Business development expectations.
- Support from partners, directors, analysts and design/proposal teams.
- Access to templates, case studies, rate cards and review processes.
- Promotion path and timing.
- Hybrid expectations and travel.

Best framing:

> "Because this is a new practice, I would like to understand how early success is judged. I can add value quickly on propositions, proposals and client-facing regulatory finance work, but the runway, support and pipeline matter. I want to make sure we set this up so that I am useful from day one and judged against the realities of a build-stage practice."

## Case-study response frame

Use this for written or verbal case studies:

1. Define the client problem.
2. Identify regulator duties, statutory constraints and stakeholder interests.
3. State what evidence is needed.
4. Choose the method: modelling, benchmarking, scenario analysis, consultation review, customer evidence, financeability analysis or cost-benefit analysis.
5. Set out options and trade-offs.
6. Identify risks, sensitivities and assurance steps.
7. Translate analysis into a recommendation.
8. Define outputs: report, model, workshop, board paper, consultation response, expert memo or implementation plan.
9. Set team roles and timetable.
10. Close with how the work helps the client make a defensible decision.

## Risk answers

### "You have not been in consulting before."

> "That is true. What I have done is highly consulting-adjacent: ambiguous problems, senior stakeholders, evidence under time pressure, regulatory submissions, workshops, policy papers and Board-level recommendations. I have also been on the client/regulator side, so I know what makes advisory work credible and what makes it weak."

### "Can you bring clients?"

> "I would not claim to arrive with a full portable book. My value is more practical: I can identify live regulatory problems, turn them into credible propositions, support warm introductions and workshops, and help partners write and pitch stronger proposals. Over time, that is how I would build a market-facing profile."

### "Can you write proposals?"

> "I understand the skill is not just writing text; it is structuring a credible answer with incomplete information. My approach would be to define the problem, regulatory context, evidence, method, options, risks, outputs and team. That is similar to the way I have built submissions, business cases and Board papers, but I would want to learn S&W's house style quickly."

## Final posture

Be warm, concise and commercially curious. Your job is to show that you are credible enough to put in front of clients, practical enough to support delivery, and thoughtful enough to help build a practice without pretending the risk is zero.
