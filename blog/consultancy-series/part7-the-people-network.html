The People Network

One chart, every documented cross-firm move in the UK economics consultancy market 2000–2025. Who came from where. Who went where. A chord diagram mapping thirty-plus senior moves across a market where the same dozen firms exchange the same people.

Leath · April 2026 · 9 min read

A village, not an industry

Count the senior economists in British competition consultancy and the total comes to perhaps a thousand. Narrow the lens to the named partners whose moves surface in Global Competition Review when they happen, and the population shrinks to a few hundred — a village, really, populated by the same dozen firms trading the same dozen people. That village has been in continuous motion for twenty-five years. The pattern of who ends up where is not random; it follows the gravitational pull of three or four dominant firms, the occasional regulator, and the intermittent breakaway that rewrites the map.

What follows is every documented cross-firm senior move that primary sources will attest to. Each arc is a single person's journey from one firm to another. The width of the arc tracks the seniority of the move — partners weighted more heavily than directors, directors more heavily than principal economists. Hover any arc to see the person and the year.

Five features of this chart deserve commentary before the numbers are discussed.

1. The big ribbon between Compass Lexecon and Econic

The fattest arc on the diagram connects Compass Lexecon to Econic Partners. In February 2025 twenty-five-plus economists walked out of Compass Lexecon to found Econic; the exiting cast included Jonathan Orszag, Mark Israel, Kirsten Edwards-Warren and — confirmed by the Financial Times in May 2025 — Enrique Andreu, who had led Compass Lexecon’s Brussels office. Part 5 tells the story in detail. On the chord diagram it is the single largest movement of senior people the UK market has seen in any single event since the records begin — a ribbon so fat it tilts the whole chart.

2. The Keystone build-and-drain

Keystone sits near the top of the diagram with two inbound ribbons carrying three senior arrivals — Coscelli and Hunt from the CMA, Calanchi from Compass Lexecon — and two outbound ribbons carrying three senior departures: Calanchi onward to Econic, Hunt to AlixPartners. (Caffarra's move to UCL sits outside this network of consultancies and so does not appear on the chart.) In two and a half years Keystone assembled a European leadership team and then lost it. Only Coscelli remains.

3. The CMA as a source node, not a destination

The CMA has a cluster of arrows leaving it — to Keystone, Oxera, Frontier, AlixPartners — and essentially no arrows arriving. This is what Part 6 called the regulator-as-recruiter phenomenon: the CMA exports talent at the senior level and does not import it. The partners who left the CMA between 2022 and 2025 are not being replaced by partners joining the CMA from private consultancy. They are being replaced, if at all, by promoting junior case handlers from within. The flow is one-way.

4. A surprising ribbon: AlixPartners to Compass Lexecon (Sangha, 2020)

Rameet Sangha's 2020 move from AlixPartners, where she was a Director, to Compass Lexecon is one of the more interesting arcs on the chart, and interesting because it runs backward. It runs in the opposite direction to the 2024–25 AlixPartners build-out; the same firm that is now hiring ex-CMA economists to stand up a competition-economics practice was, five years ago, losing a senior director to Compass Lexecon. The AlixPartners economics practice of 2020 was a different creature from the AlixPartners economics practice of 2025. That is a real market-structure story: a firm exited a segment and re-entered five years later with an entirely different strategy — and a different set of people to execute it.

5. Frontier Economics has almost no arrows

Frontier is the cleanest node on the chart. Almost nobody arrives mid-career and almost nobody leaves. The 2025 hiring of Mike Walker, ex-CMA Chief Economist, is one of the few inbound arrows in an entire decade. This is the employee-ownership effect in working form: profit at Frontier goes to staff rather than a small partner class, and staff who are drawing their share of profit do not leave as often. The Part 5 story about Frontier looks quaintly unlike the stories told about Baringa or Compass Lexecon.

Three observations follow. The first is that Compass Lexecon is the hub of the network: it holds the most inbound weight on the diagram, anchors the single fattest ribbon, and appears on more ribbons than any other firm. The economists of UK competition economics pass through Compass Lexecon more often than through any other firm. The second concerns RBB, which has the smallest footprint of any large firm on the diagram. In twenty-three years, only two documented senior moves touch it — Benoît Durand, who joined RBB as an LLP member from 1 April 2011 (per Companies House), having previously served at the Competition Commission, and Helder Vasconcelos more recently, both inbound. Nobody has walked out of RBB to found a rival. Nobody has been poached at the partner level. It is the lowest churn rate in the industry. Third: Frontier Economics sits just behind, with a single inbound arc in twenty-seven years — the second-lowest-churn node on the chart.

The two firms with the strongest staff-retention profiles are also the two firms with the most distinctive economic models. RBB runs a small closed partnership; Frontier runs on employee ownership. Everyone else is somewhere in the middle: bigger, more open to lateral movement, more exposed to the Compass-Lexecon-style risk that a senior team walks out of the door together on a Friday and founds something new on Monday.

Four people to watch

Four named individuals are disproportionately central to the network. Follow them through the chart and most of the story of the last five years explains itself.

Stefan Hunt

Hunt traces two distinct arcs across three firms on the diagram: CMA to Keystone to AlixPartners, with a final destination as of December 2025 at AlixPartners UK LLP. He is the clearest evidence on record that a single senior economist can move three times in three years and have each move stand as a story in its own right. His case is the scaffolding on which Part 5 built its thesis.

Gianmarco Calanchi

Five nodes in his career trajectory: UK Department of Energy and Climate Change, then OFT / CMA, then Compass Lexecon, then Keystone Europe, and finally Econic Partners. That is the longest trajectory any individual traces on the whole chart. If the porousness of the market needs one career to illustrate it, Calanchi's is the one.

Jorge Padilla

Padilla has two arcs, both institutional. The first runs from LECG to Compass Lexecon in 2011, when LECG went bankrupt and Padilla's European team transferred en bloc. The second runs inside Compass Lexecon itself, from partner to Chair in the 2024 reshuffle. Padilla is the economist who has held the European competition-economics centre of gravity across the single biggest firm failure in the market's history and the single biggest breakaway from it. Every other Compass Lexecon story in the last fifteen years has had to reckon with him.

Kirsten Edwards-Warren

Two arcs, one year apart. Edwards-Warren was named co-head of Compass Lexecon EMEA in February 2024. She walked out a year later with Orszag and Israel to co-found Econic Partners, and she is the only second statutory director of Econic U.K. Limited alongside Orszag himself — a structural position that makes her the most visible Compass Lexecon departure in the 2025 exodus. The framing of Econic as a breakaway from Compass Lexecon rests, more than on anyone else's, on her name.

Key takeaways

  1. Every documented senior move traceable in the UK economics-consultancy market between 2000 and 2025 fits on a single chord diagram; the market, by any honest measure, is a village rather than an industry.
  2. Compass Lexecon is the hub of the network, holding the most inbound weight on the diagram and anchoring the single fattest ribbon on the chart — the one running to Econic Partners.
  3. RBB Economics and Frontier Economics are the two lowest-churn nodes on the diagram; RBB's closed partnership and Frontier's employee ownership retain senior staff at rates no other firm matches.
  4. The CMA has a cluster of outbound arcs and no inbound arcs at the senior level; it is a talent source, not a talent destination.
  5. Four individuals — Hunt, Calanchi, Padilla and Edwards-Warren — appear on a disproportionate number of arcs, and tracing their careers does most of the work of explaining how the market has reorganised since 2020.
