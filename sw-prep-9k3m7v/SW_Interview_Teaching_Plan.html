4 · The three set questions — and the architecture of your answers

Q1 — "Tell me about you."

≤ 5 minutes. Three-act structure: This is me · This is what I've done · This is why it's relevant to what you're building.

Drilled pitch (rehearse this out loud, time yourself, then strip it back)

"I'm a senior UK economist with about ten years across regulated infrastructure, government-backed SME finance, and energy regulation. MSc Economics, Nottingham, distinction on dissertation; Government Economic Service associate. I sit on the Society of Professional Economists Council, and I'm a Non-Executive Director at Wenta, a small-business growth charity."

Act 2 — This is what I've done (3 min, three chapters).

  1. Ofgem (2015–2018): started in enforcement, including a Chapter II Competition Act case against SSE. Authored the gain & detriment penalty handbook. Then moved into Markets and Networks, co-authored two chapters of the 2017 State of the Market Report (security of supply, energy market competition), and drove behavioural-insights implementation across 200+ policies, advising on ROAMEF and CBA in Impact Assessments. That's where I learnt UK regulatory economics from the inside.
  2. British Business Bank (2018–2024): led the £825m Start Up Loan programme economics, authored four annual Small Business Finance Market reports and the UK Private Debt research report, applied causal-inference and impact-evaluation methods to a £multi-billion intervention. That gave me the financial services and policy-evaluation muscle — exactly the Green Book/Magenta Book/Aqua Book toolkit S&W uses.
  3. Smart DCC (Mar 2024–present): Senior Regulatory Finance Manager in the CFO Finance Team for the GB smart-metering monopoly. Three signature wins. First, I rebuilt the Baseline Margin model from scratch after Ofgem rejected my predecessor's submission — secured the highest BMA in DCC history, which determines about a third of Capita's annual profit. Second, I rebuilt the Penalty Interest Tracker after Ofgem tightened the variance band from 110% to 105% of Allowed Revenue, drove a £55.1m cash repatriation to customers, and avoided any penalty trigger. 6 · Schellion's three published S&W insights — read these end-to-end

She wrote (or co-authored) all three. Expect her to mention at least one. Be able to summarise the argument in 30 seconds and have one informed reaction to each.

A · UK Subsidy Control — economic reasoning comes to the fore
  • Argument: the CMA Subsidy Advice Unit has now reviewed 100+ schemes; its updated August 2025 guidance demands more analytical clarity, an explicit link between policy objective and economic mechanism, and a structured assessment of distortive effects.
  • Framework: CMO test (Commercial Market Operator counterfactual) + Schedule 1 principles assessment (policy objective, incentive effects, competition/investment impact, balancing exercise).
  • What's changing: assessments must consider indirect impacts, dynamic responses (entry/exit/investment displacement), and broader market-structure implications. Policy justification and economic reasoning developed in parallel, not sequentially.
  • Your reaction (rehearse one): "The 'parallel, not sequential' framing maps onto what I see in regulatory finance — submissions land badly when the economics is bolted on at the end. DCC has had similar feedback on Charging Methodology language."
B · Political and geopolitical risk in the cost of capital
  • Argument: CAPM-based WACCs are largely silent on political and geopolitical risk, yet utilities are more exposed than the market because price controls are themselves a policy instrument.
  • Distinction: systematic risk (war, sanctions, trade regimes) vs idiosyncratic risk (UK-specific net zero timelines, windfall taxes, vendor bans, statutory duty shifts).
  • Recommendation: don't add a UK political-risk premium on top of ERP — that double-counts and introduces subjectivity. Instead: (i) let systematic risk flow through Rf and ERP honestly; (ii) reflect sector-specific exposure in beta only where clearly systematic; (iii) handle idiosyncratic risk via cash-flow mechanisms and regulatory design, not WACC loadings.
  • Your reaction (rehearse one): "The 'cash-flow vs WACC' split is exactly the conversation DCC has been having about whether 4G geopolitical-supply-chain risk belongs in the Allowed Revenue cost stack or in the cost of capital. Your framing gives the regulator a defensible doctrine, which is what we've been missing."
C · Why UK regulators need to rethink efficiency modelling
  • Argument: OLS/COLS/SFA/DEA benchmarking rests on assumptions that no longer hold — firm homogeneity, stable technology, short-term cost minimisation, sufficient data. With only 10 water companies, 6 DNO groups, a handful of airports, inference is fragile.
  • Recommendation: next-generation efficiency modelling should integrate economics, engineering, and risk. Use scenario-based benchmarking (demand, climate, technology, risk scenarios) rather than single deterministic models. Risk-adjusted cost assessment; long-term value over single-period optimisation.
  • Quote to remember: "Efficiency modelling is becoming more multidimensional, with greater emphasis on engineering evidence, long-term value and risk-adjusted cost assessment."
  • Your reaction (rehearse one): "This lands on DCC too — we have a sample size of one. The Charging Methodology process leans heavily on engineering judgement because there's no peer group to benchmark against. Your framework gives that judgement a defensible structure, rather than treating it as the second-best to econometrics."

9 · Landmines & what to avoid

  • Never link Smart DCC to your private research. The consultancy series, the Anthropic Fellowship, the 96-firm database — these are your independent research projects, kept fully separate from your employer. If she asks about Smart DCC connections, that is the line.
  • Do not quote her on GT/Cinven. She explicitly said off-the-record. Treat that as a vault.
  • Do not over-anchor on grade. Kas said "senior manager grade, open-minded". Don't volunteer level expectations — let them shape the conversation. If she asks: "I'd be guided by you on the level — what matters more to me is the work mix and the trajectory."
  • Do not slip into sales mode on the "tell me about you". Kas's specific warning. Five minutes hard cap. Practise with a stopwatch.
  • Do not interrupt her opening monologue. She talks fast and a lot. Let her finish. Notes on paper, not the laptop.
  • Do not bluff econometrics. Jonathan is the practising econometrician at partner level; Schellion respects depth. Better to say "I want to deepen this with you" than to overclaim. Anthropic Fellowship + RCT design is a credible taste signal, not a deep-tooling claim.
  • Do not undersell DCC. A regulated-monopoly Senior Reg Finance Manager who has written price-control chapters and run a £55m repatriation is a serious credential for this firm. Lean in.
