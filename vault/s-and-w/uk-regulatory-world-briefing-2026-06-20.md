# UK Regulatory World Briefing for S&W Interview

Prepared 20 June 2026. This is a live interview-prep note on the UK regulatory developments the recruiter explicitly pointed toward: RIIO-3, ED3, water reform and Cunliffe, Smart DCC as infrastructure/data, telecoms transferability, financial services, and competition/consumer pricing.

## Bottom Line

The UK regulatory story right now is not one sector story. It is a cross-sector shift:

- Regulators are being asked to unlock investment and growth while keeping bills, prices and consumer outcomes defensible.
- Old benchmarking and price-control methods are being stretched by decarbonisation, digital infrastructure, resilience, cyber risk, asset health and political pressure.
- Water is moving toward institutional redesign; energy is moving through major network investment and electrification; telecoms is balancing fibre investment with competition; financial services is explicitly trying to rebalance risk and growth; the CMA is using stronger direct consumer powers on pricing and online choice.

The interview line:

> "The common theme across energy, water, telecoms, financial services and consumer markets is that regulation is becoming more supervisory, investment-facing and evidence-heavy. That is exactly where my background is useful: I have worked inside the regulator, inside a public finance institution and inside regulated digital infrastructure."

## Energy: RIIO-3 And ED3

Current facts:

- Ofgem published RIIO-3 Final Determinations on 4 December 2025 for electricity transmission, gas transmission and gas distribution.
- RIIO-3 runs from 1 April 2026 to 31 March 2031.
- Ofgem describes RIIO as the framework for giving monopoly gas and electricity network companies enough revenue to invest and run their networks while delivering what customers value.
- ED3 is the next electricity distribution price control. Ofgem says ED3 starts on 1 April 2028 and runs for five years to 31 March 2033.
- Ofgem published the ED3 Sector Specific Methodology Decision on 21 May 2026, covering policy, cost and finance decisions that inform DNO business plan guidance.
- The 2026 Ofgem Review final report is also part of the live backdrop: it links Ofgem reform to consumer protection, net zero, investment, growth, better accountability and a more outcomes-focused regulator.

What it means:

Energy regulation is moving from "classic cost efficiency" to "investment under uncertainty". Electricity networks must support electrification, connections, resilience and decarbonisation. Gas networks still need safety and resilience, but face uncertainty over long-term demand and asset use.

Interview answer:

> "RIIO-3 and ED3 show the central trade-off in UK regulation now: consumers need protection from inefficient spending, but networks also need investable frameworks for net zero, security and resilience. That makes cost assessment, uncertainty mechanisms, financeability, engineering evidence and incentives more important than a simple efficiency benchmark."

How to link your experience:

> "At DCC I am not working in a classic energy network, but the regulatory-finance logic is very similar: charging, allowed revenue, value for money, long-term investment, customer impacts, delivery risk and how to make a regulated company's position transparent to Ofgem."

If Jonathan asks about the Ofgem Review:

> "The Ofgem Review matters because it is about regulator capability, not just individual energy rules. Government wants Ofgem to be clearer, more accountable, more investment-aware and more outcomes-focused while preserving independence. For advisory work, that means clients need help translating policy direction into evidence, financeability arguments and practical regulatory submissions."

## Smart DCC: The Best Transferability Story

Current facts:

- Ofgem is consulting on DCC Required Revenue for November 2026 to March 2028.
- Ofgem says DCC operates as a monopoly and its costs are controlled under its licence regime.
- Ofgem is moving DCC from an ex-post form of price control to a forward-looking ex-ante framework.
- DCC2 Ltd was formally awarded the next Smart Meter Communication Licence in May 2026, with the planned transfer of responsibilities in November 2026.
- The DCC2 model is framed around not-for-profit, purpose-led operation, stronger governance, cost transparency and long-term consumer value.

Why this matters in the interview:

DCC is the bridge between your current job and S&W's wider regulatory/economic advisory work. It is energy-adjacent, telecoms-adjacent, infrastructure-adjacent and data-platform-adjacent.

Interview answer:

> "DCC is regulated by Ofgem because of the smart metering policy context, but economically it looks like critical digital infrastructure: secure communications, data flows, service performance, technology migration, cyber and resilience, and a monopoly cost-control framework. The live DCC2 transition and move toward ex-ante regulation make that directly relevant to broader infrastructure regulation."

If Jonathan pushes "why does this transfer to telecoms?":

> "Because the core problems are the same: how to regulate a platform that other market participants depend on, how to fund technology migration, how to measure service quality and resilience, and how to protect users from monopoly costs while keeping the infrastructure investable."

## Water: Cunliffe, Defra And The Single Regulator

Current facts:

- The Independent Water Commission, chaired by Sir Jon Cunliffe, published its final report in July 2025.
- GOV.UK describes the review as covering strategic direction, legislative framework, regulator reform, regulation reform, company structures and governance, infrastructure and asset health, and implementation.
- Defra's water White Paper says the government will create a powerful single new regulator by abolishing Ofwat and bringing together relevant functions from Ofwat, the Drinking Water Inspectorate, the Environment Agency and Natural England.
- The new approach is meant to give a whole-firm view of economic and environmental performance, with more proactive, risk-based, company-specific and outcomes-focused supervision.
- The White Paper emphasises financial resilience, infrastructure asset health, fair bills, investment and better environmental performance.

What it means:

Water is the most obvious example of UK regulatory legitimacy under pressure. The issue is not only bills or sewage. It is whether a fragmented regulatory architecture can manage environmental standards, consumer protection, investment, financial resilience and asset health at the same time.

Interview answer:

> "Water is where the UK is closest to a regulatory reset. Cunliffe and the White Paper are not just about abolishing Ofwat; they are about moving from fragmented oversight to a more integrated supervisory model, with stronger company-specific understanding, asset-health evidence and financial-resilience oversight."

How to compare water with energy:

> "Energy and water share the price-review toolkit: allowed revenues, cost assessment, incentives, financeability and outcomes. The difference is the pressure point. Energy is about scaling investment for electrification and net zero. Water is about restoring trust, fixing asset health, environmental performance and financial resilience."

## Telecoms And Digital Infrastructure

Current facts:

- Ofcom published the Telecoms Access Review 2026-31 statement on 17 March 2026.
- The review covers fixed telecoms markets underpinning broadband, mobile and business connections from April 2026 to March 2031.
- Ofcom frames the decisions as promoting competition and investment in gigabit-capable networks.
- The government's strategic priorities for telecoms also emphasise security and resilience, including power resilience, outage data and cross-sector links between telecoms and energy.

What it means:

Telecoms is a natural adjacent sector for S&W because it combines infrastructure investment, network competition, market power, regulated access, quality of service, resilience and data reporting.

Interview answer:

> "The telecoms story is about completing fibre investment while preserving network competition and resilience. That is very close to the regulatory economics of DCC: monopoly or quasi-monopoly infrastructure, platform dependency, technology migration, resilience, service quality and cost transparency."

If asked why your DCC work is relevant:

> "DCC gives me a real example of digital infrastructure that sits between regulated sectors. It is not just energy policy; it is communications, data, security, service levels, charging and technology migration. That makes telecoms a sensible adjacent sector rather than a leap."

## Financial Services

Current facts:

- The FCA annual work programme 2026/27 was published in March 2026.
- The FCA says 2026/27 is the second year of its five-year strategy.
- The four strategic priorities are: a smarter regulator, supporting growth, helping consumers navigate their financial lives, and fighting financial crime.
- The FCA frames its work around deepening trust, rebalancing risk, supporting growth and improving lives.
- Cross-cutting areas include payments reform, digital and data capability, proportionate regulation, capital investment and liquidity, digital innovation, consumer outcomes and financial crime.

What it means:

The financial-services story is not just conduct regulation. It is the same UK-wide theme: regulators are being pushed to be proportionate, growth-aware and consumer-outcome focused at the same time.

Interview answer:

> "My British Business Bank experience does not make me a retail banking conduct specialist, but it gives me strong financial-market evidence experience: SME finance, credit constraints, public finance schemes, value for money, policy evaluation and how finance-market frictions affect growth. That is a credible bridge into FCA and payments work, especially where the question is market functioning, consumer outcomes or public-policy evaluation."

If asked what you would need to learn:

> "I would need to build more detailed FCA sourcebook and Consumer Duty knowledge. But I know how to learn a regulatory framework quickly, and my transferable strength is evidence, market analysis, policy design and turning complex financial-market information into decision-ready advice."

## CMA, DMCC And Pricing Practices

Current facts:

- The CMA's direct consumer enforcement update says its first public enforcement focus has been drip pricing, fake reviews and online choice architecture.
- In April 2026, the CMA said AA Driving School and BSM admitted liability for drip pricing, agreed refunds of over GBP760,000 and AA was fined GBP4.2 million.
- The CMA also carried out a sweep of review publishers, issued advisory letters to 54 firms, and opened investigations into five companies over misleading reviews.
- The 2026 annual concurrency report says the CMA and sector regulators continue to coordinate on competition enforcement and markets work, with reform proposals under discussion for how concurrency would work under a proposed single-phase market review model.
- This fits S&W's own pricing article: dynamic pricing, drip pricing, partitioned pricing, reference pricing, urgency/scarcity claims, "from" pricing and default options are now live compliance and economic-evidence topics.

What it means:

Competition and consumer protection are converging around pricing architecture, online journeys and evidence. The question is not simply whether prices are high. It is whether consumers can understand, compare and act on the true offer.

Interview answer:

> "The DMCC gives the CMA a much sharper toolkit, and the early enforcement pattern is very relevant to S&W: pricing transparency, drip pricing, fake reviews and online choice architecture. Economists can add value by testing whether pricing claims are substantiated, whether consumer journeys support informed choice, and whether alternative pricing designs preserve commercial value without misleading consumers."

How to link it to your case study:

> "The Pedalford e-scooter case is the same disciplined logic: do not jump from a complaint to a legal conclusion. Start with market structure, consumer harm, counterfactual, evidence, alternative explanations and proportionate remedies."

If Jonathan asks about regulated sectors and competition:

> "The concurrency report is useful because it shows that sector regulation and competition law are not separate worlds. The CMA, Ofgem, Ofcom, FCA, PSR, Ofwat and others are still coordinating on market studies, enforcement, remedies and reform. That is exactly the kind of interface where economic consulting can help: turning sector knowledge into competition evidence and vice versa."

## The General UK Regulatory Mood

The wider UK policy backdrop is pro-growth but not deregulatory. The government's regulatory action plan says regulation should support innovation and economic growth while maintaining accountability and quality. It also says economic regulation matters for essential infrastructure and investment.

In interview language:

> "The regulatory mood is not 'less regulation' in a simple sense. It is better targeted regulation: clearer duties, more proportionate process, stronger investment signals, better evidence, and faster accountability where consumers or infrastructure outcomes are failing."

That is a good S&W pitch:

> "This creates demand for economists who can sit between regulators, companies, investors, legal advisers and government, and make the evidence credible enough to survive challenge."
