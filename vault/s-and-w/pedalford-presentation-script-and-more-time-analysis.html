Pedalford Case Presentation Script And More-Time Analysis

Prepared for Leath Al Obaidi, 20 June 2026.

Prepared for Leath Al Obaidi, 20 June 2026.

Use this with: S&W Pedalford case discussion deck

Core positioning:

My submitted answer was deliberately balanced. The evidence supports a credible competition concern and proportionate intervention, but not a finding of collusion or excessive economic profit on the evidence provided. If I had more time, I would deepen the market definition, demand evidence, profitability analysis, conduct testing and remedy cost-benefit assessment.

One-Minute Opening Script

"I would start with the central judgement. The complaint is credible because all three operators raised prices at roughly the same time, the market is highly concentrated, entry is capped, switching costs exist, demand looks inelastic and contribution margins are high. But I would not jump from that to an unlawful agreement. Parallel pricing can come from tacit coordination, a common cost shock, price leadership or normal convergence in a transparent market.

So my answer is staged. First, investigate and preserve evidence. Second, act on the clearer structural problem: the licence cap and switching frictions. Third, hold more intrusive tools, such as price controls or enforcement action, for stronger evidence of persistent harm or coordination. That is the logic I would walk the Authority through."

Slide 1 - Title

"This deck is a walkthrough of the answer I submitted, not a replacement answer. The aim is to show the reasoning chain: what the complaint tells us, what the numbers show, what they do not show, and what a proportionate Authority response would look like."

Pause point:

  • "The key theme is evidence discipline: concern is justified, but conclusions need the right evidential threshold."

Slide 2 - Source Control

"I am using the corrected version I sent to Emily at 11:03 with the PDF answer and Excel calculations. Emily confirmed receipt, so I am treating that as the submitted version. The core answer was: this is a concentrated market with pricing-power concerns, but parallel pricing alone is not proof of collusion."

If asked about the correction:

"It was a small typo correction. The substance remained the same: cautious diagnosis, proportionate remedies, and no overclaiming."

Slide 3 - The Authority's Questions

"The case can look like five sections, but really it asks three decision questions. Is competition working? Is there consumer harm? And if so, what intervention is justified? I treated Sections A to E as an evidence funnel: structure first, then pricing, then conduct, then harm, then remedies."

Good phrase:

"The calculations are there to support judgement, not to replace judgement."

Slide 4 - Bottom Line

"This is the answer in one slide. There is a credible competition concern: three operators, a licensing cap, high HHI, inelastic demand and high direct-cost margins. But the evidence is not enough for a collusion finding. We do not have communications, cost trends over time, full profitability, or evidence that firms could not have independently followed each other."

If challenged:

"The difference matters because it determines the remedy. The evidence supports investigation and structural/demand-side remedies now, but not the most intrusive tools immediately."

Slide 5 - Market Structure

"I would define the provisional product market as app-based shared e-scooter rental in Pedalford. The geographic market is likely local because the service depends on scooter availability near the user's origin and destination. Other transport modes may constrain demand, but I would need substitution evidence before widening the market."

Add:

"The decisive structural point is the operator cap. The cap means entry is not disciplining incumbents in the normal way, and no new firm has entered for two years."

Slide 6 - HHI And Shares

"The HHI is about 3,550 in Year 3, up from about 3,450 in Year 1. That is a strong screening signal of concentration. But market shares are not frozen: ZoomZip rises from 40% to 45%, FlashRide falls from 35% to 30%, and Scootly stays flat. That means rivalry has weakened, but it has not disappeared."

If asked whether HHI proves harm:

"No. HHI is a screen. It tells us the structure is capable of supporting market power, but harm still needs evidence on prices, margins, switching, entry and conduct."

Slide 7 - Unit Economics

"The unit economics use the average 12-minute trip. Revenue per ride is unlock fee plus per-minute fee times 12. Cost per ride is ride cost plus minute cost times 12. On that basis, contribution margins are around 45% to 48%, and the market average is around 46%."

Important caveat:

"I would call these contribution margins, not economic profits. A true profitability assessment needs fleet depreciation, insurance, licence fees, fixed overheads, central costs, capital employed and cost of capital."

Slide 8 - Elasticity

"The case gives a 10% price rise and a 5% fall in total rides, so the simple implied market elasticity is minus 0.5. That suggests demand was inelastic at current prices and that the price rise could increase revenue. But this is not a causal estimate. Weather, seasonality, fleet availability, service quality and alternative transport prices could also have changed."

Good phrase:

"The elasticity is directionally useful, but not robust enough to carry the case alone."

Slide 9 - Parallel Pricing

"This is the heart of the competition-dynamics question. Parallel pricing is a red flag, but it has several explanations. It could be common costs. It could be price leadership. It could be tacit coordination. It could be explicit communication. The case facts do not let us choose between those explanations yet."

If asked to rank explanations:

"The structure makes tacit coordination plausible, but the share movements and lack of cost evidence mean I would keep price leadership and cost pass-through live."

Slide 10 - Evidence That Would Change The View

"This slide is the workplan. I would request timestamped pricing data, promotion histories, cost movements, pricing papers, competitor-monitoring documents, algorithm logic, ride-level demand data, switching evidence, licence applications and any cross-ownership or communications."

Key line:

"I would preserve coordination as a hypothesis, not treat it as a finding until the plus factors are tested."

Slide 11 - Non-Price Competition

"I would also look beyond headline fares. E-scooter competition can happen through availability, coverage, battery charge, maintenance, app reliability, customer service, subscriptions and promotions. The fact that ZoomZip gains share while FlashRide loses share suggests users may be responding to price or non-price differences."

If asked why this matters:

"Because a purely price-based view could miss real rivalry or real harm. A poor-quality market with similar prices is different from a high-quality market with similar prices."

Slide 12 - Consumer Harm

"The harm case is credible. Consumers face higher prices, total rides fall, and the market has weak entry and switching constraints. Continuing users may be paying more, and some trips may be priced out. But I would avoid saying the full contribution pool is welfare loss. It is evidence of scale and pricing power, not a complete harm estimate."

Good phrase:

"The evidence is enough to justify action, but not enough to quantify final harm precisely."

Slide 13 - Caveats

"This slide is important because the caveats protect the answer. We do not have direct agreement evidence. We have only Year 3 cost data. We do not have full capital costs. The elasticity is crude. The fall in rides may have other causes. But those caveats do not mean do nothing; they mean choose proportionate steps first."

If asked whether the answer was too cautious:

"I would say the caution is the point. It makes the recommendation more robust because the remedy is matched to the evidence."

Slide 14 - Remedies

"The remedy ladder should follow the evidence. The strongest immediate case is to review the licensing cap and reduce switching and search frictions. A fare cap would be direct, but it is harder to set, can reduce quality or investment, and may become a focal price. I would keep it as a backstop."

Add:

"The cap may exist for valid safety and congestion reasons, so I would not simply remove it. I would review whether it can be made more contestable: a fourth-operator trial, periodic retendering, fleet caps, service obligations or entry tied to safety and coverage conditions."

Slide 15 - Staged Workplan

"The staged workplan is: first, open a targeted evidence phase; second, implement low-risk demand-side remedies; third, review the cap; fourth, escalate only if evidence supports stronger action. That gives the Authority something practical to do immediately without overreaching."

Closing line:

"The current facts justify action on structure and consumer mobility while preserving enforcement for stronger coordination evidence."

Slide 16 - Challenges And Answers

"These are the questions I would expect. Does parallel pricing prove collusion? No. Can I say profits are excessive? Not yet. Why intervene if evidence is uncertain? Because the structural problem is clear. Why not impose a price cap now? Because it is intrusive and information-heavy."

Practice answer:

"My approach is not anti-intervention. It is evidence-led intervention."

Slide 17 - Five-Minute Script

"If I had five minutes, I would use this exact structure: thesis, structure, numbers, dynamics, remedies. I would not try to recite every calculation. I would show that I can prioritise, explain uncertainty and give the Authority a decision-ready recommendation."

Slide 18 - Calculation Map

"If someone asks where a number comes from, I would go back to this slide. HHI is 45 squared plus 30 squared plus 25 squared. Revenue and cost per ride use the 12-minute average ride. Elasticity is minus 5 divided by plus 10. The contribution pool is margin per ride times ride volumes."

Final line:

"For every number, I would pair the calculation with its caveat."

Short Answer: What I Did Not Do That Could Have Helped

The submitted answer was strong for a one-hour exercise because it covered structure, HHI, margins, elasticity, coordination caveats and proportionate remedies. What it did not fully do was build a deeper empirical-economics work programme around the case.

If asked directly:

"With more time I would have strengthened five areas: first, market definition using a hypothetical monopolist or substitution framework; second, demand evidence using trip-level data and cross-price substitution; third, profitability using full economic costs and WACC; fourth, conduct testing using price histories, cost shocks and plus factors; and fifth, remedy cost-benefit analysis with clear monitoring metrics and a counterfactual."

That answer sounds reflective without undermining the submitted note.

Detailed More-Time Analysis From Davis & Garces

1. Stronger Market Definition

What I did:

  • I implicitly treated the relevant market as Pedalford app-based e-scooter rental.
  • I noted possible alternatives but did not formally test them.

What I could have added:

  • A provisional product-market discussion: shared e-scooters versus bikes, walking, buses, taxis, ride-hailing and private scooters.
  • A geographic-market discussion: whether competition is city-wide, zone-by-zone, commute-corridor specific or tied to origin/destination density.
  • A hypothetical monopolist / SSNIP framing: would a hypothetical monopolist of Pedalford e-scooter rental profitably raise price by 5-10%?
  • A demand-substitution evidence list: user surveys, app-level switching, cross-price responses, trip purpose, distance bands and local transport alternatives.

Why the book supports this:

  • Davis & Garces treat market definition as an evidence-based judgement, not a purely verbal label.
  • They emphasise demand substitution as central and treat SSNIP/HMT as a structured way to ask whether products outside the candidate market constrain price.

How to say it:

"My market definition was good enough for a one-hour note, but with more time I would formalise it. I would ask whether a hypothetical monopolist of Pedalford e-scooter trips could profitably impose a 5-10% rise, and I would test which alternatives users actually switch to."

2. Critical Loss Analysis

What I did:

  • I calculated that a 10% price rise coincided with a 5% fall in rides.
  • I inferred a crude elasticity of -0.5 and said demand looked inelastic.

What I could have added:

  • A simple critical-loss calculation using the market contribution margin.
  • With a market contribution margin of about 46%, the critical loss for a 10% price increase is approximately:
Critical loss = 10% / (10% + 46%) = 17.9%
  • The observed reduction in rides was about 5%, which is below the 17.9% critical-loss threshold. On a very simplified basis, that suggests the 10% price rise was profitable.
  • For a 5% SSNIP, the critical loss would be:
Critical loss = 5% / (5% + 46%) = 9.8%

Why this helps:

  • It links margins, demand response and market definition.
  • It shows that the observed price increase is not just a consumer complaint; it appears commercially sustainable.

Caveat:

  • The book warns that critical loss can mislead if current prices are already above competitive levels. High margins may themselves indicate market power, so treating the current price as the benchmark can create a "cellophane fallacy" problem.

How to say it:

"A quick critical-loss check would have supported the pricing-power story: actual volume loss was much lower than the loss needed to make a 10% rise unprofitable. But I would caveat it heavily because current prices may already be supra-competitive and the margin data is incomplete."

3. Better Demand And Substitution Evidence

What I did:

  • I used the market-level elasticity implied by the case facts.
  • I caveated that it was single-period and crude.

What I could have added:

  • Distinguish market-level elasticity from firm-level elasticity.
  • Estimate or request own-price and cross-price elasticities by operator.
  • Look at diversion ratios: when one operator raises price or has low availability, where do users go?
  • Segment demand by trip type: commute, leisure, last-mile, short trip, long trip, late-night, tourist use.
  • Use app or ride-level data: location, ride duration, time of day, weather, promotions, availability, battery level and failed search attempts.

Why the book supports this:

  • Davis & Garces put demand and elasticity at the centre of competition analysis.
  • They stress that elasticities are point-specific and that demand evidence must be interpreted carefully.
  • They also distinguish own-price responses from substitution to other products.

How to say it:

"My elasticity calculation was useful as a first pass, but with more data I would not rely on a market-level before-and-after ratio. I would estimate how users switch between operators and between e-scooters and other transport options."

4. Margin-Elasticity Consistency Check

What I did:

  • I reported margins of around 45-48%.
  • I separately reported an implied elasticity of -0.5.

What I could have added:

  • A consistency check between high margins and implied elasticity.
  • In a simple monopoly pricing model, the Lerner relationship links margin and elasticity. A 46% margin would imply a much more elastic residual demand than -0.5 if interpreted mechanically.
  • The apparent tension is informative. It suggests the observed -0.5 is probably not a clean causal own-price elasticity.

Why this helps:

  • It turns a possible weakness into a sophistication point.
  • It shows that the elasticity is useful, but not a clean structural estimate.

How to say it:

"One extra diagnostic I would add is that the 46% contribution margin and the -0.5 aggregate elasticity should not be read together mechanically. The elasticity is market-level and non-causal; the margin is contribution not full economic margin; and the market is an oligopoly with two-part tariffs. That is exactly why I treated the elasticity as indicative only."

5. Full Economic Profitability

What I did:

  • I correctly caveated that the calculated margins are not full economic profits.

What I could have added:

  • A full profitability workplan:
  • fleet purchase or lease costs,
  • depreciation and replacement cycles,
  • maintenance and charging,
  • insurance,
  • licence fees,
  • enforcement/compliance cost,
  • overhead allocation,
  • working capital,
  • capital employed,
  • cost of capital / WACC,
  • multi-year returns.

Why the book supports this:

  • Davis & Garces discuss profitability and welfare calculations as assumption-heavy.
  • They point toward more careful treatment of capital costs and WACC in modern applications.

How to say it:

"I would not call the margins excessive profit without a ROCE or economic-profit analysis. What I calculated was a useful contribution margin screen, not the final profitability evidence."

6. Welfare And Consumer Harm Quantification

What I did:

  • I identified higher prices, reduced output and weak choice as consumer harm.
  • I did not calculate a formal welfare loss.

What I could have added:

  • A consumer transfer estimate:
  • market average Year 3 fare is about GBP 3.77,
  • a 10% lower fare would be about GBP 3.43,
  • difference is roughly GBP 0.34 per current ride,
  • across 3 million rides that is around GBP 1.0m of extra annual expenditure for continuing riders.
  • A rough deadweight-loss estimate:
  • using the observed fall in rides, the triangle from the 10% price increase is small relative to transfer,
  • but this depends heavily on whether the observed fall is causal and whether the benchmark is competitive.
  • Quality-adjusted harm:
  • less availability,
  • more failed searches,
  • lower coverage,
  • worse battery reliability,
  • safety and maintenance effects.

Why the book supports this:

  • Davis & Garces discuss consumer surplus, transfers and deadweight loss, but warn that welfare calculations depend heavily on assumptions and data quality.

How to say it:

"With more time I would quantify harm in layers: first the transfer to firms from higher fares, then lost trips, then quality effects. I would keep the welfare estimate illustrative because the causal demand estimate is weak."

7. Conduct Testing And Identification

What I did:

  • I listed evidence that would help test coordination.

What I could have added:

  • A more formal identification strategy:
  • use cost shocks as supply shifters,
  • use weather, events or transit disruption as demand shifters,
  • use weather-by-distance or commute urgency as demand rotators,
  • run event studies around each price change,
  • compare Pedalford with other cities or zones if data exists,
  • test whether prices respond more to rivals than to costs.

Why the book supports this:

  • Davis & Garces repeatedly emphasise identification: the need for data variation that distinguishes hypotheses.
  • In conduct analysis, they discuss distinguishing competition, Cournot-type oligopoly and collusive models using demand/cost evidence and conduct parameters, while warning that these methods are data-intensive.

How to say it:

"The key extra piece would be an identification strategy. I would not only ask whether prices moved together; I would ask what independent variation lets us distinguish cost pass-through, price leadership, tacit coordination and explicit coordination."

8. Price Architecture And Two-Part Tariffs

What I did:

  • I used a 12-minute average ride to calculate one standard price per ride.

What I could have added:

  • A short-trip versus long-trip price analysis:
  • 5-minute trip,
  • 12-minute trip,
  • 20-minute trip.
  • Unlock fees matter more for short trips; per-minute rates matter more for long trips.
  • Operators may compete differently across trip lengths even if the headline average looks similar.
  • A better analysis would construct a trip-weighted price index using the actual ride-duration distribution.

Why this helps:

  • It prevents overreliance on a single average ride.
  • It shows how consumers with different trip patterns may face different effective price increases.

How to say it:

"Because this is a two-part tariff, I would ideally calculate a basket of representative trips rather than rely only on a 12-minute average."

9. Non-Price Competition And Quality Adjustment

What I did:

  • I mentioned app reliability, scooter availability, coverage, battery range, safety and promotions.

What I could have added:

  • A quality evidence matrix:
  • scooters available within 3 minutes' walk,
  • average wait/search time,
  • failed unlocks,
  • battery level at unlock,
  • maintenance defects,
  • accident/complaint rates,
  • geographic coverage by neighbourhood,
  • peak-time availability.
  • A quality-adjusted price or "generalised cost" metric combining fare plus search time and reliability.

Why the book supports this:

  • The book is clear that competition variables are not only price. Market definition and competitive effects can turn on quality, quantity, service and innovation.

How to say it:

"I would not want the Authority to look only at fares. If ZoomZip gained share because it had better availability or fewer failed unlocks, that is real competitive evidence."

10. Entry And The Licence Cap

What I did:

  • I treated the three-operator cap as the structural root cause.

What I could have added:

  • A more detailed entry analysis:
  • why the cap exists,
  • whether safety/congestion can be handled through fleet caps rather than operator caps,
  • whether tenders can create competition for the market,
  • whether a fourth-operator trial would lower prices or reduce quality,
  • whether incumbents have scale economies that justify fewer operators.
  • A comparison with other local markets or cities that have more or fewer operators.

Why the book supports this:

  • Davis & Garces discuss how entry thresholds and market size can reveal whether additional firms erode margins and market power.

How to say it:

"The cap is where competition and public-policy design meet. I would ask whether the same safety goals can be achieved with less anti-competitive machinery."

11. Counterfactual And Remedy Evaluation

What I did:

  • I recommended a market study, demand-side remedies and a review of the cap, with price regulation as backstop.

What I could have added:

  • Explicit counterfactuals:
  • no intervention,
  • better switching and transparency only,
  • controlled entry,
  • periodic retendering,
  • temporary fare cap.
  • Success measures:
  • weighted fare,
  • price dispersion,
  • rides and active users,
  • switching and multi-app usage,
  • scooter availability,
  • service quality,
  • complaints,
  • entry interest,
  • operator returns.
  • Sunset clauses and review points for any intrusive remedy.

How to say it:

"With more time I would turn the remedy ladder into a measurable policy package: intervention, expected channel, risk, KPI and sunset condition."

12. Documentary Evidence And Plus Factors

What I did:

  • I said I would request communications, pricing histories, cost trends, cross-ownership and evidence of deviation incentives.

What I could have added:

  • More precise plus factors:
  • communications through industry forums or licensing process,
  • unusual price-move timing,
  • advance signalling,
  • pricing algorithms monitoring rivals,
  • internal documents saying users cannot switch,
  • margin expansion not explained by costs,
  • punishment of deviations,
  • cross-directorships or investor links.

How to say it:

"The plus-factor evidence matters because parallel pricing is compatible with lawful interdependence. The Authority needs something that helps distinguish independent matching from coordination."

What To Say If Asked "Would You Change Your Answer?"

"I would not change the central answer. I would keep the same staged recommendation: investigation, demand-side remedies, cap review, and escalation only if stronger evidence emerges. What I would improve is the analytical scaffolding. I would add a short market-definition framework, a critical-loss sense check, a fuller profitability workplan, a more explicit conduct-identification strategy, and a quantified but caveated consumer-harm estimate."

Things Not To Say

  • Do not say: "I should have concluded collusion."
  • Do not say: "I should have done a full econometric model."
  • Do not say: "The answer was wrong."
  • Do not say: "The margins prove excessive profits."
  • Do not say: "The 5% output fall proves harm by itself."

Better:

"The answer was right for the time constraint. With more time, I would add depth around market definition, causality, profitability and remedy testing."

  2. "The uncertainty changes the remedy ladder, not the need to investigate."
