CH 1Stage-2 mechanics

  • Format: 1-hour take-home, single-prompt brief, sent to your home email.
  • Pass / fail: Kas was explicit — there is no pass / fail on this stage unless it is a complete and utter disaster. The aim is to test thinking, not produce a finished consulting output.
  • Format of response: typically a written 1–3 page memo. Sometimes the brief asks for a verbal walk-through at Stage 3 instead.
  • What is being measured: structure, judgement, ability to identify the right question, ability to triangulate methods, intellectual honesty about uncertainty.
  • What is NOT being measured: the final number. There is no "right answer". A well-structured wrong direction beats a poorly-structured right answer.

CH 2The universal answer architecture

Every economics case at S&W's grade-level sits on the same 7-step backbone. Memorise this and you can answer any prompt.

market failure → theory of harm → counterfactual → evidence → methods → remedies → caveats
StepThe question you answer
1 · Market failureWhat is wrong with the market that requires intervention? (monopoly, information asymmetry, externality, vulnerable consumers, public good, equity)
2 · Theory of harmHow specifically is this market failure manifesting? (price rise, output suppression, foreclosure, distortion, mis-selling) — enumerate, prioritise.
3 · CounterfactualWhat would have happened absent the conduct / intervention / restriction? Make this concrete and testable.
4 · EvidenceWhat evidence is available, what evidence is missing, what evidence would change the analysis?
5 · MethodsHow do you quantify the counterfactual? (Methods toolkit below.) Triangulate — two methods always beat one.
6 · Remedies / recommendationsWhat should the client / regulator / court do? Bounded, proportionate, evidenced.
7 · CaveatsWhat you do not know, what would change your view, what the next analytical step would be.

Walking through these 7 in 60 minutes gets you to ~8 minutes per section. The opening frame and the closing caveats are the highest-leverage parts — examiners read them first.

CH 3The seven case archetypes

Take-home prompts cluster into seven archetypes. Each gets a one-page template below. Identify the archetype from the prompt language in the first 5 minutes.

A · Competition damages / cartel quantum B · Merger control (SLC) C · Market investigation D · Subsidy Control referral E · Regulatory appeal (price control / WACC) F · Consumer protection / DMCC G · Policy intervention appraisal

A · Competition damages / cartel quantum · follow-on or stand-alone

Recognise from prompt

Mentions: claimants, defendants, infringement period, overcharge, "but-for", pass-on, follow-on, CAT.

Market failure

Horizontal coordination or vertical RPM defeats price discovery; output suppressed; consumer surplus transferred upstream.

Theory of harm

  • Direct overcharge to direct purchasers
  • Volume effect — purchases would have been higher absent the conduct
  • Lost profits — claimant's downstream sales suppressed
  • Pass-on (defence): claimant passed overcharge to its own customers

Counterfactual

Set of but-for prices (and volumes) that would have prevailed absent the cartel. Three constructions: pre/during/post window in same market; comparator (yardstick) market; structural-demand simulation.

Methods

DiD (primary), hedonic regression for product mix, structural demand (BLP) for merger simulation, financial-performance analysis for claimant lost profits.

Remedies

Damages = aggregate overcharge × volume + interest from each transaction date. Pass-on rate applied if defended. Class certification at the CAT if collective.

Caveats

Pass-on quantification fragility; volume effects often under-estimated; parallel-trends assumption; data availability.

"This is a follow-on damages claim where the infringement is established and the question is quantum. My preferred approach is a difference-in-differences against a clean comparator, triangulated with a pre/post structure in the same market — both supplemented with a hedonic adjustment for product mix. The defence will press hard on pass-on; my view on pass-on quantification has to be empirical, not assumed."

B · Merger control — SLC test · CMA Phase 1 / Phase 2

Recognise from prompt

Mentions: parties, transaction value, share-of-supply, market overlap, horizontal/vertical/conglomerate, unilateral effects, coordinated effects.

Market failure

Concentration potentially reducing rivalry; risk of unilateral price rise, foreclosure of rivals, coordinated conduct.

Theory of harm

  • Unilateral effects — merged firm finds price rise profitable post-merger as recapture from rival is internalised.
  • Coordinated effects — fewer firms makes tacit coordination feasible.
  • Vertical foreclosure — input or customer foreclosure if vertical.
  • Conglomerate — tying / bundling.

Counterfactual

The pre-existing competitive structure. NB: this is not the status quo if one party would have exited absent the merger — failing-firm defence.

Methods

Market shares + HHI; closeness-of-competition diversion ratios; GUPPI / illustrative price rise (IPR); merger simulation (Bertrand / Cournot or BLP); margin pressure tests.

Remedies

Behavioural (rare); structural (preferred) — divestiture; prohibition. Phase 2 in-depth investigation.

Caveats

Market definition fragility (cellophane fallacy); efficiencies passing test (rarely accepted); failing-firm evidence quality.

"I would structure the merger assessment around the SLC test in three steps — market definition via SSNIP, competitive assessment via diversion ratios and closeness-of-competition evidence, and counterfactual under the prevailing market structure absent the merger. The unilateral effects analysis is usually the heart of the case; coordinated effects need separate evidence of market conditions that make coordination feasible and stable."

C · Market investigation · CMA Enterprise Act Part 4

Recognise from prompt

Mentions: market study, sector inquiry, features of the market, adverse effect on competition (AEC), structural remedies.

Market failure

One or more "features of the market" preventing, restricting or distorting competition — not necessarily attributable to specific anti-competitive conduct.

Theory of harm

  • Concentrated supply
  • Barriers to entry / expansion
  • Switching costs / consumer inertia
  • Information asymmetry
  • Behavioural biases preventing effective shopping
  • Vertical relationships restricting access

Counterfactual

The market as it could function with these features mitigated. Often illustrated via comparators (international, neighbouring sectors).

Methods

Profitability analysis (Modern Equivalent Asset valuation); margin analysis; price-cost tests; switching data; consumer surveys; market-share dynamics; international benchmarks.

Remedies

Structural (most powerful — see UK retail banking remedies); behavioural; informational (open banking, comparison tools); price controls (rare).

Caveats

Profitability test methodology disputes; remedy proportionality; unintended consequences of structural intervention.

"A market investigation differs from a Chapter I or merger case in that it does not require pinpointing specific anti-competitive conduct — it asks whether features of the market prevent effective competition. The analytical workplan needs three layers: a profitability and margin diagnosis; a consumer-side switching and information diagnosis; and a structural-features diagnosis. Each layer informs a different family of remedies."

D · Subsidy Control referral · Subsidy Control Act 2022 + CMA SAU

Recognise from prompt

Mentions: subsidy, grant, public authority, CMA SAU, Schedule 1 principles, CMO test, distortion, market failure.

Market failure

The justification for intervention itself — is there a market failure (under-investment in public good, externality, equity)? Without market failure, the subsidy fails Schedule 1.

Theory of harm

  • Distortion of competition with non-recipient firms
  • Investment displacement (entry / exit / location choices)
  • Cross-subsidisation into adjacent markets
  • Equity vs efficiency trade-off

Counterfactual

Two-part assessment:

  1. CMO test — would a hypothetical Commercial Market Operator have provided the same financing on the same terms? If yes, no subsidy.
  2. Schedule 1 principles — the seven principles assessed sequentially. Additionality / proportionality / minimum-amount are the three most-litigated.

Methods

CMO benchmarking via private-investor comparables; counterfactual investment appraisal; distortion modelling via dynamic responses (entry, displacement).

Remedies

Re-design, time-limit, transparency, claw-back clauses if not retained.

Caveats

CMO test heavily fact-dependent; market-failure articulation often weak in submitting authority's materials (Schellion's published critique); dynamic distortion modelling fragile.

"Under the Subsidy Control Act 2022 a defensible referral works in two stages. First, the CMO test: would a commercial operator have done this on these terms? Second, if subsidy is established, the Schedule 1 principles — and the CMA SAU's August 2025 guidance has raised the bar on articulating market failure and the explicit link between policy objective and economic mechanism. The economic reasoning has to be in the submission from day one, not bolted on at the end."

E · Regulatory appeal — price control / WACC · CMA reference of regulator's final determination

Recognise from prompt

Mentions: Ofwat / Ofgem / CAA / Ofcom; final determination; CMA reference; WACC / cost of equity; financeability; RAB; building blocks; totex; ODIs.

Market failure

Natural monopoly. Building-blocks regulation substitutes for competitive market discipline.

Theory of harm (appellant company-side)

  • WACC under-set — return below cost of capital, capital strike
  • Totex challenge over-aggressive — frontier shift unrealistic
  • Real Price Effects under-recognised
  • Sector-specific risks not reflected (Schellion's geopol-risk piece)
  • Financeability cross-check failed

Counterfactual

The allowed revenue / WACC that would have resulted from a different parameter choice within the regulator's stated framework, or a different framework.

Methods

WACC build-up — Rf, TMR, asset beta, gearing, debt cost. Sensitivity testing each parameter. Financeability ratios (AICR, FFO / Net debt, gearing) under notional vs actual structure. Comparator regulatory determinations.

Remedies

CMA can confirm, modify or reject the regulator's determination. Parameter changes flow through the building-blocks model into allowed revenue.

Caveats

The CMA is on the regulator's framework, not the appellant's — radical reframings rarely succeed. Parameter movements at 10–30bp are the realistic battleground.

"In a price-control appeal the question is rarely whether the regulator's framework is right — it's whether the parameter choices within the framework are defensible. I would build the WACC from the regulator's own stated methodology, sensitivity-test each parameter, and identify two or three where the appellant has the strongest case. Schellion's published view on geopolitical risk maps onto this directly — the case for reflecting heightened risk runs through honest Rf and ERP, sector beta where clearly systematic, and cash-flow mechanisms for idiosyncratic UK policy risk."

F · Consumer protection / DMCC · Unfair commercial practices · pricing transparency

Recognise from prompt

Mentions: DMCC, reference prices, drip pricing, urgency / scarcity messaging, fake reviews, dark patterns, vulnerable consumers, customer journey.

Market failure

Information asymmetry; behavioural biases; vulnerable consumer protection. Standard "average consumer" test moves down to "average vulnerable consumer" when targeted.

Theory of harm

  • Misleading reference prices (savings claims not substantiated)
  • Drip pricing (mandatory fees hidden until late in journey)
  • Fake urgency (countdown timers / "only 2 left" not tied to inventory)
  • Default options not in consumer interest
  • Fake or curated reviews

Counterfactual

Customer behaviour under fair / transparent presentation. Choice-experiment methodology.

Methods

Customer-journey analysis; consumer survey / understanding testing; substantiation of savings (historical price data); substantiation of scarcity (inventory data); behavioural-economics framing.

Remedies

DMCC penalties up to 10% of global turnover (direct CMA enforcement, no court route). Consumer redress orders. Compliance commitments. Director disqualification for repeated breaches.

Caveats

Customer-journey analysis is labour-intensive; survey design has to avoid leading questions; substantiation requires longitudinal price/inventory data the firm may not have retained.

"Under the DMCC the question shifts from 'is the price too high' to 'is the pricing structure, the surrounding claims and the customer journey transparent, substantiated and fair'. The economist's role is to test substantiation: is the reference price a price at which meaningful volume actually sold? Is the urgency claim tied to actual inventory? Is the journey designed to support informed decisions? Schellion's published S&W work sets out this evidence base directly."

G · Policy intervention appraisal · Green Book / Magenta Book / Aqua Book

Recognise from prompt

Mentions: government department, intervention, evaluation, ex-ante appraisal, ex-post evaluation, value for money, distributional analysis, NPV.

Market failure

Treasury appraisal is built on identifying the specific market failure first. If you cannot articulate the market failure, the appraisal has no foundation.

Theory of harm (or benefit)

Either: where is intervention needed? (under-investment, equity, externality) Or: what specifically did the intervention change?

Counterfactual

The "do nothing" case is mandatory. Multiple options compared. Distributional and NPV-weighted.

Methods

Green Book (ex-ante): 5-case model, NPV, STPR 3.5% discount, optimism bias, distributional weighting. Magenta Book (ex-post): theory of change, contribution analysis, RCT or quasi-experimental design (DiD / IV / RDD). Aqua Book: model QA.

Remedies / recommendations

Continue, modify, scale, sunset, replicate. Distributional adjustments. Optimism-bias re-calibration.

Caveats

Counterfactual identification; optimism-bias accuracy; long-term benefits realisation; equity vs efficiency.

"A defensible policy appraisal starts from a clearly articulated market failure, applies the Treasury's 5-case model and STPR discounting, and is explicit about counterfactual and distributional assumptions. For ex-post evaluation under the Magenta Book the choice is between RCT where possible and quasi-experimental designs — DiD, IV, RDD, synthetic control — where it isn't. My preference is to triangulate two designs and to write the assumption that would falsify the result in advance."

CH 5Evidence hierarchy

  1. Contractual / documentary — dealer agreements, internal emails, board minutes. Wins object infringements.
  2. Pricing data — daily / weekly, list and transaction, by channel.
  3. Volume / sales data — for aggregation and pass-on.
  4. Comparator data — international, neighbouring product, neighbouring period.
  5. Consumer survey — primary research where journey or understanding matters.
  6. Complaint data — Citizens Advice, ombudsman, trade body, firm CRM.
  7. Behavioural / journey analytics — click-stream, A/B test results.
  8. Expert engineering / industry evidence — bottom-up cost / quality / capacity.

CH 7What the examiners are looking for

  • Structure before content. A clearly-framed weaker answer beats a brilliant unstructured answer.
  • Triangulation. Two methods, not one. Always.
  • Intellectual honesty. "I don't know but I would test X" is signal, not weakness.
  • Knowledge of the regulator's framework. Cite the CMA / DMCC / Treasury appraisal language. Use the right vocabulary.
  • The two-clause sentence. "Yes, but" — never just yes. "On balance X, although Y" — never one-sided. That cadence is the audible signature of a senior economist.
  • Bounding the claim. "Subject to data limitations" / "without doing the modelling I can't be certain" / "my preferred direction is X". Never over-claim.
  • Recommendation that survives cross-examination. Not "do X" but "do X provided Y, evidenced by Z, with the following sensitivity".

CH 8Common traps

  • Diving into methodology before identifying the question. The single most common failure mode.
  • Confusing market definition with market structure. Define the market first; then assess competitive conditions.
  • Ignoring the counterfactual. "Prices rose X%" means nothing without "compared to what".
  • Over-quantifying with bad data. Better to qualitatively bound a parameter than to assert a precise number you cannot defend.
  • Forgetting the regulator's framework. Especially in price-control appeals — the CMA reviews on the regulator's framework, not yours.
  • Pretending to know a case. Citing Interchange or Trucks vaguely is worse than not citing.
  • Saying "I think" three times in two minutes. Replace with "my view, subject to evidence, would be…".
  • Skipping the caveats. The closing two paragraphs are read first by examiners.

CH 9Pre-flight checklist

Before you start writing (5 minutes max)

  1. Identify the archetype (A–G).
  2. Identify the answer-recipient (who is the client? a regulator? a court? a board?).
  3. Identify the role you are playing (advisor / appointed expert / counterfactual analyst).
  4. Identify the data the prompt gives you and the data it does not.
  5. Note the explicit sub-questions if any.
  6. Set the framing sentence aloud in your head before typing.

Before you submit (final 2 minutes)

  1. Does the opening paragraph answer the question?
  2. Is the architecture (market failure → harm → counterfactual → evidence → methods → remedies → caveats) visible?
  3. Are there two methods, not one?
  4. Is there a "what would change my view" sentence?
  5. Are there any unsupported numerical claims? Bound or remove.
  6. Is the tone advisory, not advocacy?
