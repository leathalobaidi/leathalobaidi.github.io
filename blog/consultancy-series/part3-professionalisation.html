The Professionalisation: PE Arrives, 2010-2015

Part 3 of 7: LECG imploded, its European economists scattered across three doors, and a patient English private equity house concluded that a research boutique in the City of London was worth four times its revenues. The money had begun to arrive.

Leath · 10 April 2026 · 13 min read

Opening: The LECG Collapse, 2011

On 3 March 2011, LECG Corporation notified the Securities and Exchange Commission that it could no longer file its annual report on time; eleven weeks later it had sold off what remained and wound itself down. The collapse was sudden but the firm was not small. At its peak, a year earlier, after absorbing SMART Business Advisory and Consulting in a merger that was supposed to save it, LECG employed more than a thousand professionals across almost forty offices worldwide (SEC 8-K, June 2010). By the time the lawyers arrived the headcount had already thinned. Yet the firm had been one of the three largest global economics consultancies, and in European competition work it was the dominant independent house on the continent. It fell quickly. It fell cleanly. It took a decade of accumulated talent with it.

LECG had been founded in 1988 by David Teece, a Berkeley economist with a Kiwi accent and a taste for the adjacent practice, and it had grown by buying up boutique after boutique. By 2010 its European competition team ran to more than forty economists. Jorge Padilla, a Spaniard based in London, presided over an EMEA network that stretched to Brussels, Madrid, and Paris and included Neil Coombs, Elisabetta Coppi, and Neil Dryden. On paper the business looked valuable; in the event it was insolvent.

What killed LECG: a leveraged acquisition binge, a bad 2010 earnings print, and a stock price that fell from $20 in 2007 to under $1 by early 2011. The firm's bankers pulled the credit line in February 2011. Once the economists realised the parent could not pay bonuses, the partnership began shopping itself in pieces.

The LECG collapse is the formative event of the modern UK economics consultancy market. Nothing else between 2010 and 2015 moved so much talent, redrew competitive lines so decisively, or cleared the runway so plainly for the private equity arrivals that followed. The forty-plus European economists did not file out through a single door. They scattered through three.

LECG Corporation, March 2011
Path 1: Compass Lexecon absorbs the Padilla team
Jorge Padilla, Neil Coombs, Elisabetta Coppi, Neil Dryden and the London, Brussels, Madrid, and Paris teams joined Compass Lexecon, then a subsidiary of FTI Consulting. Padilla took the role of Senior Managing Director and Head of Europe. This single transaction established Compass Lexecon as the dominant London competition economics player for the next decade.
Path 2: OSKR forms in the United States
A group of LECG's US economists, led by Ordover, Shampine, Kalt and Rubinfeld, launched OSKR in Oakland as a direct LECG successor. The firm was pitched as "LECG 2.0" for the US market. It stayed small and never built a meaningful European presence.
Path 3: Teece founds BRG
David Teece, co-founder of LECG and its intellectual anchor, set up Berkeley Research Group later in 2011. Many LECG economists followed him directly. The client lists, the practice areas, the hiring templates all transferred. BRG is the underreported story of this period.

That last door deserves a second glance. BRG is essentially LECG reborn. The trade press treated Compass Lexecon's capture of the Padilla team as the headline story of 2011, and so it was; but in the longer arc, Teece's quiet decision to start over was just as consequential. BRG hired aggressively, grew quickly, and by 2015 its London office was billing enough to register on the GCR 100 rankings. The DNA was LECG DNA. The firm has never once marketed itself on that inheritance.

Compass Lexecon becomes the giant

Compass Lexecon arrived at 2011 as a curious hybrid. Its American side had been stitched together from two firms: Lexecon, founded at the University of Chicago in 1977 by Richard Posner, William Landes and Andrew Rosenfield, and joined soon after by Daniel Fischel; and COMPASS, founded in 2003 by a group of Clinton-era and ex-DOJ economists including Robert Willig, Janusz Ordover, and Jonathan Orszag. FTI Consulting bought Lexecon in December 2003 for about $130 million in cash, bought COMPASS in 2006 for $72 million in cash and FTI stock, and welded the two into Compass Lexecon LLC in January 2008. Its European side was a different story. London held a handful of economists; Brussels, Madrid and Paris held nothing at all.

The LECG absorption fixed that overnight. More than forty European competition economists arrived in a single package, under a CEO-grade hire in Padilla. In a matter of weeks the EMEA practice went from subscale to elite.

Kirsten Edwards-Warren joined Compass Lexecon during this phase. She had spent the 2000s deep inside the UK regulatory machine, first as Director of Economics at the Office of Fair Trading and then at the Competition Commission; over the following decade she would become a senior figure in the London practice, formally co-head of EMEA from February 2024, and would build what would later become the London base of Econic Partners. Her hire set the pattern for the era: a senior regulator walks out, a senior firm absorbs the credibility, the clients follow on the strength of a single name.

By 2015 Compass Lexecon sat in the top tier of Global Competition Review every year without interruption. Its European footprint ran through London, Brussels, Madrid, and Paris; its client roster spanned the largest merger files on the continent. The firm that had barely existed in Europe in 2010 was now the dominant competition economics practice in the region. Four years.

The PE arrival: Capital Economics, October 2014

In October 2014 LDC, the private equity arm of Lloyds Banking Group, took a minority stake in Capital Economics at a reported valuation of roughly £70 million. The firm at that point was generating £15 to £18 million of revenue; at roughly four times turnover the deal priced it well above any prior benchmark for a UK economics research business. The number was unprecedented. The number stuck.

This was the first private equity deal in a UK pure-play economics research firm. PE money had already entered the broader advisory sector — Hellman & Friedman had bought into AlixPartners in 2006, and CVC Capital Partners had taken AlixPartners from H&F in 2012 — but those were restructuring platforms, not economics boutiques. LDC was the first house in London to look at a subscription-led macro research firm, run the numbers, and conclude the business was worth more than its own management had imagined.

The firm that caught PE's eye

The economics of the deal were defensible. Capital Economics had built a subscription book with high gross margins, low client churn, and a brand anchored on Bootle himself, who had won the £250,000 Wolfson Economics Prize in 2012 for the best plan for dealing with eurozone exit — a piece of publicity no marketing budget could have bought. The firm threw off cash; it carried no real capital intensity; its costs were largely rents and salaries. In private-equity terms it was exactly the sort of business that ought to have been picked off years earlier.

LDC's thesis was simple. Fund international expansion, build out the digital subscription product, grow revenue faster than costs, exit in three to five years at a higher multiple. The plan worked. Revenue grew thirty percent in the years after the deal, and by 2017 the firm was generating £22.5 million. In March 2018 Phoenix Equity Partners would arrive for the full buyout at £95 million. But that comes later.

The LDC deal mattered for three reasons. It set a public price benchmark for a UK economics firm; it told every other PE house in London that the sector was investable; and it told the founders of the mid-tier consultancies that the businesses they were running were worth more than they themselves had thought. None of the later deals — Phoenix into Capital Economics in 2018, Growth Capital Partners into Flint in 2021, Cinven's full buyout of Flint in 2025 — would have happened in quite the same shape without LDC going first.

The second tier forms: 2013 to 2015

Three firms founded in this window define the second tier of the modern market. Each was built around a specific post-crisis niche that the incumbents could not serve, or did not want to.

Fideres Partners (founded 2009)

Financial litigation specialist
Alberto Thomas & Steffen Hennig
Both former structured products specialists at Royal Bank of Scotland
Thomas2005 to 2009: Head of Structured Products, RBS London. Led synthetic credit derivatives team.
HennigSenior RBS role after five years at Deutsche Bank. 21+ years in structured products and complex derivatives.
Founded2009, London, immediately post-crisis
NichePost-2008 financial litigation: LIBOR, FX fixing, ISDAFIX, cross-holdings

Fideres was built exclusively around post-2008 financial litigation. Thomas and Hennig knew structured products from the inside, which was a rare profile for an expert witness firm; most expert witnesses had spent their careers in academia or the regulators. On 2 July 2014 Alberto Thomas gave evidence to the Treasury Select Committee on benchmark manipulation, speaking to the LIBOR rigging scandal that had shaken confidence in London's banking quarter. For a five-year-old firm, that was a credibility event.

The strategic point is that Fideres existed because 2008 had happened. It could not have been founded in 2005. The business model required a decade of bank misconduct to create a case pipeline, a class-action culture to create a paying client base, and regulators willing to fine the industry to create a damages framework. All three arrived together between 2009 and 2014.

Fingleton (founded 2013)

Regulatory strategy for the C-suite
John Fingleton CBE
CEO of the Office of Fair Trading, 2005 to 2012
Prior roleSeven years leading the UK's primary competition and consumer protection authority
Before thatCEO of the Irish Competition Authority
Founded2013, immediately after OFT departure, months before the CMA merger
ThesisCEOs cannot make their commercial case inside regulatory investigations. Fingleton helps them.

Fingleton's founding rationale was specific, and it was drawn from the bench. In his seven years running the OFT he had watched corporate defendants arrive at hearings with excellent legal counsel and excellent competition economists but no-one able to translate the underlying commercial logic of the business into terms a regulator would accept. The lawyers spoke law; the economists spoke models; the CEO sat silent. Fingleton built a firm to sit in that gap. The pitch to clients was that regulatory strategy is a C-suite problem, not a legal one, and that a former regulator would read the room in a way that no magic circle partner quite could. The argument found its market.

Flint Global (founded 2015)

Ed Richards, Sir Simon Fraser, Nigel Gardner

RichardsCEO of Ofcom for eight years; earlier Downing Street policy adviser under Gordon Brown

Flint defined a new category: political-economic consultancy. Not public affairs, not classical economics, not management consulting, but a hybrid practice advising multinational corporates and sovereign investors on the intersection of government, regulation, and markets. The founding trio was deliberately non-partisan — Fraser had served across Labour and Conservative administrations as a civil servant, and Richards, though formerly a Number 10 policy adviser under Tony Blair, sold the firm on its independence rather than any party loyalty.

The timing was not accidental. The telecoms sector was bracing for another Ofcom strategic review; the post-crisis regulatory settlement was still being bedded down; and a political reshuffle was beginning to release senior operators back into the market — a labour supply that Part 4 traces in detail once the referendum of 2016 sharpens it. Corporates wanted senior advisers who understood how Whitehall actually worked. Flint's founders, between them, had run it.

The partnership consolidation

While the specialists were being stitched together at the edges, the mid-tier partnership firms spent the years from 2010 to 2015 consolidating what they already had. This is the quiet part of the story. It is also the most important for understanding what the sector looks like today.

Baringa Partners had spun out of The Structure Group in 2002 and had spent the intervening years building a direct book of UK energy and financial services clients. In October 2014 Adrian Bettridge, who had joined the firm in 2007, took over as Managing Partner from co-founder Mohamed Mansour. He inherited a business of £78.3 million revenue and thirty LLP members at year-end 31 March 2014 — by FY25 both figures had risen roughly sixfold (£450m revenue, 178 members). His tenure would become the fastest growth phase in Baringa's history, but the structural decision to stay partner-owned was already locked in by the time he took the chair. No external capital, no sale process, no private-equity conversations. The partnership elected to keep everything it earned.

Frontier Economics continued its employee-owned model unchanged. Founded in June 1999 by Gaysford, Burns, Elliott, Biro, and Webb, Frontier had been employee-owned from its first trading day; by 2015 the firm had opened eight European offices and was closing on 400 staff. No PE deal, no sale, no partnership restructuring. The founders had written employee ownership into the founding documents precisely to prevent any of that from happening.

RBB Economics kept its partnership tight. Approximately eighteen to twenty members across the full window, with Simon Bishop as managing partner. RBB's model was the inverse of LECG's — stay small, keep the partnership senior, turn away work rather than hire to meet it. The LECG collapse vindicated the model. By 2015 RBB had 280 combined partners and staff across eight offices, yet the inner partnership remained deliberately capped.

Oxera made the most significant structural move of the period. In April 2014 the Oxford-based firm converted its main trading entity to a Limited Liability Partnership; Oxera Consulting LLP was incorporated at Companies House on 3 April 2014 (OC392464). The legacy limited companies — Oxford Economic Research Associates Ltd, Oxera Holdings Ltd, Oxera Services Ltd — became dormant and were formally dissolved in May 2023. The conversion was a legal restructuring rather than a commercial event, but it mattered. It gave partners direct equity in the profits rather than shareholder claims on a corporate parent, and it brought Oxera into line with the dominant structure across the UK mid-tier.

The LLP pattern: RBB, Baringa, Oxera, and most of the UK mid-tier settled on the LLP structure during this period. The model rewards senior partners directly, removes the corporate tax wedge, and makes external acquisition structurally harder. It also caps the firm's ability to raise growth capital. Every structural decision has a trade-off, and the firms that chose LLP were choosing stability and partner economics over scale.

The 2015 snapshot

By the end of 2015 the UK economics consultancy market had sorted itself into a stable shape. Four years had passed since the LECG collapse; three since Fingleton had hung up his shingle; one since the LDC deal and the Oxera LLP conversion. The dust had settled. This is what the market looked like.

Two features of this snapshot deserve attention. First, the market is genuinely stable. Nothing in the table is in visible distress; NERA UK was smaller than it had been in its 1990s heyday but still operating, and every other line was flat to growing. Second, the market was almost entirely UK or US owned. No Continental European firm had anything you could call a London presence; no Chinese, Japanese, or Middle Eastern money had entered the sector at all. The only foreign ownership was American, and it ran through the subsidiaries.

The alumni start leaving (but not yet)

The alumni family tree that would define the second wave was not yet in motion. 2015 was a quiet year for spinouts. Mark Pragnell was still Managing Director of Cebr and had not yet moved to Capital Economics, let alone founded Pragmatix Advisory. The Compass Lexecon departures that would eventually create Econic Partners lay a decade in the future. The major RBB spinouts had not happened. The Oxera alumni were still inside Oxera.

What was building under the surface, quietly, was a set of senior people who had been in place for a decade or more and were starting to look at the numbers. The LDC deal had shown them that their firms might be worth four times revenue to the right buyer; the Compass Lexecon story had shown that forty economists could walk across the street and reassemble a practice inside a new brand; the Fingleton and Flint foundings had shown that a single senior regulator with the right address book could start a credible firm from scratch. None of these lessons had been available in 2010. By 2015, everyone in the senior ranks knew them.

The dominoes were set. They would start falling in 2017.

What 2010 to 2015 changed

  1. The LECG collapse scattered forty-plus European competition economists through three successor firms and installed Compass Lexecon, overnight, as the dominant London practice.
  2. David Teece founded Berkeley Research Group as an LECG successor, quietly carrying the old firm's DNA into a new brand that would grow steadily through the 2020s.
  3. LDC's £70 million valuation of Capital Economics proved the sector was investable and set the price benchmark that every later private-equity deal would reference.
  4. Fideres, Fingleton, and Flint carved out the specialist niches — financial litigation, regulatory strategy, political-economic advisory — that the incumbents could not or would not serve.
  5. Oxera converted to LLP; Baringa, Frontier, and RBB held the partner-owned line. The structural choices that locked in the mid-tier for the next decade were all taken inside this five-year window.
Coming in Part 4

2015 to 2020: The Global Arms Race

In 2015 the UK economics consultancy market was profitable, growing, and stable. Three things were about to happen in the next five years that would transform it.

First, an American invasion: Cornerstone Research had already arrived in 2014, Analysis Group would open in London in 2017, and BRG would keep on growing. Second, a private equity takeover: Phoenix Equity Partners would do the full buyout of Capital Economics in 2018, valuing it at £95 million. Third, a Brexit-fuelled boom in regulatory and political-economic consulting that would carry Flint, Fingleton, and the next wave of specialist firms into the tier above.
