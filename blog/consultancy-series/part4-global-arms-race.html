The Global Arms Race: 2015-2020

Part 4 of 7: the Americans cross the Atlantic; private equity writes its first cheque into UK economics research; Brexit throws off a regulatory advisory boom; and one firm — among those that publicly file detailed accounts — quietly posts the highest profit-per-partner in British professional services.

Leath · 10 April 2026 · 16 min read

The Invasion

For fifteen years the American economics firms watched London from a polite distance. They filed appearances at the Department of Justice and the Federal Trade Commission; they testified in Delaware; they billed corporate clients out of Manhattan and the Menlo Park suburbs. The European merger work, the Competition Appeal Tribunal work, the Ofcom and Ofgem work — all of that they left to the boutiques that Simon Bishop, Gunnar Niels, Peter Davis, and Philip Lowe had stitched together in the decade after 2002. The Atlantic, for once, felt wider than the flight time. Then it didn’t.

In 2014 Cornerstone Research opened a London office. The firm had been founded in Menlo Park in 1989 and had served US courts for twenty-five years without setting foot in Britain. But the cartel damages work was now spilling out of Luxembourg judgments, the Brussels follow-on claims were arriving in the English High Court, and the economics of that litigation paid at the rates only Americans had been paying for. Cornerstone arrived to collect.

Analysis Group followed three years later. The firm had been founded in Boston in 1981 by Bruce Stangle and Michael F. Koehn, both refugees from Arthur D. Little, and had grown into one of the most profitable consulting shops in the United States; by the mid-2010s it billed out of New York, Chicago, Los Angeles, Washington, Denver, Menlo Park, San Francisco, Montreal, Beijing, Paris, and Brussels. In February 2017 — thirty-six years after its founding — it finally opened London. The office began with a small team and a mandate to build a European litigation-economics bench from scratch.

Meanwhile Berkeley Research Group kept absorbing the European fragments of LECG. BRG had bought what it could from LECG’s 2011 collapse, kept the ex-LECG economists who wanted to stay in advisory work, and rebuilt them as a pan-European competition practice across London, Brussels, and Dusseldorf. By 2018 the global BRG group was billing into nine figures in dollar revenue, though Berkeley Research Group (UK) Limited (07245710) reported more modest UK turnover in its filed accounts. It was also losing money on the UK entity — a detail worth keeping in mind.

The traffic ran both ways. Frontier Economics added Dublin, Madrid, Berlin, and Brussels offices across the decade; Oxera grew its Brussels and Amsterdam benches; RBB Economics opened Johannesburg, Melbourne, Stockholm, and The Hague. By the end of 2020 none of the major UK firms operated only in the UK, and none of the major US firms operated without a London team. The transatlantic market had fused into a single talent pool.

The Phoenix Deal

On 27 March 2018, Phoenix Equity Partners announced a controlling stake in Capital Economics. The headline was £95 million; the revenue of the business that year was around £22.5 million; the implied multiple, therefore, was roughly 4.2 times revenue — a rich figure for a research subscription shop, though not an absurd one given the recurring income and the modest capex. For the UK economics sector it was a watershed. No founder-owned research house had ever commanded that kind of cheque.

LDC had held a minority stake since 2014 and now exited in full. Roger Bootle kept a significant piece of the equity and stayed on as Chairman; Neil Shearing stepped up to Group Chief Economist, confirming the succession Bootle had been preparing for five years. The deal did two things nobody in the sector had seen before. It put private equity into the equity stack of a UK economics firm at scale. And it put bond-market leverage onto the balance sheet.

The leverage piece is visible in Companies House. From 2018 onwards the Capital Economics filings show charges registered to Nordic Trustee, the Oslo-based bond agent used for Nordic high-yield issuance. That is not a structure a founder-owned research shop ever uses; it is the structure a private equity sponsor uses when it wants to gear a subscription business with bond debt rather than bank loans. The trail of Nordic Trustee charges across the Capital Economics group entities is a direct fingerprint of the Phoenix ownership period — the kind of detail that does not appear in a press release but sits on the public register for anyone who knows where to look.

It mattered for what came next. A PE-owned Capital Economics had to deliver returns on a timetable; a founder-owned Capital Economics had not. Pressure on margin, pressure on cross-sell, pressure on international subscription growth — all of it arrived in 2018, and all of it showed up in the accounts by 2020. Shearing took an operational role the founders had never needed.

Phoenix had form in this. Its earlier UK deals included Audiotonix, Lane Clark & Peacock’s consulting arm, and a string of mid-market financial services businesses. Capital Economics fitted the thesis: recurring revenue, asset-light, institutional client base, room to grow internationally under new management. It was the thesis that would be applied to the rest of the sector over the following five years.

The Brexit Effect

The referendum closed on 23 June 2016. By the morning of the 24th, the regulatory uncertainty that would drive economics advisory for the next five years was already priced in. Every major merger, every state aid question, every competition case, every financial services passport, every tariff line, every environmental standard, every procurement rule that touched the European Union now had to be mapped onto a political process nobody had a template for. The firms that built the templates got the work.

Four political and economic advisory shops, founded in the narrow window between 2014 and 2016, caught the wave directly. None were traditional economics consultancies. All sold senior government experience married to quantitative research; all grew their teams and client rosters materially between 2016 and 2020; none files revenue separately in a form that permits independent verification.

The Leave-Remain bridge firm

The dates tell the story. Stonehaven 2014; Flint 2015; Public First and Hanbury 2016. Every one launched as David Cameron’s operation was ending or just after it had ended. Every one drew its founding talent from Downing Street, the Foreign Office, the regulators, and the campaign shops. The timing was not coincidence — it was a labour-market reshuffle.

Flint is the cleanest example of the revolving door in action. Companies House filings show that Ed Richards (Ofcom CEO 2006–14, previously senior policy adviser to Tony Blair) and Sir Simon Fraser (Permanent Secretary at the Foreign Office 2010–15) co-founded Flint Global Ltd in September 2015 — within months of each leaving their government posts. In November 2024, James Purnell, former Secretary of State for Work and Pensions (2008–09) and later BBC Director of Strategy, joined as a director. Three former holders of some of the most senior positions in British public life, all on the board of a single advisory firm. The Cameron era threw off a decade of senior public-sector operators who now had to earn a living in the private market; Brexit handed them the clients to do it.

The traditional economics firms felt the referendum differently. The Competition and Markets Authority took on workload the European Commission used to absorb. Merger cases that would have gone to Brussels stayed in London. State aid work reinvented itself as subsidy control. The CMA began building a bigger panel of external economists, a bigger case team, and a bigger appetite for market studies, and RBB, Frontier, Oxera, CRA, and Compass Lexecon all hired into the expanded CMA practice. The UK competition economics market, which had looked uncomfortably dependent on EU referrals, suddenly found itself with a domestic regulator that needed significantly more external help than it had before. The referendum, intended to shrink Britain’s regulatory surface area, had enlarged it.

RBB’s Breakout

RBB Economics had spent the 2000s and early 2010s doing exactly one thing very well. The firm — built out of NERA’s London office in April 2002 by Simon Bishop, Derek Ridyard, and Simon Baker, in the manner Part 2 narrates at length — focused on competition economics, kept its partnership small, refused to diversify into regulation or policy, and let word-of-mouth among competition lawyers do the business development. By 2015 the pattern was fifteen years old and looked like a plateau. Then the numbers broke.

Between 2018 and 2020 RBB’s revenue climbed above £60 million on a member count that stayed in the teens. Operating margin held north of 60 per cent; the partner count did not change materially; what changed was the average bill per case, the mix of pan-European cartel damages work, and the firm’s grip on the most profitable end of UK and EU merger review. The current managing partner, who had joined from the Office of Fair Trading in 2002, took over operational leadership. Simon Bishop remained a member through to his retirement in April 2023.

Profit per member at RBB by 2020

The comparison is instructive. A magic circle law firm partner in 2020 earned between £1.8 million and £2.1 million in profit per equity partner; RBB’s numbers matched or beat that on a headcount fifteen times smaller than any of the law firms. A typical Big Four management consulting partnership earned £600,000 to £900,000. RBB was running at three times that — quietly, and without the marketing department to say so.

There was no trade press coverage of RBB’s margins. The firm did not publish league tables; it did not give interviews; it filed its LLP accounts at Companies House with the statutory minimum of disclosure. The profit numbers are visible in those filings if you know where to look, but no analyst or journalist appears to have extracted them before this series.

The quiet part: by 2020 RBB was on a profit-per-member number that put it ahead of Slaughter and May, Linklaters, and Freshfields on a like-for-like basis. It had fewer than twenty members. It had no back office the clients could see. It had three UK partners running the practice. Among UK professional-services partnerships that publicly file detailed accounts, it was the most profitable people business we can document — and the sector press treated it as a competition boutique.

The Emerging Ecosystem

Beneath the established firms a new layer arrived. Most of the launches happened in the 2014–2016 window, and none of them came from the traditional RBB-Frontier-Oxera-Compass alumni pipeline; they came from Treasury, from Which?, from Downing Street, from HSBC macro research, and from the structured-products desks at the investment banks. The pedigree had shifted, and with it the shape of the work.

Fideres deserves a separate line. Alberto Thomas had appeared as a Treasury Select Committee witness on benchmark manipulation in July 2014 — one of the very few external economists invited to give oral evidence in the LIBOR aftermath — and the firm took that positioning and built on it. By the late 2010s Fideres was billing into follow-on damages cases across Europe and the US, had a litigation-funder client base that no traditional competition economics firm had targeted, and had grown to a headcount comparable with Frontier’s early years. Fideres operated as a financial economics litigation engine rather than a competition boutique, and it occupied a segment — litigation-funded damages claims — that the established firms had simply never gone near. That was the trick. Build the franchise where nobody else was looking.

Quiet Scaling: Frontier and Oxera

Frontier Economics entered the decade as an employee-owned partnership; it exited the decade the same way. No PE money, no bond debt, no holding company restructure, no US acquirer. Between 2015 and 2020 the firm added revenue year on year, opened offices in Madrid and Berlin, and closed 2020 at around £61 million of UK revenue. The partner count grew, the profit share stayed within the partnership, and the firm kept doing the same mix of regulation, competition, and policy work it had done since Simon Gaysford, Philip Burns, Dan Elliott, Zoltan Biro and Michael Webb founded it out of London Economics and the Bank of England in 1999 — with Sarah Hogg chairing the board, and the equity given away on day one. It was, in a sector that now wore private capital like a badge, an almost quaintly old-fashioned way of compounding.

Oxera took a parallel path. In 2014 the firm converted to LLP structure and Gunnar Niels took the managing partner role. Through the second half of the decade Oxera was the Brexit winner inside the UK sector. Its Dutch and Belgian offices kept it in the European market; its Oxford and London offices absorbed the new CMA workload; its financial services practice, built over a decade, caught the wave of post-Brexit regulatory reconfiguration that the FCA and the Bank of England were now commissioning. Revenue climbed towards £35 million by 2020.

Neither firm had a dramatic year. Neither took outside capital. Neither hit the trade press. Both compounded through Brexit without drama while other firms restructured, sold, or chased the PE money. Between them, they were the sector’s most consistent scaling stories of the decade.

The Vivid Economics Story Begins

Vivid Economics was founded in 2006 by Robin Smale and Professor Cameron Hepburn. Hepburn held an Oxford economics chair; Smale came out of the climate and energy policy side of government; and the founding thesis was straightforward enough — the gap between the traditional economic consultancies and the management consultancies wanted a climate specialist to fill it. For a decade Vivid was a small player, respected in climate circles, with limited visibility outside them.

Between 2015 and 2020 that changed. The firm grew to roughly 130 staff across London, Amsterdam, and the United States; its client base expanded from UK and EU government departments to development finance institutions, multilaterals, central banks, and a growing roster of corporate clients that now treated climate risk as a board-level question rather than a sustainability-report line item. The Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures, the Paris Agreement follow-through, and the arrival of climate scenario stress testing all pushed work onto Vivid’s bench faster than it could hire.

McKinsey noticed. The firm had been building a climate practice organically and hitting the familiar generalist problem: climate modelling is a specialist discipline, and the junior analysts McKinsey hired could not replicate an Oxford climate economist in six months. In January 2021 McKinsey launched McKinsey Sustainability as a dedicated practice; two months later it announced the acquisition of Vivid Economics and Planetrics, the climate analytics software suite. The press release framed it as a strategic addition. The headcount math framed it as an acqui-hire — McKinsey was buying 130 specialist climate consultants in one transaction because organic hiring could not deliver them fast enough.

Cameron Hepburn retained his Oxford chair and moved to a senior adviser role. The integration into McKinsey began in March 2021. We will return to it in Part 5, because it did not end the way either side expected.

The End of 2020 Snapshot

By the close of 2020 the UK economics consulting sector looked more defined than it had ever been. The US firms were all operational in London; the PE money had found its first target; Brexit had enlarged the domestic regulatory demand base; a new layer of political-economic advisory firms had scaled into eight-figure revenue; and the traditional competition boutiques were posting margins the magic circle law firms were beginning to notice. Five years earlier none of that had been obvious. Five years later it would look inevitable.

Key Takeaways

  1. Between 2014 and 2017 every major US economics firm opened or scaled a London office; the transatlantic market fused into a single talent pool.
  2. In March 2018 Phoenix Equity Partners bought Capital Economics at roughly 4.2 times revenue — the first full PE buyout in UK economics research — and the Nordic Trustee charges at Companies House mark the bond structure sitting underneath the equity.
  3. Brexit threw off a political-economic advisory boom: Stonehaven (2014), Flint (2015), Public First (2016), and Hanbury (2016) all launched into the window, staffed from Downing Street, the Foreign Office, the regulators, and the campaign shops.
  4. RBB Economics broke through £60m revenue on fewer than twenty members, posting profit per member north of £2m and becoming — among UK professional-services partnerships that publicly file detailed accounts — the most profitable people business we can document, without anyone outside the sector noticing.
  5. Frontier and Oxera compounded quietly through the same period without outside capital, each in its own unglamorous way.
  6. Vivid Economics grew into a 130-person climate specialist and was acquired by McKinsey in March 2021 as the anchor of the new McKinsey Sustainability practice.

Part 5: The Profit Machine, 2020-2025

As 2020 closed, UK economics consulting looked stable. Over the next five years, RBB’s revenue would almost double to £108m on the same partnership count; McKinsey would acquire and then dissolve Vivid Economics; BRG would turn profitable after years of losses; Brattle would go loss-making in the UK; a Goldman Sachs-backed spinout called Econic Partners would poach 25+ economists from Compass Lexecon; and the UK market would exceed £2 billion in combined revenue.
