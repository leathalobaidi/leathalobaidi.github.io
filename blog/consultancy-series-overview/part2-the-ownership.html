Who Actually Owns These Firms?

The Vivid Economics acqui-hire

McKinsey bought Vivid Economics in November 2021. The press release called it a flagship addition to McKinsey Sustainability, positioning Vivid as global leaders in green economics and climate finance.

Twenty-one months later, in August 2023, Vivid Economics Limited entered Members Voluntary Liquidation. PricewaterhouseCoopers took on the wind-up. Steven Sherry and Jen Whatcott signed as the appointed liquidators. The company is due to be fully dissolved on 27 May 2026.

This was never a business purchase. McKinsey bought the team. Cameron Hepburn, Robert Ritz and the rest of the Vivid economists moved into McKinsey Sustainability. The Planetrics climate transition risk tool became part of McKinsey's in-house analytics stack. The client relationships transferred to McKinsey Advisory Limited. What remained of Vivid Economics Limited was a legal shell with 85 staff already onboarded elsewhere and a balance sheet of £2.9m in net assets.

Companies House tells the story step by step. The last accounts filing covers the year to 30 June 2020, back when Vivid was still independent. No accounts were filed for FY2021 or FY2022 because, by the time those would have been due, the team was already inside McKinsey. On 17 November 2021, the day the deal closed, Vivid filed a change of accounting reference date extending the year-end from June to December. That is the classic pre-liquidation tidy-up: align the stub period, run off any residual contracts, settle tax, then appoint liquidators. Twenty-one months later PwC arrived.

Acqui-hire mechanics. Vivid never disclosed turnover in audited form. It filed under the small company exemption for its entire 15-year life. McKinsey did not buy the revenue. McKinsey bought 85 climate economists and the Planetrics software. The standalone UK legal entity was surplus to requirements the day the deal signed. The 21 months between acquisition and MVL is the time it takes to transfer contracts, settle tax, extract IP, and pay off PwC's final invoice.

The ownership audit produced a messier picture than expected. Some of the UK's best-known economics consultancies are owned by people most of their own employees have never met. Others are owned by charitable trusts. One is owned by a husband-and-wife team operating through a holding company. A handful are owned by Americans who founded the firms four decades ago and have never let go.

Three ways to own an economics consultancy

Every firm in the database falls into one of three archetypes. The boundaries are clean because Companies House forces them to be: a firm is either independent, a subsidiary, or a partner-owned LLP. There is no fourth category.

The remaining firms in the database are either small-company filers with incomplete PSC records, dormant shells retained for brand reasons, or UK establishments of overseas entities that disclose ownership abroad. The three archetypes cover the working firms.

Each archetype creates different incentives. Independents hoard profit. Subsidiaries extract management fees. LLPs distribute everything to members each year and start again from zero. The legal structure shapes who gets hired, how much gets paid, and what the firm is willing to risk.

Walker founded Oxford Economics as a commercial spin-off from the university in 1981 and held the controlling stake for forty-four years until his unexpected death in March 2026. The group produces £74m of revenue at a razor-thin 2% margin. That low margin is not a sign of distress. Most of the value flows to the 400-plus economists on the payroll through salaries. The structure looks almost identical to Frontier's: a cash-generative research business run for the benefit of employees, with the equity held by one person in the background. The post-death PSC transition has not yet appeared on the Companies House register as of April 2026.

Adrian Cooper ceased to be a PSC in 2019 when the group was restructured. That restructuring consolidated Walker's holding through Oxford Economics Group Limited, the new top company. Walker was a British national resident in the UAE. At the time of his death the equity sat with him personally; the generational transition was already in motion, Innes McFee had been appointed Chief Executive from 4 December 2025, Walker's son Neil Walker had been appointed Deputy CEO and joined the Board, and Adrian Cooper had moved to Executive Chairman in the same announcement, but the ultimate ownership question will not be settled on the public register until the PSC filings catch up.

The private equity layer

Two firms show what a modern PE buyout looks like on the Companies House register. Both were bought inside the last eight years. Both sit inside multi-tier holding structures designed to park debt and capture carried interest. Neither is a secret, but neither is widely understood.

Flint Global and the Spark chain

Flint Global is one of the most profitable firms in the entire dataset. It generates £31m in revenue at a 30% margin. The firm was founded by former civil servants and built a niche in regulatory and geopolitical advisory work. In November 2021 Growth Capital Partners took a minority stake, putting the Project Spark holding chain in place; four years later, in December 2025, Cinven completed the full buyout at an enterprise value of £190 million. The ownership chain looks like this.

Bidco, Midco, Topco. The naming convention is standard PE architecture. Bidco holds the operating asset and the senior debt. Midco holds the mezzanine or PIK layer. Topco holds the equity and sits at the sponsor's registered address. All three Spark entities were incorporated in November 2021, within days of each other, and all three have their registered office at 38 Jermyn Street, Princes House. That address is a classic London PE service address.

Flint generates roughly £9.3m of operating profit a year. The Bidco-Midco-Topco stack distributes that profit between the senior lender, the sponsor, and the management equity rollover. Every person paid by Flint at the operating level is working to service a financing structure that sits two levels above them.

Capital Economics and the bond issuance

Capital Economics has a different story. Roger Bootle founded it in 1999 as an independent macro research shop. Phoenix Equity Partners bought it in a 2018 management buyout. Revenue now sits at £22m with a 20% margin, driven almost entirely by subscriptions to its macro research product.

The charge register is the notable filing, not the PE ownership itself. Capital Economics has filed charges in favour of Ocorian Trustee (UK) Limited, trading as Nordic Trustee. Nordic Trustee acts as security agent on bond issuances, which means Capital Economics, a £22m macro research firm, has issued corporate bonds to institutional investors. That is unusual for a firm of its size. The sister entity Capital Economics Research Ltd (09242255) sits alongside the main trading company as part of the group's debt-bearing structure.

A yield asset, not a growth asset. A macro research business with a subscription revenue model attracts credit investors because the revenue is recurring and the cash conversion is high. The bond issuance shows Phoenix running Capital Economics for yield. That is a different operating philosophy from Flint.

The UK entity now sits inside a company that also builds chemical plants and operates NASA ground systems. That is a very long way from willingness-to-pay for birdsong.

The trusts and the charities

Three firms in the sample have ownership structures that fit none of the preceding categories. They are worth flagging because they reveal what the founders wanted to preserve.

Cambridge Econometrics is the outlier. The Cambridge Trust for New Thinking, a registered charity, holds a partial stake, and founder Terence Snarr Barker retains a direct holding alongside it. This is as close as the UK economics consultancy market gets to the Scott Trust model that owns The Guardian. BiGGAR, based in South Queensferry, chose the employee ownership trust route instead, which has become popular with Scottish professional services firms as a way to transition founder equity to the next generation without triggering a capital gain.

The partner-owned LLPs

Twelve firms in the database are Limited Liability Partnerships with no single controlling party. Every one of them files a PSC statement saying the same thing: no individual with significant control. The members own the firm collectively, and no member holds enough to qualify as a PSC under the 25% threshold.

These are the purest form of economics consultancy from an ownership standpoint. Nobody outside the partnership has any claim on the profits. Nobody inside the partnership can sell their stake to a third party without the agreement of the other members. The cap table is whatever the members' agreement says it is, and no one outside the firm gets to see it.

RBB Economics LLP is the standout. Eighteen members splitting £61m of distributable profit works out to an average of more than £3.3m per member before tax. A professional services partnership at the top of its game, and by design, invisible to anyone who is not one of those 18 people.

Note on Independent Economics LLP. This is the firm previously known as Llewellyn Consulting. John Llewellyn, the former OECD chief economist, restructured and rebranded the partnership in 2022. The old name still appears in older databases.

Three archetypes, very different concentrations. Subsidiaries of foreign parents dominate by revenue, accounting for more than half of the sector's disclosed turnover despite being only a quarter of the firm count. LLPs contribute nearly a third of revenue from just twelve firms, because the LLPs skew enormous: Baringa and AlixPartners alone clear £700m. Independents are numerous but individually smaller. The median independent files revenue under £5m.

What the ownership audit shows

  1. McKinsey already wound up Vivid Economics. An acqui-hire. McKinsey bought 85 climate economists and the Planetrics software. The UK legal shell entered members voluntary liquidation 21 months later. The Vivid name will be gone from Companies House by May 2026.
  2. Founder-ownership is alive at the top. Oxford Economics, CEPA and Public First all still have their founders or founders' families as ultimate beneficial owners, some more than 40 years in. (AlixPartners' Jay Alix retains a minority interest but the firm has been PE-controlled since 2017.)
  3. PE has arrived, quietly. Flint Global and Capital Economics both sit inside multi-tier Bidco-Midco-Topco structures. One is a growth asset, one is a yield asset.
  4. The sector is half-American at the parent level. Eight of the eleven foreign-owned firms report up to a US company, and four of those are listed on NYSE or NASDAQ.
  5. LLPs are still the profit machines. RBB's 18 members split £61m. Eighteen people, no PSC, no public filings of cap table. This is the structure that wins in this market.
