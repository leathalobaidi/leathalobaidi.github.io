The Money: Where Economics Consultancy Actually Prints Cash

Debt facilities, growth trajectories, top earner pay, and the structural reasons why one firm generates £3.3m per partner while another runs at 1.4% margin.

Leath · 10 April 2026 · 16 min read

The RBB Partner Machine

Eighteen people share £60 million of profit. That works out at £3.34m each. One of them — the accounts do not name the recipient — took home £5.77m in the year to March 2025. This is the highest disclosed profit-per-partner figure in the roughly ninety-firm dataset. No other UK economics consultancy comes close.

RBB Economics LLP runs no global brand and files no press releases. Its 199 Bishopsgate office serves competition authorities and the corporate lawyers who need rebuttal evidence against them. Each year the gap between RBB and the rest of the industry widens.

Two Models of Scaling

The Compressor

RBB Economics
  • Revenue +79%
  • Partners 20 to 18
  • Staff 131 to 269 (+105%)
  • Profit per partner +65%
  • Grow the team, shrink the club

The Distributor

Baringa Partners
  • Revenue +123%
  • Partners 98 to 178 (+82%)
  • Staff 813 to 1,820 (+124%)
  • Profit per partner flat
  • Grow everything proportionally

Both strategies produce a functioning business. The difference is who captures the upside. At Baringa, a new partner dilutes no one because the business grows at the same pace as the partnership. At RBB, a new partner costs every existing one £3m per year in foregone profit. That explains the promotion rate.

If RBB admitted two partners tomorrow at the current revenue run-rate, the per-partner draw would fall from £3.34m to about £3.00m. Each existing partner would lose £340,000 a year. The accounts make the incentive to keep the door closed obvious.

HSBC is the bank of UK economics consulting

Baringa, Frontier, Oxera, Fideres and Cebr all bank with HSBC UK Bank PLC: five of the largest names in the sector on the same relationship manager's book. One bank holds the working capital facilities for most of the British-owned side of the industry.

The US-owned firms break the pattern. BRG uses PNC Bank, an American lender with a minor UK footprint. Brattle routes its charges through its US parent. AlixPartners and FTI follow the same structure. The line between British banking relationships and American ones runs directly along the line between independent firms and subsidiaries.

RBB uses neither HSBC nor an American lender. It uses RBS Invoice Finance for receivables and holds charges in favour of Zurich Assurance. The Zurich filings relate to insurance-linked security, not standard lending. RBB funds itself differently because its cash position lets it.

Capital Economics has bond debt

One firm in the dataset carries public debt. Capital Economics Ltd has two outstanding charges in favour of "Ocorian Trustee (UK) Limited trading as Nordic Trustee." Nordic Trustee is the leading bond trustee for Scandinavian high-yield issues. A bond trustee rather than a bank lender means Capital Economics issued debt securities to investors, not a credit line. That structure appears in PE-backed companies after a recapitalisation, and Capital Economics fits that profile. No other economics consultancy in Britain has touched the bond market.

The A&M top earner takes home more than £11.8m: more than the CEO of Whitbread, Pearson, or ITV. One senior partner at a firm most British graduates have never encountered earns more than the executives running listed companies in the index.

Top-earner pay as a share of firm operating profit clusters in a tight band. At every large firm that discloses, between 7% and 11% of profit goes to one person. The most productive rainmaker at a professional services firm consistently commands close to a tenth of the surplus.

Note on the Baringa figure. Baringa's accounts disclose that the highest-paid member received 7% of "profit for the financial year available for discretionary division among members". The £11.3m figure above is that 7% applied to Baringa's FY25 discretionary pool of £161.9m — a derived number, not a disclosed absolute. RBB's £5.77m, AlixPartners' £4.73m, Oxera's £2.09m and A&M Europe's €14.2m (£11.8m) are disclosed absolutes. Note on FX. The A&M Europe top-earner figure is converted at the LLP's stated balance-sheet-date rate of €1 = £0.8291 (per note 1.15 of the filed accounts). The aggregate revenue totals in Part 1 use the 2024 annual-average rate of €1 = £0.85; the small difference between the two rates (c.2%) does not materially affect the league-table ranking.

Why Margins Are So High

RBB at 56% and Oxera at 38% are not normal operating margins for a services business. Accenture runs at 15%. Deloitte's UK arm sits at similar levels. Economics consultancy lets the winners capture far more of what they bill.

A caveat on the headline gap between RBB and Oxera. Both are LLPs and both strike operating profit before any members’ remuneration charge, so the 56.3% versus 38.3% comparison is like-for-like and the eighteen-point gap is real. What differs is how each firm classifies what flows below the operating-profit line. RBB and Baringa both take £0 as a members’-remuneration expense — their full operating surplus flows through interest and corporation tax to the profit-available-for-discretionary-division line. Oxera alone among the three takes £8.3m of its £27m below the line as members’ remuneration, leaving £18.7m distributable after tax. On a distributable-profit-over-turnover basis the comparison bifurcates by P&L structure: RBB 55.6% and Baringa 36.0% (each essentially equal to its operating margin because there is no below-line rem charge), Oxera 26.5%. On that basis the RBB–Oxera gap widens from eighteen points to roughly twenty-nine, and the Baringa–Oxera gap widens from minus-two to plus-nine-and-a-half. RBB is the only firm in the dataset at the upper extreme on either basis. Note also that RBB’s FY20→FY21 accounting-policy change moved its own members’ remuneration from above to below the operating-profit line, so pre-FY21 RBB margins are not comparable with post-FY21 RBB margins (the standalone RBB piece covers this).

Four reasons appear consistently across the filings.

  1. Hourly rates. Senior competition economists bill in the range of £800 to £1,500 per hour (indicative; specific rates are not routinely published but surface in Competition Appeal Tribunal cost judgments and in public CMA framework-agreement procurement). Court-ready expert work reaches higher. A partner on a Phase II merger investigation will log 1,500 chargeable hours in a calendar year.
  2. Mandatory work. An EC Phase II merger investigation must hire an economist. A damages claim in the Competition Appeal Tribunal must hire an economist. The regulator requires the analysis. The client has no discretion over whether to pay for it.
  3. Utilisation. M&A cycles drive sustained high booking rates. When deal volumes rise, the entire senior bench runs full. When they fall, the litigation work picks up the slack because companies sue each other in downturns.
  4. Small partnerships. Deliberate scarcity creates the retention tool. A 10-year associate knows there are 18 partners and sees the £3m draw. The incentive to stay another two years is obvious. The cost of leaving is the entire upside.

Frontier Economics

Frontier is employee-owned. Its accounts show three consecutive years of roughly £1.4m in operating profit on nearly £100m of revenue. Frontier can generate margin; it routes value through salaries and bonuses instead of leaving it as profit. Frontier employs 460 staff against RBB's 269 for roughly the same revenue. The surplus goes to the people doing the work, not to a small group of equity partners.

Oxford Economics

The pattern repeats under a different ownership structure. Founder John Walker, who passed away in March 2026, controlled the company until his death and drew compensation through routes other than operating profit. The £1.5m of reported operating profit understates what the business produces because most of the surplus sat in the salary line and owner-manager remuneration.

A low operating margin at a UK economics firm raises one question first: who owns the equity and where do they want the cash to appear.

Brattle's UK Problem

Brattle Group runs a large, profitable global practice out of Boston and Washington. Its UK subsidiary tells a different story. The latest accounts show £39m of UK revenue and a £0.9m operating loss. Four charges at Companies House remain outstanding. The US parent is strong; the UK unit loses money.

A loss-making subsidiary inside a profitable global group points to one of three causes: transfer pricing that strips margin out of the UK, a hiring spree that has not yet matched revenue, or a genuine commercial problem in the London office. Brattle's UK headcount has held steady and the filings show no sign the losses are a one-off. A correction or wind-down is worth tracking.

What This Means for the Economist

A graduate with a distinction in econometrics has a real choice to make, and these numbers change how to make it.

  • The RBB path. One-in-eighteen shot at £3m or more per year. Ten years minimum to partner. Narrow subject matter. Small office. Highest ceiling in the market.
  • The Frontier path. Broad equity distribution across hundreds of staff. Secure salary. Wider subject matter. Lower ceiling, higher floor.
  • The Oxford Economics path. Salary model at scale. Global footprint. Macro and forecasting work. Career stability with no partnership lottery.
  • The Big Four path. Volume competition work at 10-15% margin. Clear promotion criteria. Brand recognition that opens doors elsewhere.
  • The boutique path. Spend ten years at one of the above, then start your own. Own 100% of something small instead of a sliver of something big.

The filings do not tell you which path to take. They tell you what each one pays.

Key Findings, Part 4

  1. RBB Economics generates £3.34m of profit per partner. No other UK economics consultancy comes close.
  2. Baringa grew revenue by 123% over five years and held profit per partner flat. RBB grew revenue by 79% and increased it by 65%. Different scaling philosophies produce different partner outcomes.
  3. HSBC banks most of the independent British side of the industry: Baringa, Frontier, Oxera, Fideres, Cebr. US-owned firms bank with US lenders.
  4. Capital Economics is the only firm in the dataset with bond debt, routed through Nordic Trustee.
  5. The top earner at A&M Europe took home £11.8m, more than most FTSE 250 CEOs.
  6. Frontier Economics and Oxford Economics run at sub-2% margin by choice, not necessity. Value flows to salaries.
  7. Brattle's UK arm loses money despite a strong global parent. Worth watching.

The Closing

RBB Economics: 23 years of case work, £5.77m to the highest-paid (unnamed) designated member. Eighteen partners share £60m of profit on £108m of revenue from a single London office, at a firm most people have never heard of. The gap between RBB and the rest of the industry grows each year. The filings at Companies House have said so all along.

Four parts, around ninety active firms (95 identified; five removed on review), 368 Companies House PDFs. The data sits in the public register. The people inside the industry already know the numbers.

Methodology

  • ~90 active firms tracked across the full UK economics consultancy market (95 identified; five removed as non-core or dormant)
  • 368 Companies House account PDFs downloaded and parsed
  • Five-year time series constructed for RBB, Baringa, Frontier and Oxera
  • Full enrichment via Companies House API: officers, PSC ownership, charges, insolvency, filing history
  • Database: SQLite with seven tables, full CSV exports available on request
  • All figures verified against audited statutory accounts
