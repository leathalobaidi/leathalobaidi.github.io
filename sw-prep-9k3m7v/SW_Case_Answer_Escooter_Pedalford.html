0The 30-second read

What the data actually says

  • Highly concentrated: three licensed firms, HHI ≈ 3,550 (well above the 2,000 "highly concentrated" threshold), with entry capped at 3 by statute.
  • High profitability: share-weighted margin ≈ 46%; ≈ £5.2m annual profit on ≈ £11.3m revenue. Prices sit far above marginal cost.
  • Inelastic market demand: +10% price → −5% rides ⇒ market elasticity ≈ −0.5. Consumers have few good alternatives; firms have pricing power.
  • Parallel conduct: near-identical prices, all moved within a 2-week window, no entry for 2 years, switching costs — classic coordination markers.
  • BUT: parallel pricing alone is not proof of unlawful coordination — it is equally consistent with oligopolistic interdependence (conscious parallelism). And the likely root cause is the statutory cap of three, i.e. a regulatory barrier, not necessarily firm misconduct.

Bottom line: a genuine competition concern with real consumer harm, but the evidence is not yet sufficient to prove coordination. Recommend (i) gather "plus-factor" evidence, (ii) review the licensing cap as the proportionate structural fix, (iii) low-cost switching/transparency remedies — and hold price control as a backstop, not a first move.

Demand elasticity

Market arc elasticity ε = %ΔQ ÷ %ΔP = (−5%) ÷ (+10%) = −0.5 → INELASTIC (|ε| < 1)

The single most important number in the case — and the cleverest point you can make

Market demand is inelastic (−0.5). Two consequences, and the second is the one that separates a strong candidate:

  1. Inelastic demand means consumers lack close substitutes → it confers pricing power and explains why a 10% rise stuck.
  2. A profit-maximising monopolist never prices on the inelastic part of the demand curve (cutting output would raise revenue and cut cost). So if these firms were perfectly coordinating as a joint monopolist, we would expect them to have pushed prices even higher until demand turned elastic. The fact that they sit at ε = −0.5 suggests either (a) coordination is imperfect/tacit rather than a full cartel, (b) −0.5 is a short-run elasticity and long-run is more elastic, or (c) they are restrained by the licence, entry threat or political risk. Either way, it tempers an over-confident "this is a cartel" conclusion.

Cross-check via the Lerner index on the per-minute margin: L = (0.22−0.10)/0.22 ≈ 0.55, implying each firm acts as if it faces a firm-level elasticity of 1/0.55 ≈ 1.8. Firm-level demand (≈1.8) being more elastic than market demand (0.5) is exactly what you expect under oligopoly with some substitution between apps — consistent with rivalry, not a textbook monopoly.

2Part 2 · The briefing note (what you'd actually submit)

Style: briefing note to your CA manager, to brief the Authority. ~2 pages. Bullets where they earn their place. Covers Sections A–E.

TO: Manager, Comparative Advantage  |  FROM: [Candidate]  |  RE: Pedalford e-scooter market — preliminary competition assessment

Headline: The market shows clear symptoms of weak competition — high concentration, high margins, inelastic demand and parallel pricing. The evidence justifies further investigation and likely intervention, but does not yet prove unlawful coordination, and the binding constraint may be the statutory cap of three operators rather than firm conduct. I recommend a pro-competition package (review the cap; cut switching costs) over price control, plus targeted evidence-gathering on coordination.

Section A — Market structure

  • Tight oligopoly, statutorily closed. Three operators, HHI ≈ 3,550 (highly concentrated) and rising. Entry is capped at three by the licensing regime — an absolute legal barrier, the most important structural fact in the case.
  • Homogeneous service, repeat low-value purchase, switching costs (separate apps, accounts, stored credit) → consumers tend to single-home, which softens competition further.
  • Implication: structure is conducive to both unilateral market power and tacit coordination. With three symmetric-ish firms, a closed market and homogeneous output, the conditions for coordination are present — but conditions are not conduct.

Section B — Pricing & profitability

  • Profitability: per 12-minute ride, revenue ≈ £3.6–4.0, cost ≈ £2.0, profit ≈ £1.6–1.8 → contribution margin ≈ 46%; ≈ £5.2m annual industry profit. Caveat: estimated costs likely exclude capital, depreciation, platform and licence costs, so economic profit is lower — but no cost increase is recorded to justify the 10% price rise.
  • Demand elasticity: +10% price, −5% volume ⇒ market elasticity ≈ −0.5 (inelastic). Few substitutes; pricing power present.
  • Pricing power: yes — high margins, inelastic demand, and the ability to raise price 10% and retain 95% of volume. Lerner on the per-minute margin ≈ 0.55. Note the nuance: a fully-coordinated monopolist would not stop on the inelastic part of demand, which argues coordination (if any) is imperfect.

Section C — Competitive dynamics

  • Why similar prices / simultaneous increases? Three non-exclusive explanations, which the evidence does not yet separate:
    1. Tacit coordination / conscious parallelism — firms recognise their interdependence and match, without any agreement (generally lawful absent a "plus factor").
    2. Explicit coordination — an agreement or concerted practice / information exchange (unlawful) — possible but unproven.
    3. Common cost or demand shock — competitive firms passing through a shared input-cost rise. Weakened here because no cost increase is in the data.
  • What the evidence infers: weak price competition, but the share dynamics cut against a perfectly stable cartel — ZoomZip is gaining (40→45%) at FlashRide's expense (35→30%). Scootly's share is frozen at exactly 25% for three years, which is unusual and worth probing.
  • Non-price competition you'd expect: scooter availability/density and geographic coverage, app reliability and UX, battery range, safety, speed of unlock, loyalty/subscription bundles. With a closed market and fat margins, even non-price rivalry may be muted — worth testing.
  • Extra evidence on coordination ("plus factors"): internal emails/board minutes; any direct or indirect contact or signalling (public price pre-announcements); membership of/data-sharing via a trade body; use of shared pricing algorithms or the same pricing software (algorithmic collusion); common ownership / overlapping investors; the full price/cost/margin time-series; how prices moved at earlier dates; reasons no entrant applied.

Section D — Market failure & need for intervention

  • The failure: market power in a statutorily closed oligopoly producing prices above the competitive level, an inelastic-demand consumer base (limited self-protection), and allocative inefficiency — the 10% rise priced out ~150,000 rides/year (the −5% volume), against the original policy goals of cutting congestion and emissions.
  • How it manifests: high margins, parallel pricing, no entry, stable shares. Distributionally, a transfer from consumers (many of them commuters, some low-income) to operators.
  • Is the evidence sufficient? Sufficient to establish a competition concern and justify a deeper market investigation; not sufficient to prove unlawful coordination (parallel conduct has innocent explanations) or to leap to heavy regulation. Key uncertainties: costs are estimated and lack a time-series; −0.5 may be short-run; switching costs may reflect product design not strategy; only three years of data; and the cap of three is a regulatory barrier the Authority itself (or government) controls — this may be as much regulatory failure as market failure.

Most proportionate: a package of 1 + 2, evidence-led. Start with the low-cost switching/transparency remedies and a review of the licence cap (the root structural cause), run in parallel with targeted evidence-gathering for "plus factors" on coordination. Reserve price control as a backstop if structural and behavioural measures fail, because it is the most information-hungry and distortion-prone tool and does nothing about the underlying closed-market structure. Sequencing matters: fix the structure and the information environment before regulating the price.

What would change this recommendation: hard evidence of an agreement/algorithmic coordination (→ enforcement first); evidence that costs genuinely rose ~10% (→ harm is smaller, conduct more benign); or evidence the market cannot sustain a fourth operator (→ drop option 1, lean on 2 + targeted price oversight).

3Part 3 · Defence pack & the traps

The five judgement calls that separate strong from average

  1. Don't shout "cartel". The whole case is engineered to tempt you into declaring illegal collusion. The disciplined answer distinguishes tacit coordination / conscious parallelism (lawful, no agreement) from a concerted practice (unlawful) and says the evidence can't yet tell them apart. That nuance is the test.
  2. Spot that the cap is the root cause. Entry is capped at three by the regulator's own regime. A lot of the "market power" is manufactured by policy. The mature answer treats this partly as regulatory failure and puts "review the cap" at the centre.
  3. Use the inelastic-demand paradox. ε = −0.5 both explains pricing power and argues against a perfect cartel (a monopolist wouldn't stop there). Holding both ideas at once signals real economic literacy.
  4. Caveat the margins honestly. 46% is a contribution margin on estimated costs that likely omit capital/depreciation/platform/licence. Don't present it as economic profit. But note there's no cost increase to justify the 10% rise.
  5. Sequence the remedies and tie to proportionality. Structural + behavioural before price control; evidence-gathering before enforcement. Proportionality is the examiner's word — use it.

Traps planted in the data

  • "They all charge the same → collusion." No — homogeneous oligopoly often produces near-identical prices competitively. Need a plus factor.
  • "Prices rose → consumer harm proven." Only if not cost-justified. Flag the missing cost time-series.
  • Scootly at exactly 25% for 3 years. Suspiciously stable — a coordination flag worth naming — but equally could be a capacity-constrained fringe player. Name it, don't over-read it.
  • HHI alone. High HHI shows structure, not conduct or harm. Pair it with margins + elasticity.
  • Per-minute vs per-ride margins. Be explicit which you're using; the unlock fee is a fixed per-trip charge, not a marginal-minute cost.
"My read is that this market has the structure and symptoms of weak competition — HHI around 3,500, ~46% contribution margins, inelastic demand and parallel pricing — but the data can't yet distinguish tacit oligopolistic interdependence, which is lawful, from an unlawful concerted practice. And the binding constraint is arguably the statutory cap of three, which is the Authority's own instrument. So my instinct is a proportionate, evidence-led package — review the cap, cut switching costs, gather plus-factor evidence — and to hold price control as a backstop rather than a first resort."

4Part 4 · Stage-3 conversation drill

The brief says the note "will be used as the basis of a conversation." Expect Schellion / the panel to push on exactly these. Rehearse the answers aloud.

Q. Is this a cartel?
A. On this evidence I can't say so, and I'd be cautious asserting it. The pricing is parallel and the structure is conducive, but parallel pricing in a homogeneous three-firm oligopoly is consistent with lawful conscious parallelism. To allege a concerted practice I'd want a plus factor — contacts, signalling, information exchange, shared pricing algorithms, or common ownership. That's where I'd direct evidence-gathering.
Q. You computed a 46% margin — so they're gouging consumers?
A. It's a 46% contribution margin on estimated costs that almost certainly exclude capital, depreciation, platform and licence costs, so economic profit is lower. But it's high, and crucially there's no recorded cost increase to justify the 10% price rise, so it does support a pricing-power concern — I'd just resist calling it 46% economic profit.
Q. Demand elasticity is −0.5. So what?
A. Two things. It confirms pricing power — few substitutes, so a 10% rise only shed 5% of rides. But it also tempers the cartel story: a profit-maximising monopolist wouldn't price on the inelastic part of demand, so if they were perfectly coordinating I'd expect higher prices still. That suggests imperfect/tacit coordination or external restraint, not a textbook cartel.
Q. What's your single most important recommendation?
A. Review the statutory cap of three operators. It's the binding structural barrier and it's the Authority's own instrument — so before regulating conduct or price, I'd ask whether the regime itself is manufacturing the market power, balanced against the congestion and safety reasons the cap exists.
Q. Why not just cap prices now?
A. Proportionality. Price control is the most information-hungry and distortion-prone tool — it needs the true cost base I don't yet have, risks under-investment and worse availability, and does nothing about the closed structure. I'd use it as a backstop if structural and behavioural remedies fail.
Q. What evidence would most change your view?
A. Proof of an agreement or shared pricing algorithm would move me to enforcement first. Evidence that input costs genuinely rose ~10% would shrink the harm and make the conduct look competitive. And evidence the market can't support a fourth operator would drop the entry remedy and push me toward switching-cost measures plus light price oversight.

5Assumptions register (state these in the note)

  • Average ride = 12 minutes applied to all operators; revenue/cost per ride built on that single average (the data gives no per-operator usage split).
  • "Cost per ride" treated as a fixed per-trip cost and "cost per minute" as the marginal riding cost; both as given and labelled "estimated".
  • Estimated costs are contribution-level and likely exclude capital, depreciation, platform/R&D, overhead and licence fees — so margins overstate economic profit.
  • 3,000,000 total rides/year split by the Year-3 market shares for aggregate revenue/profit.
  • Elasticity is an arc/market-level estimate from a single Y2→Y3 change; treat as indicative, possibly short-run, not a precise structural parameter.
  • No cost time-series provided → cannot attribute the 10% price rise to a cost shock; treated as an open question, not assumed either way.
