Series · April 2026

UK Economics Consultancy: A Historical Series

Eleven pieces on the £2 billion industry nobody writes about. Ninety firms, twenty-five years, every Companies House filing read.

There are roughly ninety economics-consultancy firms in the United Kingdom. Together they collect more than £2 billion in annual fees. They advise competition authorities, price regulators, government departments, law firms, and corporate clients on the most consequential economic decisions in Britain — CMA merger clearances, Ofwat and Ofgem price reviews, CAT damages awards, Treasury fiscal policy. Most of their work happens behind closed doors. Very little of it is written about.

This series traces the industry's history from 2000 to 2025. It is built on the filed Companies House accounts of every active firm in the market, on twenty-five years of Global Competition Review coverage, on press cuttings, obituaries, and firm histories. The dataset, the code, and the source PDFs are all open, and every claim in the prose traces to a primary source that a reader can check. Start anywhere.

Who Was Here in 2000

The year the UK economics-consultancy market did not yet exist in its modern shape. A portrait of the four firms, fifteen people, and three cases that seeded the industry.
Part 2 · 2000–2010

The Founders' Decade

In January 2000, NERA ruled the UK economics market; by 2010, three defectors had built a rival that would eventually post the highest profit-per-partner in British professional services.
Part 3 · 2010–2015

The Professionalisation — PE Arrives

LECG imploded, its European economists scattered across three doors, and a patient English private equity house concluded that a research boutique in the City was worth four times its revenues. The money had begun to arrive.
Part 4 · 2015–2020

The Global Arms Race

The Americans cross the Atlantic; private equity writes its first cheque into UK economics research; Brexit throws off a regulatory advisory boom; and one firm quietly posts the highest profit-per-partner in British professional services.
Part 5 · 2020–2025

The Profit Machine

An eighteen-partner competition-economics firm on a single floor of 199 Bishopsgate booked the highest profit-per-partner of any UK professional-services LLP that files in full. McKinsey swallowed Vivid; Jacobs completed a seven-year roll-up of PA Consulting at an implied £3.05 billion enterprise value; Goldman Sachs bankrolled a twenty-five-person walkout from Compass Lexecon.
Part 6 · CMA → private sector

The CMA Drain

Five senior economists walked out of the CMA in three years; Stefan Hunt walked out twice. Here is where they went, and who paid to bring them in.
Part 7 · Network

The People Network

One chart, every documented cross-firm move in the UK economics consultancy market 2000–2025. Who came from where. Who went where. A chord diagram mapping twenty-five years of churn.
Standalone deep-dive
RBB Economics · 2002–2025

£2m to £108m — Twenty-three years inside RBB Economics

Three economists walked out of NERA London in April 2002 and set up a competition-economics boutique from scratch. Twenty-three years later, the firm they built posts £108m of revenue and £3.34m per partner — the highest documented profit-per-partner in any UK professional-services LLP that files in full.
