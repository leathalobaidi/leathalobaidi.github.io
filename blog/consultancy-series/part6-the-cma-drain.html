The CMA Drain

Five senior economists walked out of the CMA in three years; Stefan Hunt walked out twice. Here is where they went, and who paid to bring them in.

Leath · 10 April 2026 · 14 min read

One economist, three firms, three years

On 2 December 2025, Companies House recorded a new LLP member at AlixPartners UK, a starchy entry in a starchy register: Stefan Alexis Hunt, no job description, no fanfare, just the date and the name and the firm. Less than three years earlier, in January 2023, Hunt had joined Keystone Strategy as Head of K.ATS Europe, the boutique's newly-launched Advanced Technology Services arm; less than a year before that, he was still inside the Competition and Markets Authority, where he had spent nearly five years building its Data and Technology Unit into one of the most advanced analytics teams of any competition regulator in the world. Three firms in three years. The file entry tells you nothing. It tells you everything.

Stefan Alexis Hunt
CMA · Data & Tech Unit→Keystone · Head of K.ATS Europe→AlixPartners · Partner & MD
5 years · Jan 2023 · 2 Dec 2025

On its own, Hunt's move reads as an ambitious economist trading up. Set against the other moves in the same three-year window, it reads as the symptom of a system running at speed. Five senior CMA economists have gone into five separate private firms since Andrea Coscelli stepped down as chief executive; four of those firms are now building new competition-economics practices around the hires; and Cinven has paid £190 million for one of them while Goldman Sachs has financed another. The regulator is draining into the consultancies. The consultancies are paying premium multiples for what comes out. This is the story of a single flow.

Section 1: The regulator as recruiter

The CMA employs around 1,160 staff — 1,157 payroll plus five non-payroll as of December 2024 — and since the OFT and the Competition Commission were welded together in 2014, the new authority has invested in data analytics and behavioural methods faster than any other European competition agency. Andrea Coscelli ran that build as chief executive from 2016, and by the time he stepped down in 2022 the chief economist's team looked less like a civil-service unit and more like a frontier-economics department parked inside Whitehall. The work was quiet, the output was formidable, and the partner class of the London consultancies was paying close attention.

Since Coscelli's departure, five senior economists have left that team for private practice. Each move carries a named announcement and a date.

Economist CMA role Destination Date

When Francesca Sala's move to Oxera was announced, Global Competition Review flagged her as the third senior CMA economist to cross into private practice in as many months. The framing aged badly. By the time the year closed the third had become the fifth, and the trade press had stopped counting.

The CMA is losing economists to every corner of the market, and no single firm has absorbed the flow. Coscelli and Hunt went to a Boston strategy boutique; Walker went to an employee-owned regulatory specialist; Sala went to an Oxford competition house; Bon went to a US-listed turnaround outfit. The receiving firms differ in genre, ownership, and sales pitch. The common feature is that every one of them wants a piece. Hunt is on the table twice because he moved twice, and his second move — from Keystone to AlixPartners in December 2025 — is the one that gives the whole pattern its shape. Read what happened at AlixPartners in the fourteen months before.

Section 2: The Keystone European build

Greg Richards and Professor Marco Iansiti founded Keystone Strategy in Boston in 2003, and for its first two decades the London office was a small outpost feeding the US parent's big-tech antitrust caseload. In 2022 Keystone decided to turn that outpost into a full European platform, and the sequence of hires that followed tells the story. The build happened in three steps, not one. Cristina Caffarra partnered with Keystone in July 2022, crossing over from Charles River Associates where she had run the European competition practice for more than a decade; she was the anchor. Roughly six months later, on 29 November 2022, Keystone announced that Andrea Coscelli, the outgoing CEO of the CMA, would join as joint Head of Europe from 3 January 2023 — a move the UK's Advisory Committee on Business Appointments blessed in a formal advice letter. Five weeks later, on 24 January 2023, Keystone announced that Stefan Hunt would arrive as Head of K.ATS Europe, reporting to Rohit Chatterjee, the global K.ATS chief. PYMNTS's CPI wire ran the notice the same week.

"Dr Hunt is an advanced analytics leader and economist with 20 years of experience using rigorous analytics in competition, regulation and business strategy. Stefan will work closely with Andrea Coscelli and Cristina Caffarra."

The Caffarra–Coscelli–Hunt triangle was the strategy, built sequentially over six months: hire the most visible European antitrust economist first, use that anchor to land the departing CMA chief executive, then use the combined pull to bring in the regulator's senior data-science lead. It was an incremental build, not a coordinated swoop. Over the next eighteen months Keystone added Gianmarco Calanchi, and in April 2024 a round of principal economists from Spotify and Amazon. By mid-2024 the London office was carrying a name for itself.

But Keystone lost more than Hunt. Cristina Caffarra left Keystone in 2023, about a year after joining, and did not move to a rival. She became Honorary Professor at UCL, co-founded the CEPR Competition Research Policy Network, and launched EuroStack and the Escape Forward podcast — a portfolio retirement that reads less like exit and more like reinvention. Two years on, Keystone lost Gianmarco Calanchi as well. Calanchi had spent a decade at the OFT and CMA running Phase I and Phase II merger reviews, crossed to Compass Lexecon as a Vice President, then joined Keystone in 2023 as part of the European build, then joined Econic Partners as a Partner in London in 2025. His arc is a single loop through the market: public sector, Compass Lexecon, Keystone, Econic. Five firms, fifteen years, one man.

Gianmarco Calanchi
DECC→OFT / CMA · 20+ mergers→Compass Lexecon · VP→Keystone · Sr Principal→Econic · Partner
15+ years · five firms

Of the four economists Keystone named as its European leadership between November 2022 and early 2023 — Caffarra, Calanchi, Coscelli, Hunt — three were gone by the end of 2025. Caffarra to UCL, Calanchi to Econic, Hunt to AlixPartners. Only Coscelli stayed. AlixPartners is a US-headquartered turnaround firm that has run through a consortium of CDPQ, PSP Investments and Investcorp since a buyout announced in April 2017 at around $2.5 billion; it was the firm's third round of private-equity ownership, after Hellman & Friedman in 2006 and CVC Capital Partners in 2012. Before October 2024 it had no dedicated competition-economics practice in London. The institutional capital behind AlixPartners is what makes a rapid three-senior-hire build — Mazzarotto, Wilkinson, Bon and Hunt inside fourteen months — financially feasible in a way that a partner-owned firm without a balance sheet could not replicate.

Section 3: The AlixPartners build

Before October 2024 the competition-economics team at AlixPartners London was a handful of managing directors doing disputes work. Between October 2024 and December 2025 the firm made three senior hires and rebuilt the practice around a new brand.

The AlixPartners build-out, Oct 2024 – Dec 2025
Oct 2024 · Mazzarotto & Wilkinson (ex-KPMG) Jun 2025 · Julie Bon (ex-CMA Deputy Chief Economist) Dec 2025 · Stefan Hunt (ex-Keystone, ex-CMA)
3 senior hires · 14 months · new practice

Nicola Mazzarotto and Caitlin Wilkinson crossed over from KPMG in October 2024; Julie Bon, the CMA's then-Deputy Chief Economist, joined in June 2025; and on 2 December 2025 Companies House filed Stefan Hunt as an LLP member, with the firm's own press release following the next day. Hunt was to lead a new practice named, with corporate precision, Behavioural Economics and Technology Evidence. The name tells you the bet. AlixPartners wanted a data and behavioural competition capability, and it was buying the people who had built the CMA's.

Keystone lost Hunt to a direct rival three years after hiring him from the CMA. Read one way, the move is a vindication: Keystone built a European platform on CMA alumni and proved the model. Read the other way, Keystone has become the training ground that other firms now raid for the reason Keystone once raided the regulator. Hunt carried his skills and his network with him to AlixPartners. That is what AlixPartners paid him for.

A question no firm will answer on the record. If the CMA is the source of the most sought-after economists in London, and four firms are now hiring from it to build practices that compete for the same cases, what happens to those valuations when any one of them loses a key partner?

Section 4: Compass Lexecon and the second exit

The biggest movement of economists in the UK in 2025 was not a CMA departure at all; it was the Econic Partners breakaway from Compass Lexecon. On 15 February 2024, Compass Lexecon named new EMEA leadership: Lorenzo Coppi, Neil Dryden and Kirsten Edwards-Warren became co-heads, while Jorge Padilla, who had run the European practice since arriving from LECG in 2011, moved to Chair. The firm called it a succession plan. A year later it read as the prologue to the largest UK economics-firm departure since the LECG bankruptcy.

On 19 February 2025, Jonathan Orszag, Mark Israel (the former Compass Lexecon president) and Kirsten Edwards-Warren — one of the co-heads named twelve months earlier — walked out with more than twenty-five economists to launch Econic Partners. Israel formally joined on 2 April 2025. Goldman Sachs Alternatives financed the launch, and the Willig and Ordover families wrote cheques alongside Goldman. Robert Willig and Janusz Ordover are the Princeton economists who founded COMPASS in 2003; FTI bought COMPASS and merged it with Lexecon in 2008 to create Compass Lexecon. Read that again. The families who founded the firm that became Compass Lexecon financed the breakaway that took its senior team. By May 2025 the Financial Times had confirmed that Enrique Andreu, who had led Compass Lexecon’s Brussels office, had followed Edwards-Warren across to the new firm. The story of 2025’s market reaction — the fourteen per cent share-price slide at FTI, the $35 million-plus guided profit hit, the ten per cent defection rate inside the economics division — sits in Part 5, Section 4.

Econic's London office is built on Compass Lexecon alumni. Kirsten Edwards-Warren is a founding partner and one of the two Companies House directors of record. Andy Parkinson spent over a decade at Compass Lexecon, rising to Senior Vice President, before moving to Econic as a Partner in London and Brussels. Rameet Sangha has the most unusual path of the four.

Rameet Sangha
Cambridge / LSE / King's→AlixPartners · Director→Compass Lexecon · from 6 Jan 2020→Econic · Partner, London + Brussels
20+ years · GCR Women in Antitrust

Sangha joined Compass Lexecon in January 2020 from AlixPartners, where she had been a Director. Five years on, she left Compass Lexecon for Econic. Her career connects AlixPartners, Compass Lexecon and Econic as nodes in the same recruiting circuit, and it quietly undermines the tidy version of the AlixPartners story told in the previous section. The firm now building a competition-economics practice by hiring from the CMA and from Keystone was, in 2020, losing a senior director to Compass Lexecon. The rotation has been running in both directions for at least six years.

Compass Lexecon economists walked to Econic · Feb 2025

Compass Lexecon is not folding. Padilla stays as Chair; the firm retains a deep European bench and the most visible caseload in EU antitrust; on 21 April 2025 it hired Faten Sabry as a Senior Managing Director in New York, after twenty-seven years at NERA where she had chaired the Global Securities and Finance Practice. But the Sabry hire reinforced the US securities-valuation side of the business rather than London antitrust, and Compass Lexecon has not publicly replaced the London partners it lost to Econic. The Econic launch is the first time since Ridyard, Bishop and Baker left NERA in 2002 to found RBB that a UK economics team has walked out together at this scale. What separates Econic from RBB is the money. Ridyard, Bishop and Baker bootstrapped. Econic launched with Goldman's cheque already cleared.

A structural footnote. Econic is a Limited company, not an LLP. Every rival in the top ten of this market files as an LLP: FTI Consulting LLP (which houses Compass Lexecon's London practice), RBB Economics LLP, Oxera Consulting LLP, Baringa Partners LLP, AlixPartners UK LLP. In an LLP, every designated member appears in Companies House filings within fourteen days of joining. Econic U.K. Limited (16147541) has exactly two directors on record: Jonathan Orszag (20 December 2024) and Kirsten Warren (18 June 2025). Calanchi, Parkinson, Sangha and Duquesne are partners in the professional-services sense and leave no public trace in Companies House officer filings at all. The structural choice is not trivial. When a partner joins an LLP, the UK's public registry tells you within two weeks. When a partner joins Econic London, you read the firm's own website.

Section 5: Grant Thornton quietly opens a door

The CMA is not the only source pool. The other major new entrant into London competition economics over the last five years came in through a side door marked Alvarez & Marsal. On 9 November 2021, Grant Thornton UK launched a new Economics Consulting practice by hiring Schellion Horn and Chris Williams as partners, both from A&M. Horn had spent twenty years in competition economics, moving through FTI, Deloitte, PwC and A&M; Williams had thirteen years at KPMG before A&M. Robert Hannah, Grant Thornton's Head of Large and Complex Advisory, sponsored the hire. Horn and Williams positioned the new team around regulated-industry work, competition and state-aid cases — the space between the Big Four and the boutiques. Before November 2021, Grant Thornton had no standalone economics brand; by 2024, Horn's team was bidding against Oxera, Frontier and CRA. Two partner hires closed the gap.

AlixPartners did the same thing fifteen months later. A firm with no economics brand decides it wants one, finds the people who can bring the brand in a box, and hires them. Grant Thornton raided A&M. AlixPartners raided the CMA and Keystone. The source pool differs. The mechanic is identical.

Section 5b: The revolving door is wider than the CMA

The CMA is the most visible source in the regulator-to-consultancy pipeline, but a systematic search of Companies House officer filings across the major UK economics houses shows a revolving door that reaches well beyond a single regulator. Brussels is one of the rooms it opens onto.

The EU pipeline

Two senior officials from DG Competition have crossed to UK economics boutiques. Sir Philip Lowe, Director-General of EU DG Competition from 2002 to 2010, appeared briefly as an LLP member of Oxera Consulting LLP between May and August 2019; a quiet three-month stint, the sort of appointment that registers only because the registrar requires it. Miguel de la Mano, once acting Chief Economist at DG Competition, joined RBB Economics LLP as a member in May 2022 and remains there. Oxera added Helene Bourguignon, another ex-DG Competition economist, as an LLP member from November 2021. Brussels is a source pool the CMA framing underweights.

The utility-regulator pipeline

Joe Perkins, former Chief Economist at Ofgem, joined FTI Consulting LLP as a designated member on 4 June 2024. His Companies House trail also includes a directorship at the Regulatory Policy Institute in Oxford (April 2020 to January 2023) and an ongoing board seat at Sustainability First, an energy-regulation charity. The Ofgem-to-FTI path mirrors the CMA-to-AlixPartners path that Stefan Hunt took six months later: senior regulator leaves, joins a US-parented multi-practice firm as a partner. William Rickett CB, former Director General for Energy at BERR and DECC (the forerunners of BEIS), joined CEPA LLP as a designated member in August 2019, and has been a director of the parent company since 2012. CEPA is a specialist regulatory-economics house advising Ofwat, Ofgem, and the National Infrastructure Commission — the exact bodies Rickett once oversaw.

The OFT and the dual-role model

Professor Amelia Fletcher, former Chief Economist at the OFT, demonstrates a third variant: the simultaneous dual role. Fletcher served as a non-executive director of the FCA from April 2013 to March 2020 and of the Payment Systems Regulator over the same period, while simultaneously running Coreco Economics Ltd, her own consultancy, from June 2013 onwards. The regulator seat and the private practice overlapped for seven years. The arrangement is legal, publicly declared on the FCA register, and common at senior levels in UK financial regulation. It is also a structural feature of this market that the Part 7 chord diagram does not yet capture.

Section 6: What the private-equity deals are pricing

Capital Economics tells the clearest returns story. LDC bought a minority stake in 2014 at a £70 million valuation; Phoenix took control in 2018 at £95 million; LDC exited at 2.5× cash-on-cash and a 43 per cent IRR. Fund committees pay attention to numbers like that. A firm with £22 million of revenue and twenty per cent margins justifies the sequel only if the buyer believes the revenue will double.

Cinven paid £190 million for a majority stake in Flint Global in December 2025, and Bloomberg put the multiple at roughly 16× EV/EBITDA. Flint had grown from founding in 2015 to £31 million of revenue by 2024 at a 30 per cent margin. Ed Richards, the former Ofcom chief executive, and Sir Simon Fraser, formerly the FCO permanent secretary, stayed on as shareholders. Sixteen times EBITDA for a ten-year-old political-advisory firm is a specialty-services multiple, not a consulting multiple — closer to what buyers pay for regulated-industry platforms with defensible client relationships than what they pay for general advisory houses. Capital Economics in 2014 was the first UK economics firm to take private-equity money. Flint in 2025 is the first UK policy-advisory firm to clear the institutional-multiple threshold. Eleven years separate them, and eight other deals fill the gap.

Section 7: Three PE strategies, one market

Private equity is not doing one thing in economics consulting. It is doing three distinct things, and the distinction matters for how the market evolves.

Strategy 1: Buying subscription and advisory businesses for their cash flow

Capital Economics (LDC 2014, Phoenix 2018) and Flint Global (Cinven 2025) are classic private-equity plays: recurring revenue, high margins, low capex, defensible client relationships. PE buys these for the same reason it buys any professional-services firm — the economics are attractive. The product happens to be economics consulting, but the thesis is cash-flow arbitrage, not a bet on the discipline. Phoenix would have bought a veterinary chain on the same multiples if the numbers worked.

Strategy 2: Funding breakaways to capture talent arbitrage

Econic Partners (Goldman Sachs Alternatives, February 2025) is a different animal. Goldman did not buy an existing firm; it bankrolled a team walking out of an incumbent. The bet was that the talent — Orszag, Israel, Edwards-Warren — would take the clients with them, and that the value of a named competition economist is higher as the founding partner of a new firm than as an employee of FTI's subsidiary. The Willig and Ordover families co-investing makes the same point: they built the predecessor firm, and they judge the breakaway team the better vehicle for the next cycle. This is new. RBB bootstrapped its 2002 breakaway from NERA with no outside capital. Econic launched with Goldman's cheque already cleared. The private-equity involvement compresses the timeline and raises the stakes.

Strategy 3: Buying platforms and bolting on economics practices

AlixPartners (Hellman & Friedman 2006, CVC 2012, CDPQ/PSP/Investcorp announced April 2017 at around $2.5 billion) and BRG (Endeavour Capital 2017, TowerBrook Capital Partners February 2025) are restructuring and disputes shops building economics practices inside private-equity-owned shells. AlixPartners hired Stefan Hunt and Julie Bon from the CMA not because its private-equity owners directed a competition-economics build, but because the existing platform had the client relationships and the balance sheet to absorb senior hires. A partner-owned firm without that balance sheet could not have made three senior competition hires in fourteen months the way AlixPartners did. BRG is the same story from the other direction. TowerBrook bought a majority stake in February 2025 — the same month Econic launched. BRG already had the LECG DNA and a loss-making UK operation; TowerBrook's capital lets BRG absorb the UK losses while it builds scale. Two of the biggest structural events in UK economics consulting in 2025 happened within days of each other: Goldman backed a breakaway from Compass Lexecon, and TowerBrook recapitalised the firm built from LECG's wreckage.

The combined effect

Ten years ago, the only externally capitalised economics firms in the UK were subsidiaries of listed corporates: FTI/Compass Lexecon (NYSE: FCN), CRA (NASDAQ: CRAI), and NERA (Marsh & McLennan, NYSE: MMC). Private-equity or institutional money now sits behind at least six of the top twenty firms in the dataset. The sector's ownership structure has shifted from founder-partnerships to institutional-backed vehicles faster than most participants have recognised. The implication is that the PE story told in the earlier parts of this series was too narrow; it read as a single origin tale, PE discovered economics consulting in 2014 with LDC's investment into Capital Economics and has been buying ever since. The fuller picture is more layered. PE discovered the sector's cash flows in 2014, discovered its talent arbitrage in 2025, and has been capitalising the platform firms that compete for the same economists since 2006. Three different strategies, one market; and the combined effect is that almost every new hire, every breakaway, and every senior departure described in this series has institutional capital on at least one side of the transaction.

Section 8: The talent reallocation

Between January 2023 and December 2025, the UK economics-consultancy market reallocated. The total number of senior economists is roughly the same as it was five years ago; what changed is where they sit. The CMA lost its visible leaders to private practice; Compass Lexecon lost a quarter of its European team to Econic; Keystone hired Stefan Hunt and lost him to AlixPartners three years later. AlixPartners and Grant Thornton built new competition-economics practices from near-zero by hiring other firms' partners, and Cinven, Phoenix, Stonehaven, Goldman Sachs, TowerBrook, News Corp and McKinsey paid premium multiples for the resulting teams or financed the platforms that employed them. No new cohort entered the industry at scale in those three years. The CMA is not replacing departures at the same speed. The firms are not growing by training. They are growing by recruiting each other and the regulator. The private-equity buyers are paying for the stock of experience inside particular heads, and the price of those heads has risen in proportion to how many other firms want to hire them.

Every UK firm launched in this window — Econic, Pragmatix, Stonehaven's roll-up — started with founders who were already partners somewhere else. The first-time founding that built RBB in 2002, Vivid in 2006, and Flint in 2015 is missing from the last three years. The market is consolidating into a fight between incumbents for a shrinking stock of senior talent, underwritten by private-equity money that assumes the CMA flow will continue. It will, until it doesn't. The CMA does not pay £1 million a year for senior economists and cannot match Keystone or AlixPartners on cash; the harder question is what happens when the pipeline runs dry. The CMA built its data and technology team between 2017 and 2022; the most senior members have already left, and the next cohort needs another five years before it reaches partner-level seniority. By 2028 or 2029 the direction of travel may reverse: senior economists leaving AlixPartners and Keystone to rejoin the regulator on the AI-competition cases that do not exist today.

Key takeaways

  1. The CMA has become the single largest source of senior economists feeding London's competition-economics market: five named senior moves in three years, spread across five different receiving firms.
  2. Stefan Hunt moved three times in three years — CMA, Keystone, AlixPartners — the cleanest single example of how fast this market can poach and re-poach the same person.
  3. AlixPartners and Grant Thornton Economics stood up new competition-economics practices from near-zero in 2021 and 2024–25, entirely by hiring existing partners from rivals.
  4. The Econic Partners launch in February 2025 is the largest mid-career breakaway since RBB left NERA in 2002, financed by Goldman Sachs Alternatives and the families who founded COMPASS.
  5. Cinven paid roughly £190 million at ~16× EV/EBITDA for Flint Global in December 2025 — the highest published multiple for a UK policy-advisory house on record.
  6. No firm launched in the 2023–2025 window was founded by first-time entrepreneurs; every one was started by partners from existing firms, and the CMA is not replenishing its senior cohort at the same speed it is losing them.

What to watch for in 2026

Three things to track. First, Compass Lexecon. Succession discipline after a breakaway is what decides whether the departure is a dent or a shock; watch the next six months for a second wave of senior exits. Second, what Cinven does with Flint. Private-equity owners of advisory firms drive consolidation, and Capital Economics is the template: a seven-year hold followed by a trade sale or a secondary. If Flint becomes a roll-up platform for mid-sized UK policy shops — Stonehaven scale but with a larger cheque — the bottom two-thirds of the ninety-firm list will look very different by 2028. Third, Stefan Hunt. Nothing in the public record rules out a third move, and he would not be the first person in this industry to stop somewhere for less than three years. If Hunt leaves AlixPartners in 2027, the question stops being whether the CMA is a pipeline and starts being whether the receiving firms are interchangeable to a senior data scientist — and what that answers for the practices trying to build around him.
